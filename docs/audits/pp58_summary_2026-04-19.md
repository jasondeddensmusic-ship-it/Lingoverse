## Language-Specific Structural Observations

### French (93%) — Strongest non-German language
- Full A2 coverage (20/20) — the only non-German language to achieve this
- 8 A1 units (A1.1/1.2/1.3) give better function distribution than languages with 6
- Only gaps: appointments (A1), admin situations (B1), dispute resolution (B2), personal significance (B2)

### Spanish (92%) — Close second
- Closely mirrors French in structure and coverage
- Health at A2 (F6) is the distinctive gap vs French — no "ir al médico" unit
- U91-95 (B2.4) add thematic vocabulary but no new communicative functions

### Italian (89%) — Best 36-unit language
- Best B1 coverage of all 36-unit languages (21/22 = 95%)
- A1 weakest relative to Italian's structure: daily routine and ability (can/cannot) absent at A1
- Law & Justice unit (U26) covers dispute resolution — unique among non-German languages at B2

### Dutch (88%) — Grammar-heavy, function-thin
- No dedicated weather unit at A1/A2 — the only European language with this gap
- Grammar units (Er-forms, passive, indirect speech) crowd out scenario-based function units
- Strong B1/B2 coverage due to register and academic writing units

### Korean (86%) — Best Asian language
- Units 31-69 are vocabulary batches (TOPIK word lists), contributing zero function coverage
- Core communicative coverage from U1-30 is solid
- Comparatives placed at B1 (U12) — late for CEFR A2
- TOPIK-specific functions (honorific speech levels, 반말/격식체) not in the German reference list but are covered

### Japanese (85%) — Strong B1, weak B2
- Script-learning via Foundations exempts F15/F19 (forms/spelling) from unit coverage
- Weather covered at A1.3 (U10) — better than Dutch and Italian
- B2 thematic units (philosophy, law, literature) contain vocabulary but minimal communicative practice

### Chinese (82%) — Newest with structural gaps
- Ability (F17) deferred to B1.2 — significantly late by CEFR standards
- Past tense introduced at B1.1 (U14) — all A2 past-narration functions technically start at B1
- Chengyu unit (U32) provides strong B2 abstract/idiomatic coverage

### Portuguese (82%) — Newest, shallowest A1
- A1 is the weakest of all languages (15/20 = 75%)
- Past tense, future, and imperative all deferred to B1 — nearly all A2 functional units are labeled B1
- The personal infinitive (U20) is a unique communicative function not in the German reference list but appropriately taught
- Tell-time function partial at A1 — time adverbs yes, clock-reading no

### Russian (79%) — Lowest coverage
- No dedicated comparatives unit anywhere — unique gap across all 9 languages
- 6-case grammar at B1 (U16-21) creates 6 consecutive grammar-mechanics units with no communicative scenario framing
- Past tense correctly at B1.1 due to aspect complexity — this is justified for Russian
- B2 thematic units (society, science, business, arts, history, formal language) provide decent high-level coverage

## Systemic Patterns: Why These Gaps Exist

### Pattern 1: Appointments (A1) — Missing in all 9
German teaches appointments at U6 via a dedicated story dialogue and FB exercise. No other language has a "making an appointment" scenario at A1. All cover calendar/days/time, but none scaffold the *speech act* of requesting/confirming a meeting time. **Estimated fix: 1 lesson per language, story-dialogue format.**

### Pattern 2: Simple messages and forms (A1) — Missing in all 9
German (U3, U6) teaches writing simple personal messages and filling in forms. For non-Latin-script languages (Korean, Japanese, Chinese, Russian), this is partly architectural — learners need the script Foundations before writing tasks. For Latin-script languages (French, Spanish, Italian, Dutch, Portuguese), there is no structural barrier. **Fix for Latin-script languages: 1 lesson in A1.2 per language. Non-Latin: add to Foundations.**

### Pattern 3: Administrative situations (B1) — Missing in all 9
German (U19-20) covers Behördengang (dealing with authorities), banking with formalities, and official forms. Every non-German B1 unit covers professional/formal contexts, but the specific speech act of *handling bureaucratic/institutional situations* (making a formal request at an office, understanding official letters, dealing with civil service) is absent. **This is a certification-grade gap: DELF/DELE/NT2/TOPIK B1 all test administrative comprehension.**

### Pattern 4: Personal significance writing (B2) — Missing in all 9
German (U33, U36) has explicit personal narrative and "what matters to me" writing units. All non-German B2 units focus on formal/academic writing, argumentation, and current events. The *personal significance* function (expressing what something means to you, reflective writing, personal voice in a formal context) is absent. **This is a DELF B2 and Goethe B2 exam task type.**

### Pattern 5: Ability expression (A1/A2) — Missing in 5-6 languages
German introduces können (can) at A1 (U5-6). Italian, Japanese, Chinese, Portuguese, and Russian defer ability modal verbs to A2 or later. Korean covers 못 (cannot) at A1.2 but not the full ability frame. This delays learners' ability to express practical needs ("I can't eat X", "Can you help me?").

## Priority Remediation by Impact

### CRITICAL (certification gap, all 9 languages)
1. **Administrative situations lesson at B1** — One lesson per language with bureaucratic scenario. All major exams (DELF/DELE/Goethe B1) test this.
2. **Personal significance writing at B2** — One lesson per language. Required for DELF B2 production task.

### HIGH (affects A1 exam readiness, all 9 languages)
3. **Make appointments lesson at A1** — Story dialogue + FB exercise. 1 lesson per language.
4. **Write simple messages at A1** — Latin-script languages only (FR, ES, IT, NL, PT). 1 lesson per language.

### MEDIUM (affects specific languages)
5. **Ability (can/cannot) at A1** — Add to Italian U5-6, Portuguese U5-6, Chinese U5-6. Japanese already at A2.1.
6. **Comparatives at A2** — Russian needs a dedicated comparatives unit (currently absent across the entire curriculum).
7. **Weather at A2** — Dutch and Italian need weather units (Dutch U9 area, Italian U10 area).
8. **Health at A2** — Spanish and Japanese need a health/doctor unit at A2 (currently at A2.1 for Chinese/Russian/Korean).
9. **Past tense at A2 (not B1)** — Portuguese, Russian, Chinese all defer core past narration to B1.1. For exam alignment, past tense should be introduced at A2.1 minimum.

### LOW (structural, hard to fix without restructuring)
10. **Korean vocabulary-batch units** — U31-69 do not practice communicative functions. Some could be restructured with scenario dialogues.
11. **Russian case units** — U16-21 could include one communicative scenario per case to satisfy PP58 partially.

## Framework Ambiguity Notes

1. **Vocabulary-batch units** — Units labeled "NT2 B1 Vocabulary", "TOPIK B1", "DELE B2" etc. contain teach cards and quizzes but are not communicative function units. PP58 requires *practice* (learner performs the function), not just vocabulary exposure. These units are correctly excluded from PP58 mapping in this audit.

2. **Grammar-mechanics units vs communicative units** — Russian case units, Japanese te-form unit, Italian subjunctive unit — these *enable* communicative functions but are not themselves communicative function lessons. A grammar unit that teaches passé composé forms without a scenario where the learner describes their weekend does not satisfy PP58 F1 (Describe past events). However, if the grammar unit includes story dialogues where the function is demonstrated and practiced, it is counted as covered.

3. **Non-Latin-script write/spell functions** — F15 (write simple messages) and F19 (spell and give personal details) cannot be fully assessed in the same way for Korean, Japanese, Chinese, and Russian. These languages address script via Foundations, not lesson units. The audit marks these as missing in lesson units while acknowledging the Foundations workaround. A future audit of Foundations content could recover some of these as covered.

4. **Cultural uniqueness** — TOPIK-specific functions (Korean honorific speech levels, 존댓말/반말 distinction), Japanese keigo (polite language), Chinese chengyu (four-character idioms) represent communicative functions not in the German reference list. A language-specific PP58 extension would add these. This audit uses only the 84 German-reference functions for cross-language comparability.

5. **Sub-level mapping** — The German reference maps functions to exact units (U1-36). Non-German languages with different unit counts (Korean 69, Dutch 89, French/Spanish 90+) require mapping to the equivalent CEFR sub-level rather than unit number. This audit uses CEFR levels (A1.1, A1.2, A2.1, etc.) as the mapping frame.
