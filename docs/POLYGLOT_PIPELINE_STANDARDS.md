# PART 1: THE POLYGLOT ACQUISITION PHILOSOPHY

## What This Platform Is Actually Doing

Most language apps teach you to repeat phrases until they stick. LingoVerse does something different: it teaches you how the language **thinks**, so you can generate language you have never seen before.

The founder is a polyglot with multiple European and Asian languages. This curriculum encodes that lived experience. **The faster you internalize the underlying system, the faster you stop memorizing and start generating.**

This applies equally to Dutch, Korean, German, French, Spanish, and Arabic. The MECHANISMS are universal. The CONTENT is language-specific.

---

## The Polyglot Acquisition Sequence

This is how experienced language learners actually acquire:

```
1. EXPOSURE     → Encounter grammar in natural context, no explanation
2. PATTERN FEEL → Brain recognizes pattern without conscious labeling
3. INSTINCT     → Correct forms feel right before learner knows why
4. CONFIRMATION → Explicit label arrives as recognition, not instruction
5. GENERATION   → Learner produces forms never seen before
```

The P26 spiral replicates this. Steps 1–3 happen across multiple units. Step 4 is the harvest lesson. Step 5 is what sets LingoVerse learners apart from phrase-app learners.

**The failure mode to avoid:** Jumping from Step 1 directly to Step 4. Naming grammar before it has been felt. This produces learners who know the rule but can't use it. This is what textbooks do. This is what we do not do.

## The Five Universal Pillars

### Pillar 1: Show Before Name (P26 Spiral)
Grammar is shown in natural use for 2–4 units before it receives a formal label. The name arrives as confirmation of something the learner's instinct already knows. The delay between first exposure and formal naming is not a delay — it is the teaching.

**Every named grammar point must have a documented seed location.** No harvest without planted seeds.

### Pillar 2: The LEGO Principle (Morphological Systems)
Every language has building blocks that generate words:
- **Korean:** Sino-Korean morphemes (60% of vocabulary — 80 morphemes → 500+ words)
- **German:** Prefixes (un-, ver-, be-, ent-, er-) + suffixes (-heit, -keit, -ung, -lich, -ig)
- **French:** Latin/Greek root network (-tion, -ité, -ment, -eux/-euse, re-)
- **Spanish:** Similar to French + diminutive/augmentative system (-ito, -ón)
- **Dutch:** Compound word system (the world's longest words in a Germanic language)
- **Arabic:** Trilateral root system (most powerful LEGO system in any language — k-t-b: كَتَبَ wrote, كِتَاب book, مَكْتَبَة library, كَاتِب writer)

**Every compound or derived word must be decomposed on first appearance.** Show the pieces. Name the morpheme. Add it to the morpheme bank.

### Pillar 3: The Structural Metaphor (Language-Adapted)
Each language needs its own central metaphor that explains how the language builds meaning:

| Language | Central metaphor | Explanation |
|----------|-----------------|-------------|
| **Korean** | The Train | Verb stem = engine. Grammar endings = cars that snap on. Tense car, politeness car, aspect car. |
| **German** | The Frame | Verb frame holds the sentence: conjugated verb in position 2, infinitive/participle at the end. The frame is fixed. What goes inside it can change. |
| **Dutch** | The Slot | Similar to German but simpler frame. Main verb position 2, past participle at end. The slot system for separable verbs. |
| **French** | The Agreement Web | Everything agrees with everything: adjectives with nouns, past participles with subjects in certain tenses, pronouns with verbs. The web connects everything. |
| **Spanish** | The Conjugation Tower | Each verb person has an ending. The tower of endings is regular for most verbs. The stem might change (stem-changers) but the tower is predictable. |
| **Arabic** | The Root Garden | Three consonants = a root. Patterns of vowels and affixes are planted in the root like seeds in soil. Different patterns = different word categories. Same root, different pattern = related meaning. |

Introduce each language's metaphor in A1 and return to it every time new grammar is taught.

### Pillar 4: Culture as Explanation
Culture is not decoration. It is the explanation for why grammar works the way it does. Every cultural anchor must connect to the grammar of the unit.

The question to answer in every cultural anchor:
> "Why does this language work this way? What aspect of this culture made this grammatical structure necessary?"

Examples:
- Korean honorifics: Because Confucian hierarchy requires every utterance to encode the speaker's relationship to the listener. Not politeness — social cartography.
- Dutch directness: Because Dutch trading culture valued clear, unambiguous communication. Why Dutch has fewer politeness layers than French.
- German word order: Because Germanic languages evolved to front the most important information (V2 rule) while keeping the frame closed (verb at end). Information structure, not arbitrary rule.
- French gender: Because all nouns in Latin had gender, and French evolved from Latin. The gender is a historical fossil — but knowing it lets you predict adjective agreement.
- Arabic VSO order: Because in Classical Arabic, the verb carried so much information (person, gender, number) that the subject was often predictable from the verb alone. The verb comes first because it often contains the whole sentence.

### Pillar 5: Reframe Every Hard Thing (unchanged)
Every language's "scary" concepts have a logical structure. The curriculum's job is to find that logic and present it as a feature, not a bug.

| Language | "Scary" concept | The reframe |
|----------|----------------|-------------|
| Korean | 7 speech levels | "You need 2 in daily life. 1 more for work. The other 4 are academic. Three = complete." |
| Korean | 은/는 vs 이/가 | "Topic vs new information. Once you feel it, you never get it wrong." |
| German | 4 cases | "Only 4 patterns to learn for articles. After 200 sentences, the ear does it automatically." |
| German | Separable verbs | "The prefix separates and goes to the end. It's not unpredictable — it's consistent." |
| French | Gender | "Roughly 2/3 of nouns follow phonetic patterns. Learn the sounds, not the genders." |
| French | Passé composé vs imparfait | "Completed action vs background state. Same distinction English makes with 'I was eating' vs 'I ate'." |
| Spanish | Ser vs estar | "Permanent identity vs current state. Once you feel it, you never confuse them again." |
| Spanish | Subjunctive | "Doubt, desire, emotion, and hypotheticals. The mood of the unreal." |
| Arabic | Script | "28 letters, all consonants. Vowels are context. Once you know the 28, you can read anything." |
| Arabic | Broken plurals | "They follow patterns. There are 30+ plural patterns but frequency-ranked, only 10 cover most words." |

### Pillar 6: Anti-Cramming Doctrine (P54, D110) — THE MOST IMPORTANT CONTENT RULE

Content must NEVER be crammed to fit a predetermined unit count, lesson count, or step count. The curriculum exists to serve the LANGUAGE, not a spreadsheet. This is the foundational principle of LingoVerse's polyglot vision.

**The doctrine:**
- If a language needs 50 units and 400 lessons to properly teach A1-B2, it gets 50 units and 400 lessons. If it only needs 10 units, it gets 10 units.
- The number of units per CEFR level is determined by the CONCEPTS that need to be taught at that level, compared against established language learning models (textbooks, official exam frameworks, major teaching platforms). NOT by a template copied from another language.
- Every grammar construct deserves the space it needs. A concept that gets a full unit in a textbook does not get squeezed into one lesson. A concept that's a single lesson in a textbook does not get inflated into a unit.
- Agents must approach each language open-mindedly: FIRST catalogue ALL concepts that CEFR requires at each level for this specific language, THEN determine how many units and lessons are needed to teach them properly, THEN build. Never start with "30 units, 8 lessons each" and work backwards.
- 18 steps is a FLOOR, not a target. Complex grammar may need 25-30 steps. Simple vocabulary may need exactly 18. Build what the content needs.
- NEVER add filler to reach a count. NEVER compress to fit a quota. The learner's comprehension is the only metric.
- Every language is different. Every language deserves its own lesson-count, its own unit-count, its own breadth and depth. Source language proximity matters: a Dutch speaker learning German needs different pacing than an Arabic speaker learning German.

**The violation that created this pillar:**
In March 2026, German, French, and Spanish were all built with a mechanical 8-8-8-6 or 8-8-7-6 CEFR distribution template. The gold standards (Korean/Dutch) use 6-4-10-10. The result: 16 units on beginner material that could fit in 10, and only 6 units on advanced material that needs 10. The owner caught this by visual inspection in seconds. Twelve audit agents running for hours did not. This pillar ensures that unit counts and CEFR distributions are always concept-driven, never template-driven.

### CEFR Distribution Rules (P51)

Every audit and every build MUST validate the unit-to-CEFR-level mapping:
1. B1+B2 combined must NEVER have fewer units than A1+A2 combined (advanced levels are where real learning depth lives)
2. Distribution must be justified per language based on that language's actual complexity at each CEFR level
3. Sub-level labels must be consistent within each language (no gaps, no jumps)
4. Compare against gold standards (Korean: 6-4-10-10, Dutch: 6-4-10-10) and explain deviations

### Strict Teach-Before-Use (P52)

"Taught" means the word has its OWN dedicated teach card with target-language and source-language fields (currently named `nl` and `en` for legacy reasons):
- A word appearing ONLY in another card's `example` field does NOT count as taught
- A word mentioned ONLY in a `deepDive` does NOT count as taught
- Every quiz word must trace to a prior dedicated teach card
- If a teach card's example uses an untaught word, that word MUST be explained in `note` or `deepDive`

### Audit Completeness (P53) — EXPANDED TO 15 ITEMS (D112)

Every cross-language audit MUST pass ALL 15 checks:
1. CEFR distribution (P51)
2. P8 all 5 leak types (20+ samples per level per language)
3. P52 strict teach-before-use (FULL automated verification)
4. P48 step type correctness
5. P49 no meta-curriculum
6. P22c no em-dashes
7. Density per lesson (P43)
8. board:true
9. Sub-level label consistency
10. P55 vocabulary completeness (ALL official exam vocabulary as teach cards)
11. P57 grammar completeness (ALL official exam grammar taught + practiced + recycled)
12. P58 communicative functions coverage
13. Synonym coverage from official lists
14. P56 unit count adequacy (concept-driven, not template)
15. Exam simulation readiness (learner could pass official exam using only LingoVerse)

Missing ANY = audit INCOMPLETE. An audit that passes 14 of 15 is NOT a PASS.

### Pillar 7: Vocabulary Completeness (P55, D112) — CERTIFICATION-GRADE

Every language must achieve certification-grade vocabulary completeness. Every word on the official certification exam vocabulary list at each CEFR level must have a dedicated teach card.

**Official sources per language**: TOPIK vocabulary (Korean), NT2 Basiswoordenlijst (Dutch), Goethe-Wortliste (German), DELF vocabulary (French), PCIC Nociones especificas (Spanish).

**Verification**: Automated script compares official list against extracted teach card `nl:` fields. Zero gaps = PASS.

**Why this pillar exists (D112)**: No previous audit verified actual vocabulary against official exam lists. The question "does every word on the TOPIK vocabulary list have a teach card?" was never asked.

### Pillar 8: Concept-Driven Unit Sizing (P56, D112)

Unit count follows from concept cataloguing, never templates. FSI Category V languages (Korean, 2,200 hours) should NOT have the same unit count as Category I languages (Spanish, 600 hours) by default. Korean A2 with 4 units is flagged for expansion.

### Pillar 9: Zero Grammar Gaps (P57, D112)

Every grammar construct on official exam lists must be taught (teach/tip card), practiced (3+ quiz steps), and recycled (2+ later lessons). Full grammar inventory tables required per language per level. Zero gaps.

### Pillar 10: Communicative Functions (P58, D112)

CEFR defines what learners can DO (introduce self, express opinions, write essays). Every communicative function at each level must map to practice lessons. Grammar and vocabulary without communicative practice is incomplete.

# PART 2: THE PIPELINE RULES

## What Is the Pipeline?

The pipeline is the set of rules that guarantee content quality regardless of who is writing. Each rule has a number (P-number) and exists because a mistake was caught. When a rule says NON-NEGOTIABLE, that means exactly that.

---

## P8: ANTI-LEAK SYSTEM (Questions) — NON-NEGOTIABLE

Every exercise must be unanswerable without the target skill.

**Before adding any question, ask:** "Can this be answered by pattern-matching, reading the question, or guessing without actually knowing the language?"

Five leak types to eliminate:

| Leak type | Description | Fix |
|-----------|-------------|-----|
| **Visual leak** | Answer appears visually in the question text | Remove answer from question |
| **Script leak** | Target script glyph appears in question when task is to identify it | Use sound/meaning as question, script as answer |
| **Hint leak** | Hint reveals the answer rather than guiding | Rewrite hint to guide the skill, not the answer |
| **Pattern leak** | Answer is significantly longer or shorter than distractors | Equalize distractor length |
| **Position leak** | Correct answer always appears in same position | Randomize position |

**The core rule:** Question and answer must use DIFFERENT representations.
- Question = meaning/sound/source language → Answer = target language form
- Question = target language → Answer = meaning/translation

---

## P24: COMPOUND DECOMPOSITION (LEGO Principle) — NON-NEGOTIABLE

Every word made of multiple morphemes must be decomposed on FIRST appearance.

**Format:**
```
note: "학생 = 학 (study/학문) + 생 (person/생명). Literally: study-person."
```

**This applies to ALL languages:**
- Korean: 학교 = 학(study) + 교(teach). 도서관 = 도서(books) + 관(building).
- German: Krankenhaus = Kranken(sick/ill) + haus(house). Hospital, literally.
- French: bibliothèque = biblio(book) + thèque(place for storing). Book-store.
- Spanish: paraguas = para(for/against) + aguas(waters). Umbrella, literally.
- Dutch: vliegtuig = vlieg(fly) + tuig(equipment). Aircraft, literally.
- Arabic: مَكْتَبَة = k-t-b root (writing) + ma- prefix (place of) + -a suffix. Place of writing = library.

---

## P26: SHOW BEFORE NAME (The Spiral) — NON-NEGOTIABLE

Grammar must be SHOWN before it is NAMED.

**The rule:** Every grammar item that gets a formal naming lesson must have been present in at least 2 units of natural-context exposure before the naming lesson.

**The harvest lesson format:**
1. Begin with: "You've been seeing/using [X] since Unit [N]."
2. Show 3–5 examples from previous lessons where it appeared
3. Now give it its name and formal description
4. Practice exercises that feel like confirmation, not introduction

**Never name grammar cold.** If a grammar item has not been seeded, plant seeds in the current unit and delay the naming lesson by 2 units.

**Seeds must be documented.** Use the seed inventory in each language's curriculum document to track what has been planted and where.

---

## P32: JRTF (Just Remember This Form) FLAG SYSTEM

For forms that appear before their explanation unit, use the JRTF flag system.

**When to use JRTF:**
- A grammar form appears naturally in context
- The unit is 2+ units before the explanation lesson
- Removing it would make the dialogue/example unnatural

**How to use JRTF:**
```javascript
{type:"tip", title:"⚡ JRTF: Just Remember This Form",
 text:"[Form] appears in this sentence. Don't worry about how it works yet — just notice it. It gets a full lesson in Unit [N]."}
```

**JRTF is a seed-planting tool.** Every JRTF creates a debt: the explanation lesson must come and it must say "you saw this in Unit X."

---

## P39: TIP CARD DISCIPLINE

**Tip cards are for:**
- Cultural anchors (how this culture explains the grammar)
- System overviews (the full picture of a category)
- Reframe cards (making a scary concept logical)
- JRTF flags (flagging early appearances)
- Morpheme bank updates

**Tip cards are NOT for:**
- Teaching new vocabulary without exercises
- Explaining grammar that should have its own teach card
- Information dumps with no connection to the lesson's content
- Pure trivia with no grammar or culture connection

**Deep dive discipline:** If a tip has optional advanced content, put it in a `deepDive` field. Keep the main text scannable (5–8 bullet points or 3–4 short paragraphs maximum).

---

## D58: NOTE FORMATTING STANDARDS — NON-NEGOTIABLE

Every `note:` field in a teach card must follow this format:

**DO:**
```
note: "Short explanation.\n\nExamples on separate lines:\n가다 → 가요\n먹다 → 먹어요\n\nOne key rule or contrast."
```

**DO NOT:**
```
note: "This is a really long paragraph that goes on and on without any structure explaining many different things at once including the rule and exceptions and examples and cultural context all mixed together without any breathing room..."
```

Rules:
- Maximum 3 logical sections in a note
- Blank line (\\n\\n) between sections
- Examples on their own lines
- No nested explanations (that's what deepDive is for)
- Korean characters on their own centered line, not inline with English text

---

## P8-EXTENDED: STEP TYPE STANDARDS

### intro steps
```javascript
{type:"intro", title:"[Short title]", desc:"[2-3 sentences max — the hook]", goals:["Goal 1","Goal 2","Goal 3"]}
```
Goals: 3 items, each a concrete thing learner will be able to do.

### teach steps
```javascript
{type:"teach", nl:"[target word/phrase]", en:"[English]", phonetic:"[romanization or IPA]",
 example:"[Target language sentence]", exampleEn:"[English translation]",
 note:"[Structured note — see D58]"}
```
Required fields: nl, en, example, exampleEn, note. Phonetic: required for A1-A2, optional A2+ (based on language romanization phase-out rules).

### tip steps
```javascript
{type:"tip", title:"[emoji + title]", text:"[Main content — max 8 bullets or 4 paragraphs]",
 deepDive:{title:"[Deep dive title]", text:"[Advanced content for polyglots and curious learners]"}}
```

### mc steps
```javascript
{type:"mc", q:"[Question]", opts:["Option A","Option B","Option C","Option D"],
 ans:"[Exact text of correct option]"}
```
- 3–4 options per question
- Options must all be similar length (P8)
- Correct answer must not always be in same position (P8)
- Question must not contain the answer (P8)

### fb steps
```javascript
{type:"fb", q:"[Sentence with ___ for blank]", ans:"[Answer]",
 hint:"[Guiding hint — must not reveal answer]"}
```

### match steps
```javascript
{type:"match", pairs:[["Left 1","Right 1"],["Left 2","Right 2"],["Left 3","Right 3"],["Left 4","Right 4"]]}
```
4 pairs preferred. Left = target language or question, Right = translation or answer.

### drag_fill steps
```javascript
{type:"drag_fill", sentence:"[Sentence with ___]", options:["correct","distractor1","distractor2"],
 answer:"correct"}
```
- 3 options minimum (1 correct + 2 distractors)
- Distractors must be plausible (similar form, related meaning — not random)

# PART 3: PER-LANGUAGE CURRICULUM STANDARDS

## 🇰🇷 Korean — Special Considerations

Korean is the most morphologically complex of the launch languages. It receives maximum density and maximum unit count.

**Unique Korean pipeline rules:**

1. **Irregular verb batching:** 5 batches in specific units. Never teach an irregular verb before its batch lesson. Use JRTF for any early appearances.
   - Batch 1 (ㄷ/ㅂ): U7 L5
   - Batch 2 (ㄹ/으): U9 L7
   - Batch 3 (ㅎ): U11 L9
   - Batch 4 (ㅅ): U12 L9
   - Batch 5 (르): U13 L5

2. **Vowel harmony:** Never teach a new verb ending without noting whether it triggers -아 or -어. This is not optional — every ending introduction must show the harmony pattern.

3. **Romanization phase-out:**
   - U1–U3: Romanization shown on all vocabulary
   - U4–U6: Romanization on hover only
   - U7+: No romanization except for phonetically irregular forms

4. **The Train metaphor** must be referenced every time a new verb ending is introduced.

5. **SOV enforcement:** Every example sentence must end with a verb. If editing an example and the verb is not last, it is wrong.

---

## 🇳🇱 Dutch — Special Considerations

Dutch is the anchor course. It is the most complete. However:

1. **The legacy track (23 units) is below density standard.** After Korean B1 is complete, Dutch v2 and legacy tracks receive retroactive polish to match new card standards.

2. **NT2 alignment:** Dutch content must be auditable against NT2 A2 before declaring A2 complete. The inburgeringsexamen (civic integration exam) represents the real-world A2 standard.

3. **'t Kofschip rule** for regular past tense formation is uniquely Dutch and must be taught as a system (U7 L2 in the Dutch curriculum).

4. **The "er" system** (er + preposition, er as subject placeholder, er as location, partitive er, passive er) is as complex as Korean's -는데 system. It receives dedicated treatment in Dutch U14.

5. **De vs het gender:** Only two grammatical genders but no simple rule. Must be taught with each noun from lesson 1, not explained as a system. The only rule: diminutives (-je) are always het.

---

## 🇩🇪 German — Special Considerations

German A1-B2 COMPLETE (D103, 30 units, 240 lessons, 4,518 steps). The following rules were applied during the build and remain active for future audits/C1 expansion.

**German curriculum non-negotiables:**

1. **Cases must be systematic:** Nominative (who/what does the action), Accusative (direct object), Dative (indirect object, prepositions), Genitive (possession). Each case gets its own introduction lesson. Do not scatter case teaching across vocabulary lessons.

2. **Article system:** der/die/das (nominative) must be taught WITH each noun from lesson 1. The case endings are taught systematically in the case lessons.

3. **Verb position (V2 rule):** This is as fundamental to German as SOV is to Korean. Introduce it in A1 L1 and enforce it in every example sentence.

4. **Separable verbs:** Must receive dedicated unit treatment. Never teach a separable verb without noting the separated prefix goes to the end.

5. **Modal verbs** must be taught as a family (können, müssen, dürfen, sollen, wollen, mögen/möchten).

6. **Strong vs weak verb distinction:** Weak verbs (regular) get -te past. Strong verbs (irregular) have vowel changes. Group them. The German strong verb system is smaller than English's and teachable as a system.

7. **Phonology requirements in Foundations:**
   - ü, ö, ä — front rounded vowels
   - ß (sharp s) — when to use vs ss
   - ch — two pronunciations (after front vowels: palatal; after back vowels: velar)
   - Devoicing at word-final position (d→t, b→p, g→k)

---

## 🇫🇷 French — Special Considerations

French A1-B2 COMPLETE (D105, 30 units, 240 lessons, 4,380 steps). The following rules were applied during the build and remain active for future audits/C1 expansion.

**French curriculum non-negotiables:**

1. **Gender from lesson 1.** Every noun introduction: le/la/un/une. Never introduce a noun without its article. The article IS part of the word.

2. **Liaison and elision in Foundations.** You cannot teach French vocabulary without explaining why "le + hôtel = l'hôtel" and why "les enfants" sounds like "lezenfants." This affects every word boundary.

3. **Nasal vowels in Foundations.** English speakers hear "an," "in," "on" as individual sounds. French nasals are different phonemes that must be trained before vocabulary can be pronounced correctly.

4. **Verb groups as systems:**
   - Group 1 (-er): 90% of verbs. The regular system. Learn this first.
   - Group 2 (-ir with -iss-): Regular secondary system.
   - Group 3 (irregular): être, avoir, aller, faire, vouloir, pouvoir, devoir — the most common verbs. Teach these as individual entries, not a "group."

5. **Avoir vs être in passé composé:** All movement/state verbs use être (DR/MRS VANDERTRAMP). All others use avoir. This distinction must receive dedicated treatment.

6. **Passé composé vs imparfait** needs a full unit at A2, not one lesson. It is the most tested DELF item.

7. **Liaison rules:** Mandatory (les enfants), optional (nous avons), forbidden (et_il). Teach the three categories.

---

## 🇪🇸 Spanish — Special Considerations

Spanish A1-B2 COMPLETE (D108, 30 units, 240 lessons, 4,363 steps). The following rules were applied during the build and remain active for future audits/C1 expansion.

**Spanish curriculum non-negotiables:**

1. **Ser vs estar from lesson 1.** This is the most fundamental Spanish distinction. It requires:
   - Early exposure (A1) with clear examples
   - Regular return visits as more contexts are introduced
   - Formal naming lesson at A2 with the complete rule set

2. **Two genders, but more predictable than French.** Most nouns ending in -o are masculine, -a are feminine. Exceptions must be explicitly flagged (el problema, la mano).

3. **Reflexive verbs as a system.** 30-40 common verbs are reflexive. Teach the system (me/te/se/nos/os/se), not individual verbs.

4. **Stem-changing verbs.** Three patterns (e→ie, o→ue, e→i) in present tense. Teach as a category, not case-by-case.

5. **Direct and indirect object pronouns + their order.** The combinations (me lo, te lo, se lo) are fixed and must receive explicit treatment.

6. **Subjunctive at B1 — it cannot be deferred to B2.** DELE B1 tests present subjunctive in wish (quiero que...), doubt (no creo que...), and emotion (me alegra que...) contexts.

7. **Preterito indefinido vs imperfecto** needs a full unit, not one lesson. It is the most tested DELE item.

---

## 🇸🇦 Arabic — Special Considerations

Arabic is simultaneously a target language AND a source language. This creates dual requirements.

**As a target language:**

1. **Script is mandatory before vocabulary.** The Arabic alphabet must be fully mastered in Foundations. This includes: all 28 letters, connected forms (initial/medial/final/isolated), short vowel diacritics (fatha/kasra/damma), sukun, shadda, and tanwin.

2. **The diglossia decision must be made.** LingoVerse teaches Modern Standard Arabic (MSA) as the primary register, with acknowledgment of major dialect families (Egyptian, Levantine, Gulf, Maghrebi) in cultural anchors.

3. **The trilateral root system is the LEGO Principle for Arabic.** It must be introduced early (A1 U2) and reinforced constantly. Every new vocabulary item: trace it to its root.

4. **Broken plurals must be taught as patterns.** There are 30+ patterns but frequency-ranked: the top 10 cover 80%+ of common words. Teach patterns, not individual plurals.

5. **Gender agreement is pervasive.** Every adjective, verb, and pronoun must agree with its noun's gender. This is not like French (adjective only) — it affects the verb too.

6. **VSO word order** (Verb-Subject-Object in classical Arabic; SVO becoming common in MSA). Introduce explicitly. Contrast with English SVO.

7. **Dual forms.** Arabic has singular, dual, and plural. The dual suffix (-ان nominative, -ين oblique) must be taught as a system.

**As a source language (instructional text in Arabic):**

The instructional layer (tip cards, hints, grammar notes) must be written in Modern Standard Arabic that is accessible to educated speakers of major dialect groups. Avoid highly formal Classical Arabic vocabulary in instructional text. Use clear, accessible MSA.

# PART 4: THE SEED FRAMEWORK — ALL LANGUAGES

## What Seeds Are

Seeds are deliberate early appearances of grammar concepts before their formal naming lesson. They are not accidents. They are not optional. They are the mechanism by which the P26 spiral works.

## Universal Seed Rules

1. **Every seed must be documented.** Add it to the language's seed inventory before finishing the lesson.

2. **Every seed must have a harvest date.** You cannot plant a seed without knowing when it will be harvested. If you don't know where it will be named, you're not planting a seed — you're leaking information.

3. **Seeds use one of three forms:**
   - **Natural appearance** (no flag): grammar appears in dialogue/example naturally. Best for very frequent items.
   - **JRTF flag:** brief tip noting the form exists, will be explained later. For items that might confuse learners.
   - **Notice tip:** "Just notice this pattern — you don't need to understand it yet." For patterns the learner is likely to see multiple times.

4. **Seeds must not answer their own harvest lesson.** A seed that explains the grammar destroys the harvest. Seeds show. They never explain.

## Seed Inventory Template (use for every language)

Every language curriculum document must maintain a seed inventory table:

```markdown
| Grammar item | First shown | Unit | Lesson | Form | Harvest |
|-------------|-------------|------|--------|------|---------|
| [item] | [how it appears] | [U#] | [L#] | [natural/JRTF/notice] | [U# L#] |
```

# PART 5: CULTURAL ANCHOR STANDARDS

## The Cultural Anchor Formula

Every unit must have at least one cultural anchor. It must answer:
> "Why does this language work this way? What about this culture made this grammatical structure necessary?"

**Template:**
1. Open with the cultural fact (concise, interesting)
2. Connect it explicitly to the grammar of the unit
3. Give 1–2 concrete examples of how the grammar reflects the culture
4. Close with what this means for the learner's practical communication

## Cultural Anchors by Language — What to Cover

### Korean
- 정 (jeong): interpersonal bonds → explains 우리 vs 나, explains why Koreans share food, explains age questions
- 눈치: social reading → explains indirect grammar (-는데 softening, -것 같아요 hedging)
- 빨리빨리: speed culture → explains 배달, transit efficiency, Korea's development pace
- 나이 계층: age hierarchy → explains speech levels, explains why Koreans ask your age immediately
- 한류: Korean wave → explains vocabulary domains, explains why Korean is worth learning

### Dutch
- Directheid (directness): why Dutch say what they mean → explains simpler politeness system vs French
- 正直さ vs formality: why Dutch are direct but also law-abiding → explains formal register contexts
- Gezelligheid: coziness/togetherness → explains 자주-used words like gezin, samen, genieten
- Cycling culture → explains Dutch transport vocabulary, spatial language

### German
- Gründlichkeit (thoroughness): explains why German has rules for rules → connects to grammatical precision
- Pünktlichkeit (punctuality): explains time expressions, why vague time words are less common
- Formality: Sie vs du → connects to V2/grammar formality, workplace language

### French
- Politesse: layers of formality → connects to vous/tu, formal register vocabulary
- Culture du dîner: meal culture → connects to vocabulary, explains temporal language around meals
- L'Académie française: language preservation → connects to why French resists anglicisms

### Spanish
- Hora española: flexible time culture → contrasts with German, explains context of temporal language
- Family centrality: family as social unit → explains vocabulary priorities, politeness through family terms
- Tuteo vs ustedeo: regional variation in tú/usted → connects to register, connects to dialect geography

### Arabic
- ضيافة (hospitality): guest culture → explains formal greeting sequence, why certain phrases are obligatory
- الإسلام (Islam) in language: explains السلام عليكم, إن شاء الله, الحمد لله as linguistic facts not religious statements
- فصحى vs عامية (MSA vs dialect): explains why Arabic seems to "have two versions"
- الأسرة (family): explains naming conventions (أبو, أم + child's name)

- Agents write A2 content without re-reading A1 seeds (because A1 is 5,000 lines above)

- No machine-readable list of what grammar has been planted where
- Every session requires re-reading thousands of lines of content to know what exists
