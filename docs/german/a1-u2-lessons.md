### Cumulative Vocab: 149 + 22 = **171**

### Step Sequence
1. **intro** — Episode title, scene description: Verumius at Brandenburger Tor, pink dawn, paper map upside down. Goals: learn Berlin landmarks, first direction words.
2. **teach** — Straße(die) / "the street" — Fun: Germany has ~650,000 streets. The longest name? Bischofszeller Strasse. Gender: die Straße (crimson).
3. **teach** — Platz(der) / "the square/plaza" — Fun: Alexanderplatz was named after Russian Tsar Alexander I. Gender: der Platz (blue).
4. **teach** — Brücke(die) / "the bridge" — Fun: Berlin has more bridges than Venice (~1,700!). Gender: die Brücke (crimson).
5. **teach** — Kirche(die) / "the church" — Fun: The Gedächtniskirche was deliberately left half-ruined as a peace memorial. Gender: die Kirche (crimson).
6. **teach** — Rathaus(das) / "the city hall" — Fun: Berlin's Rathaus is called "Rotes Rathaus" because of its red bricks, not politics. Gender: das Rathaus (green).
7. **cinematic scene 1** — Narrator sets the scene: Verumius stands at Brandenburger Tor, pink sky, holding a paper map upside down. He reads street names aloud, mispronouncing everything. Exposes: Straße, Platz, Kirche, stehen. Direction: camera pans across Berlin landmarks.
8. **teach** — links / "left" — Fun: In old Germanic, "links" was considered the unlucky side. Left-handers were forced to switch!
9. **teach** — rechts / "right" — Fun: "Recht" also means "right/correct" and "law." Rechts = right in every sense.
10. **teach** — geradeaus / "straight ahead" — Fun: Literally "straight-out." Germans love compound words for directions.
11. **teach** — wohin / "where to" — Fun: German splits "where" into wo (where/location) and wohin (where to/direction). English lost this distinction centuries ago.
12. **cinematic scene 2** — Verumius flips the map, realizes it was upside down. Sees signs for U-Bahn, Bus. Mutters "Wohin... wohin gehe ich?" Exposes: Bahnhof, U-Bahn, Bus, Taxi, Denkmal, Museum, Hotel, da, dort, Richtung, suchen, sehen, stehen. Camera follows him walking tentatively toward an intersection.
13. **teach batch (remaining 8)** — Denkmal(das) "the monument" (green, Fun: Germany has ~100,000 protected monuments), Museum(das) "the museum" (green, Fun: Berlin's Museumsinsel has 5 world-class museums on one island), Hotel(das) "the hotel" (green, Fun: same word in most European languages, from French hôtel), Bus(der) "the bus" (blue, Fun: from Latin "omnibus" = for everyone), U-Bahn(die) "the subway" (crimson, Fun: U = Untergrund, Berlin's first line opened 1902), Bahnhof(der) "the train station" (blue, Fun: Berlin Hauptbahnhof is Europe's largest crossing station), Taxi(das) "the taxi" (green, Fun: Berlin taxis are always cream/beige by law), Richtung(die) "the direction" (crimson, Fun: -ung ending = always feminine. Always!).
14. **tip** — "Gender color code: der = blue (masculine), die = crimson (feminine), das = green (neuter). Watch the colors on every word card. Your brain will start seeing patterns."

2. **teach** — Apotheke(die) / "the pharmacy" — Fun: German pharmacies have a red A symbol. They sell medicine, not shampoo. For shampoo, you need a Drogerie. Gender: die Apotheke (crimson).
3. **teach** — Bank(die) / "the bank" — Fun: Same word, same meaning as English. But die Bank also means "the bench." Context is everything. Gender: die Bank (crimson).
4. **teach** — Bibliothek(die) / "the library" — Fun: Berlin's Staatsbibliothek has over 11 million books. Gender: die Bibliothek (crimson).
5. **teach** — Bäckerei(die) / "the bakery" — Fun: Germany has ~11,000 bakeries. That's more than McDonald's, Burger King, and Starbucks combined. Words ending in -ei are always feminine. Gender: die Bäckerei (crimson).
6. **teach** — Café(das) / "the café" — Fun: das Café = the place, der Kaffee = the drink. Don't mix them up! Gender: das Café (green).
7. **teach** — Geschäft(das) / "the shop/store" — Fun: Also means "business" (as in Geschäftsmann = businessman). Gender: das Geschäft (green).
8. **teach** — Innenstadt(die) / "the city center" — Fun: Literally "inner city." Germans build nouns by stacking: Innen+Stadt. Gender: die Innenstadt (crimson).
9. **teach** — Kino(das) / "the cinema" — Fun: From Greek "kinema" = movement. Germans say "Wir gehen ins Kino" even for Netflix dates. Gender: das Kino (green).
10. **teach** — Laden(der) / "the shop" — Fun: More casual than Geschäft. A Laden feels small and personal. A Geschäft feels professional. Gender: der Laden (blue).
11. **teach** — Markt(der) / "the market" — Fun: Every German town has a weekly Wochenmarkt. Berlin's Turkish Market on the Maybachufer is legendary. Gender: der Markt (blue).
12. **mc** — Wo ist ___? Which place would you go for bread? opts: [die Bäckerei, die Bank, die Bibliothek, die Apotheke] ans: die Bäckerei. Hint: Think of Bäcker (baker).
13. **teach** — Parkplatz(der) / "the parking space" — Fun: Park+Platz = parking+space. Compound nouns take the gender of the LAST word (Platz = der). Gender: der Parkplatz (blue).
14. **teach** — Restaurant(das) / "the restaurant" — Fun: Pronounced more German than French: res-toh-RAHNG. Gender: das Restaurant (green).
15. **teach** — Schloss(das) / "the castle/palace" — Fun: Also means "lock"! das Schloss = the castle AND the lock. Context saves you. Gender: das Schloss (green).
16. **teach** — Theater(das) / "the theater" — Fun: Pronounced "teh-AH-ter." Berlin has over 150 theaters, more than any other city in the world. Gender: das Theater (green).
17. **match** — pairs: [{nl:"die Apotheke", en:"pharmacy"}, {nl:"das Café", en:"café"}, {nl:"der Markt", en:"market"}, {nl:"die Bäckerei", en:"bakery"}, {nl:"das Kino", en:"cinema"}]
18. **teach** — Stadtplan(der) / "the city map" — Fun: Stadt+Plan. Verumius should have used a better one. Gender: der Stadtplan (blue).
19. **teach** — Eingang(der) / "the entrance" — Fun: Ein+Gang = "in+walk." The exit? Ausgang = "out+walk." Gender: der Eingang (blue).
20. **teach** — Rundfahrt(die) / "the sightseeing tour" — Fun: Rund+Fahrt = "round+ride." Literally a round trip of the sights. Gender: die Rundfahrt (crimson).
21. **teach batch (remaining transport + directions)** — Auto(das) (green, Fun: Germany invented the automobile. Karl Benz, 1886.), Bahn(die) (crimson, Fun: die Bahn = the train/railway system, short for Deutsche Bahn), Bahnsteig(der) (blue, Fun: Bahn+Steig = "train+platform," literally where you climb onto the train), Fahrrad(das) (green, Fun: Fahr+Rad = "drive+wheel"), S-Bahn(die) (crimson, Fun: S = Schnell = fast, the above-ground city train), Straßenbahn(die) (crimson, Fun: Straßen+Bahn = "street+train" = tram), Verbindung(die) (crimson, Fun: -ung ending = always feminine. Means "connection," especially transit connections), Zug(der) (blue, Fun: also means "pull" and "feature." A very versatile German word), Ecke(die) (crimson, Fun: "um die Ecke" = around the corner, one of the most useful phrases), Haltestelle(die) (crimson, Fun: Halte+Stelle = "stop+place" = bus/tram stop), Kreuzung(die) (crimson, Fun: from Kreuz = cross. An intersection where streets cross), Norden(der) (blue, Fun: compass directions are always masculine in German), Süden(der) (blue, Fun: Berlin used to be split: West in the west, and the Wall ran north-south through the center).
22. **mc** — Which word means "connection" (for transit)? opts: [die Verbindung, die Kreuzung, die Haltestelle, der Bahnsteig] ans: die Verbindung. Hint: The -ung ending tells you it's a process or result of connecting.
23. **mc** — You need medicine. You look for ___. opts: [die Apotheke, die Bäckerei, die Bank, das Café] ans: die Apotheke. Hint: The red A symbol marks these in Germany.

24. **teach** — Wagen(der) / "the car, vehicle" — Fun: More versatile than "Auto": Wagen can also mean train carriage. In a train: "Wagen 12, Platz 34" = car 12, seat 34. Der Wagen is the general word; das Auto is the everyday colloquial word. Gender: der Wagen (blue). A: "Nehmen wir den Bus oder den Wagen?" B: "Nehmen wir den Bus — der Wagen ist kaputt!"

25. **tip** — "Gender pattern spotlight: Words ending in -ung, -ei, -keit, -heit, -ie are ALWAYS feminine (die). Words ending in -chen are ALWAYS neuter (das). Compound nouns take the gender of the LAST word. These rules cover hundreds of nouns."

- **Exposed:** Modal verbs können/müssen/dürfen (used naturally, not formally drilled yet)

### Communicative Functions
- #16 Ask for help / make requests — PRACTICE (Können Sie mir helfen? Entschuldigung, wo ist...?)
- #8 Ask for and give directions — PRACTICE (Gehen Sie geradeaus, dann links.)
- #2 Introduce others — practice (Das ist die Bibliothek. Das ist der Markt.)

### Cumulative Vocab: 202 + 29 = **231**

### Step Sequence
1. **intro** — "Verumius has a map, but no phone signal. Time to talk to actual humans. In German."
2. **teach** — können / "can, to be able to" — Fun: Können Sie...? is the magic phrase. It opens every polite request in German.
3. **teach** — müssen / "must, to have to" — Fun: Unlike English "must," German müssen is just neutral obligation. "Ich muss gehen" = "I have to go," not dramatic.
4. **teach** — dürfen / "may, to be allowed to" — Fun: "Darf ich?" = "May I?" The politeness superpower.
5. **teach** — möchten / "would like" — Fun: Technically a subjunctive form of mögen. Germans use it more than "ich will" (which sounds demanding).
6. **teach** — bitte / "please" — Fun: Also means "you're welcome" AND "here you go." One word, three English phrases.
7. **teach** — doch / "yes (contradicting no) / indeed" — Fun: Untranslatable! Someone says "Du sprichst kein Deutsch." You say "Doch!" = "Yes I DO!" English has no single word for this.
8. **teach** — mal / "just, once (softener)" — Fun: "Schau mal" = "just look." Mal makes everything sound friendlier and less demanding.
9. **bubble scene 1** — Verumius approaches a woman at a street corner. Uses "Entschuldigung, können Sie mir helfen?" She responds with rapid directions using links, rechts, geradeaus. Verumius nods but understands nothing. Exposes: geben, zeigen, schauen, an, auf, bei, nach, neben, zu, dann, denn, jemand.
10. **teach** — nach / "to, toward (for cities/countries), after" — Fun: "nach Berlin" = to Berlin. "nach links" = to the left. "nach dem Essen" = after the meal.
11. **teach** — zu / "to (for specific places/people)" — Fun: "zum Bahnhof" = to the train station. "zu" for specific places, "nach" for cities/countries. This distinction doesn't exist in English.
12. **teach** — neben / "next to" — Fun: "Die Bank ist neben der Apotheke." Neben = physically beside something.
13. **teach batch (remaining)** — geben "to give" (Fun: "Es gibt" = "there is/are," literally "it gives"), halten "to stop/hold" (Fun: Anhalten = stop completely, festhalten = hold tight), zeigen "to show/point" (Fun: "Können Sie mir zeigen?" = Can you show me?), schauen "to look" (Fun: Southern German/Austrian for "sehen." Both work.), lesen "to read" (Fun: irregular: ich lese, du liest. The vowel changes!), schreiben "to write" (Fun: cognate with English "scribe"), hören "to hear" (Fun: zuhören = to listen actively, hören = to hear passively), denken "to think" (Fun: "Ich denke, also bin ich" = I think, therefore I am. Descartes in German.), glauben "to believe" (Fun: "Ich glaube" can mean "I believe" or "I think" in casual speech), bleiben "to stay/remain" (Fun: "Bleiben Sie gesund!" = Stay healthy! A common German farewell.), an "at/on (vertical)" (Fun: an der Wand = on the wall, am Montag = on Monday), auf "on (horizontal)/at" (Fun: auf dem Tisch = on the table), bei "at/near/with" (Fun: "bei mir" = at my place), noch "still/yet" (Fun: "noch einmal" = one more time, "noch nicht" = not yet), schon "already" (Fun: "Bist du schon da?" = Are you already there?), denn "then/because (flavoring particle in questions)" (Fun: "Was ist denn das?" adds curiosity. Without denn, the question sounds flat.), dann "then/next" (Fun: "erst..., dann..." = first..., then...), jemand "someone" (Fun: jemand = someone, niemand = no one. Perfect pair.).
14. **bubble scene 2** — THE JAYWALKING SCENE. Verumius crosses on red. An elderly woman (Ältere Frau) tuts: "Das darf man nicht! Die Ampel ist rot!" Verumius freezes, mortified. Other pedestrians stare. He apologizes profusely. Exposes: dürfen, doch, noch, schon. Cultural moment: Germans NEVER jaywalk. Even at 3 AM on an empty street.
15. **mc** — Verumius asks for help. Which is most polite? opts: [Können Sie mir helfen?, Hilf mir!, Wo ist das?, Gib mir den Weg!] ans: Können Sie mir helfen? Hint: The "können Sie" formula is your politeness key.
16. **mc** — Someone says "Du sprichst kein Deutsch." You DO speak German. You reply: opts: [Doch!, Nein!, Ja!, Bitte!] ans: Doch! Hint: This word contradicts a negative statement.
17. **fb** — Entschuldigung, {1} Sie mir bitte den Weg zeigen? opts: [können, müssen, dürfen, möchten] a: können. Hint: Which modal expresses ability and polite requests?

18. **teach** — kennenlernen / "to get to know, to meet" — Fun: Separable: "Schön, Sie kennenzulernen!" = Nice to meet you! Lernen = to learn, kennen = to know. Literally: "to learn to know." A: "Darf ich Sie kennenlernen? Ich heiße Verumius." B: "Gern! Ich bin Maria. Schön, Sie kennenzulernen!"

19. **tip** — "JAYWALKING CULTURE: In Germany, crossing on red is genuinely frowned upon, especially if children are watching. You might get a lecture from a stranger. It's not law enforcement people fear. It's the social judgment. Verumius learned this the hard way."

## L4: "Der, die, das — Das Artikelmonster" — Grammar

**verbs (6):** anfangen, beginnen, aufhören, dauern, passen, tun
**prep+art (2):** im, am

### Grammar
- **Taught:** #8 Definite articles nominative (der/die/das/die — full paradigm with rules), #9 Indefinite articles nominative (ein/eine/ein — full paradigm), #13 Noun genders (pattern rules: -ung=fem, -chen=neut, -heit=fem, -ei=fem, compounds=last word), #24 Predicative adjectives (Das Hotel ist groß. - #6 Describe places and things — PRACTICE (Das Museum ist groß. Die Straße ist lang.)

### Cumulative Vocab: 231 + 19 = **250**

### Step Sequence
1. **intro** — "The German article system: der, die, das. Three genders, one monster. Let's tame it."
2. **tip** — "THE NOMINATIVE CASE: The subject of a sentence (who/what DOES the action) uses the nominative case. der Mann sieht. die Frau steht. das Kind spielt. In nominative: der = masculine, die = feminine, das = neuter, die = plural."
3. **teach** — groß / "big, tall, great" — Fun: Deutschland = "the land of the great people" (Deutsch originally meant "of the people"). A: Ist das Hotel groß? B: Ja, das Hotel ist sehr groß!
4. **teach** — klein / "small, little" — Fun: "Klein aber fein" = small but fine. A beloved German saying. A: Die Bäckerei ist klein. B: Ja, aber das Brot ist gut!
5. **teach** — lang / "long" — Fun: "Lang" for physical length, "lange" for time duration. Die Straße ist lang.
6. **teach** — kurz / "short (length/time)" — Fun: "Kurz und knapp" = short and concise. Germans value brevity.

14. **mc** — Das Hotel ist ___. (big) opts: [groß, große, großes, großer] ans: groß. Hint: After "sein" (is), adjectives have NO ending. Just the base form.
15. **teach** — breit / "wide, broad" — Fun: "Die Straße ist breit" = The street is wide. A: Ist die Brücke breit? B: Ja, sehr breit!
16. **teach** — hoch / "high, tall" — Fun: "Hochhaus" = skyscraper (literally "high house"). Hoch is irregular: ein hohes Gebäude (the -ch becomes -h- when declined). But after sein? Just "hoch."
17. **teach** — schnell / "fast, quick" — Fun: Germans on the Autobahn define "schnell" differently than most humans.
18. **teach** — langsam / "slow" — Fun: "Langsam, bitte!" = Slowly, please! Your survival phrase when Germans talk too fast.
19. **teach batch (remaining)** — anfangen "to start/begin" (Fun: separable! "Ich fange an." The "an" goes to the end.), beginnen "to begin" (Fun: non-separable synonym of anfangen. Fancier.), aufhören "to stop/quit" (Fun: separable! "Hör auf!" = Stop it!), passen "to fit/suit" (Fun: "Das passt!" = That works/fits! Very common.), tun "to do" (Fun: irregular: ich tue, du tust. "Was tun?" = What to do?), im "in the (dat.masc/neut)" (Fun: im = in + dem. Contraction is mandatory.), am "at the (dat.masc/neut)" (Fun: am = an + dem. "Am Bahnhof" = at the station.).

21. **mc** — Which is correct? opts: [Das Museum ist groß., Das Museum ist großes., Der Museum ist groß., Das Museum ist große.] ans: Das Museum ist groß. Hint: Museum is neuter (das). After "ist," adjectives get NO ending.

22. **teach** — dauern / "to last, to take (time)" — Fun: "Wie lange dauert das?" = How long does that take? One of the most useful time questions. "Das dauert noch fünf Minuten" = That takes another five minutes. A: "Wie lange dauert der Zug nach München?" B: "Das dauert ungefähr vier Stunden."
