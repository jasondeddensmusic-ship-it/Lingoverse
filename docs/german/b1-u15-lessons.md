## Lesson 1: "Moin, Hamburg!" (Hello, Hamburg!)

**Type:** Story intro (Scene 1: Hamburg Hauptbahnhof Arrival)
**Mode:** Cinematic. Hamburg Hauptbahnhof, red brick, rain, maritime blue-grey palette, cobblestones reflecting lights.
**Characters:** Verumius (arriving, happy, no umbrella), Hamburg colleague (holding a sign: "Herr Verumius")
**id:** deu_r15l1
**board:** true
**Step count:** 22
**New vocabulary (18):** Hauptbahnhof(der), Hafen(der), Region(die), Gegend(die), Gebiet(das), Nebel(der), Richtung(die), Strecke(die), Verbindung(die), Station(die), Linie(die), Metropole(die), Nordsee(die), Ostsee(die), Nacht(die), feucht, nah, nahe
**Embedded variants (2):** (none standalone, "Moin" taught as cultural note in story card)
**Grammar:** EXPOSED: Genitive case #12 (first sighting in story: "die Atmosphare des Hafens"), recycled from U14: relative clauses #10 (in story context)
**Communicative functions:** Describing locations, discussing weather, understanding regional greetings, navigating train stations
**Cumulative vocab after: 1,803**

### Step sequence:
1. **intro:** "Moin, Hamburg!" . Goals: Arrive in Hamburg with Verumius. Learn travel and geography vocabulary. Hear your first genitive construction. Experience northern German culture.
2. **story:** speaker:narrator, mood:descriptive, art:de-ham-hauptbahnhof . trg: "Hamburg Hauptbahnhof. Regen, roter Backstein, der Geruch der Nordsee in der Luft. Alles hier ist anders als in Berlin." src: "Hamburg Central Station. Rain, red brick, the smell of the North Sea in the air. Everything here is different from Berlin."
3. **story:** speaker:colleague, mood:friendly, art:de-ham-hauptbahnhof . trg: "Moin! Sie mussen Herr Verumius sein. Willkommen in Hamburg!" src: "Moin! You must be Herr Verumius. Welcome to Hamburg!"
4. **story:** speaker:verumius, mood:happy, art:de-ham-hauptbahnhof . trg: "Hallo! Danke, dass Sie mich abholen. Was bedeutet 'Moin'?" src: "Hello! Thanks for picking me up. What does 'Moin' mean?"
5. **story:** speaker:colleague, mood:amused, art:de-ham-hauptbahnhof . trg: "Moin ist unser Hallo hier im Norden. Morgens, abends, immer: Moin!" src: "Moin is our Hello here in the north. Morning, evening, always: Moin!"
6. **teach:** Hauptbahnhof(der) . Fun info: Haupt (main) + Bahnhof (train station). Every major German city has one. Hamburg Hauptbahnhof sees 550,000 travelers daily, making it one of Europe's busiest stations.
7. **teach:** Hafen(der) . Fun info: "Harbor/port." Hamburg has the third-largest port in Europe. The word is related to English "haven." Der Hafen shaped Hamburg's identity for over 800 years.
8. **teach:** Region(die) . Fun info: Borrowed from Latin "regio." Die Region. Germany has strong regional identities. A Hamburger and a Munchner might as well be from different countries.
9. **teach:** Gegend(die) . Fun info: "Area/vicinity." From "gegen" (against/toward). Die Gegend describes the area around you. "In dieser Gegend" = in this area. Less formal than Region.
10. **teach:** Gebiet(das) . Fun info: "Territory/area." More formal and official than Gegend. Das Gebiet. "Wohngebiet" = residential area, "Industriegebiet" = industrial area. Used on official signs.
11. **teach:** Nebel(der) . Fun info: "Fog." Same root as Latin "nebula." Hamburg is famous for fog and rain. "Nebel uber der Elbe" (fog over the Elbe) is practically Hamburg's motto.
12. **teach:** Richtung(die) . Fun info: "Direction." From "richten" (to direct/align). "In Richtung Hafen" = toward the harbor. You see it on every road sign in Germany.
13. **teach:** Strecke(die) . Fun info: "Route/stretch." Die Strecke. "Die Strecke Hamburg-Berlin dauert 1:45 mit dem ICE." A Strecke is a defined path from A to B.
14. **teach:** Verbindung(die) . Fun info: "Connection." Ver- + Bindung (bond). Die Verbindung. On DB (Deutsche Bahn), a "Verbindung" is your specific train connection. "Gibt es eine direkte Verbindung?" = Is there a direct connection?
15. **teach:** Station(die) . Fun info: From Latin "statio." Die Station. Used for subway stops and smaller stops. Hauptbahnhof for the main station, Station for the rest.
16. **teach:** Linie(die) . Fun info: "Line." Die Linie. "U-Bahn Linie 3" = Subway Line 3. From Latin "linea." Germans are precise: not just "take the subway" but "take Line 3."
17. **teach:** Metropole(die) . Fun info: From Greek "metropolis" (mother city). Die Metropole. Hamburg, with 1.9 million people, is Germany's second-largest city after Berlin.
18. **teach:** Nordsee(die) . Fun info: "North Sea." Nord + See (sea). Die Nordsee. Bordered by Germany, the Netherlands, Denmark, Norway, and the UK. Hamburg sits on the Elbe, 100 km from the Nordsee.
19. **teach:** Ostsee(die) . Fun info: "Baltic Sea." Ost (east) + See (sea). Die Ostsee. Germans call it "East Sea" while most other languages call it "Baltic." Same sea, different perspective.
20. **teach:** Nacht(die) . Fun info: Same root as English "night" and Latin "nox." Die Nacht. "Gute Nacht" = Good night. Hamburg's Reeperbahn is famous for its Nachtleben (nightlife).
21. **teach:** feucht . Fun info: "Damp/humid." Hamburg in one word. Not "nass" (wet), not "trocken" (dry), but feucht: that in-between dampness that gets into everything. Related to English "damp" via Proto-Germanic.
22. **teach:** nah . Fun info: "Near/close." "Der Hafen ist nah" = The harbor is near. Comparative: naher (nearer). Superlative: am nachsten (nearest). The base form for proximity.
23. **teach:** nahe . Fun info: "Near (preposition/adverb form)." "Nahe dem Hafen" = near the harbor. Takes dative. Slightly more literary than "nah." Both forms are common, used in different grammatical slots.
24. **mc:** Regional greeting . q: "A colleague greets Verumius in Hamburg. What does 'Moin' mean?" opts: ["Hello (northern German greeting)", "Good morning only", "Goodbye", "Thank you"] ans: "Hello (northern German greeting)". hint: "This greeting works at any time of day in northern Germany."
25. **mc:** Geography . q: "Hamburg liegt an der Elbe, nahe der ..." opts: [Nordsee, Ostsee, Donau, Alpen] ans: Nordsee. hint: "Hamburg faces northwest, toward the sea between Germany and England."
26. **fb:** Transport vocab . s: "Die {1} Hamburg-Berlin dauert etwa zwei Stunden." a: ["Strecke"] opts: [Strecke, Station, Linie, Richtung] hint: "Which word describes a defined route between two cities?"

## Lesson 2: "Unterwegs in der Stadt" (Getting Around the City)

**Type:** Vocab teach (transport + urban navigation)
**Mode:** Bubble. No scene. Pure vocabulary.
**Characters:** None (vocabulary instruction)
**id:** deu_r15l2
**board:** true
**Step count:** 24
**New vocabulary (20):** U-Bahn(die), Tram(die), Fahre(die), Eisenbahn(die), Fahrzeug(das), Motorrad(das), Kraftfahrzeug(das), Fahrbahn(die), Einbahnstrasse(die), Einfahrt(die), Ausfahrt(die), Fussganger(der), Fussgangerin(die), Fussgaengerzone(die), Radfahrer(der), Radfahrerin(die), Zone(die), Spur(die), Kurve(die), Lage(die)
**Embedded variants (2):** Velo (Swiss for Fahrrad, noted in Radfahrer card), Trottoir (Swiss for Gehsteig, noted in Fussganger card)
**Grammar:** RECYCLED: Articles + compound noun gender rule (from A1). EXPOSED: Genitive in compound nouns ("Fussganger + Zone")
**Communicative functions:** Describing urban transport, giving/understanding directions, identifying road features
**Cumulative vocab after: 1,823**

### Step sequence:
1. **intro:** "Unterwegs in der Stadt" . Goals: Master urban transport vocabulary. Learn road and traffic terms. Navigate a German city like a local.
2. **teach:** U-Bahn(die) . Fun info: Untergrund (underground) + Bahn (railway). Die U-Bahn. Hamburg's U-Bahn opened in 1912. Berlin, Munich, Hamburg, and Nuremberg all have U-Bahn systems. The yellow U sign is iconic.
3. **teach:** Tram(die) . Fun info: Short for "Trambahn." Die Tram. Hamburg actually removed its trams in 1978, but most German cities kept theirs. Berlin has one of Europe's largest tram networks.
4. **teach:** Fahre(die) . Fun info: "Ferry." From "fahren" (to drive/travel). Die Fahre. Hamburg's harbor ferries are part of the public transport system. You can ride them with a normal HVV ticket.
5. **teach:** Eisenbahn(die) . Fun info: Eisen (iron) + Bahn (track/railway). Die Eisenbahn. Literally "iron track." The first German Eisenbahn ran in 1835 between Nuremberg and Furth, just 6 km.
6. **teach:** Fahrzeug(das) . Fun info: Fahr (drive) + Zeug (stuff/thing). "Driving-thing" = vehicle. Das Fahrzeug. Same -Zeug pattern as Flugzeug (flying-thing = airplane). Gender comes from the last element.
7. **teach:** Motorrad(das) . Fun info: Motor + Rad (wheel). Das Motorrad. Germany is the birthplace of the motorcycle. Gottlieb Daimler built the first one in 1885 in Bad Cannstatt.
8. **teach:** Kraftfahrzeug(das) . Fun info: Kraft (power) + Fahrzeug (vehicle). Das Kraftfahrzeug, abbreviated KFZ. On every German vehicle registration. "KFZ-Versicherung" = car insurance. Bureaucratic precision.
9. **teach:** Fahrbahn(die) . Fun info: Fahr (drive) + Bahn (track). Die Fahrbahn = the actual road surface where vehicles drive. Not the sidewalk, not the bike lane, just the car lane.
10. **teach:** Einbahnstrasse(die) . Fun info: Ein (one) + Bahn (track) + Strasse (street). One-way street. Die Einbahnstrasse. German cities are full of them, especially in old town centers.
11. **teach:** Einfahrt(die) . Fun info: Ein (in) + Fahrt (drive). "Entry/entrance (for vehicles)." Die Einfahrt. On garage doors: "Einfahrt freihalten!" = Keep entrance clear! Germans take this very seriously.
12. **teach:** Ausfahrt(die) . Fun info: Aus (out) + Fahrt (drive). "Exit (for vehicles)." Die Ausfahrt. On the Autobahn, every exit has a number. "Ausfahrt 23: Hamburg-Harburg." Every tourist giggles at how often they see "Ausfahrt."
13. **teach:** Fussganger(der) . Fun info: Fuss (foot) + Ganger (goer). "Pedestrian." Der Fussganger. Germany has strict pedestrian rules. Jaywalking is frowned upon, and you will get scolded for crossing on red. In Switzerland, the sidewalk is called "Trottoir" (from French).
14. **teach:** Fussgangerin(die) . Fun info: Feminine form of Fussganger. Die Fussgangerin. The -in suffix for feminine persons: Lehrer/Lehrerin, Fahrer/Fahrerin, Fussganger/Fussgangerin. Consistent pattern.
15. **teach:** Fussgaengerzone(die) . Fun info: Fussganger + Zone. Pedestrian zone. Die Fussgaengerzone. Every German city center has one: no cars, only people, shops, and cafes. Hamburg's Monckebergstrasse is a famous one.
16. **teach:** Radfahrer(der) . Fun info: Rad (wheel/bicycle) + Fahrer (driver). "Cyclist." Der Radfahrer. Germany has excellent cycling infrastructure. In Switzerland, a bicycle is called "Velo" (from French velocipede).
17. **teach:** Radfahrerin(die) . Fun info: Feminine cyclist. Die Radfahrerin. Same -in pattern. Hamburg is investing heavily in cycling infrastructure with the "Velorouten" network.
18. **teach:** Zone(die) . Fun info: From Greek "zone" (belt/girdle). Die Zone. "Umweltzone" = low-emission zone (most German city centers). "Zeitzone" = time zone. Versatile word.
19. **teach:** Spur(die) . Fun info: "Lane/track/trace." Die Spur. "Die rechte Spur" = the right lane. Also means "trace" or "clue" in detective stories. From the same root as English "spur."
20. **teach:** Kurve(die) . Fun info: "Curve/bend." Die Kurve. "Vorsicht, scharfe Kurve!" = Caution, sharp curve! Related to English "curve" via Latin "curvus." On mountain roads, you see this sign constantly.
21. **teach:** Lage(die) . Fun info: "Location/situation." Die Lage. "Die Lage des Hotels" = the location of the hotel (genitive preview!). "Die politische Lage" = the political situation. Context determines meaning.
22. **mc:** Compound logic . q: "Kraftfahrzeug is made of Kraft + Fahrzeug. What does Kraft mean?" opts: [Power, Fast, Big, New] ans: Power. hint: "KFZ is a power-vehicle. What does that first element contribute?"
23. **fb:** Urban vocab . s: "In der Hamburger Innenstadt gibt es eine grosse {1}." a: ["Fussgaengerzone"] opts: [Fussgaengerzone, Einbahnstrasse, Ausfahrt, Fahrbahn] hint: "A zone where only pedestrians walk, no cars allowed."

25. **mc:** Recycled compound gender . q: "Das Kraft-FAHRZEUG. Why is it 'das'?" opts: ["The last element (Zeug/Fahrzeug) is neuter", "All transport words are neuter", "Kraft is neuter", "Long words are always neuter"] ans: "The last element (Zeug/Fahrzeug) is neuter". hint: "In German compounds, the last element determines the article."

6. **teach:** Gebaeude(das) . Fun info: From "bauen" (to build). Das Gebaeude = building. The Ge- prefix creates a collective noun (like Gepack). The Elbphilharmonie is Hamburg's most famous Gebaeude.
7. **teach:** Turm(der) . Fun info: From Latin "turris" (tower). Der Turm. Hamburg's Michel (St. Michaelis church tower) is the city's landmark. "Fernsehturm" = TV tower. Berlin's is 368 m tall.
8. **teach:** Hof(der) . Fun info: "Courtyard/yard/farm." Der Hof. "Bahnhof" = train yard. "Bauernhof" = farm. "Hof" is one of German's most productive compound elements.
9. **teach:** Vorort(der) . Fun info: Vor (before/in front of) + Ort (place). "Suburb." Der Vorort. Hamburg's Vororte stretch far along the Elbe. German suburbs feel different from American ones: denser, with better public transport.
10. **teach:** Ufer(das) . Fun info: "Bank/shore (of a river)." Das Ufer. "Am Ufer der Elbe" = on the bank of the Elbe. Related to English "over" via Proto-Germanic. Hamburg's Hafencity neighborhood sits on the Ufer.
11. **teach:** Kuste(die) . Fun info: "Coast." Die Kuste. From Latin "costa" (rib/side). "Die deutsche Nordseekuste" = the German North Sea coast. Famous for Wattwanderung (mudflat hiking).
12. **teach:** Ozean(der) . Fun info: From Greek "Okeanos." Der Ozean. Hamburg's connection to the Atlantic made it one of Europe's greatest trading cities. "Ubersee" (overseas) is another Hamburg word.
13. **teach:** Distanz(die) . Fun info: From Latin "distantia." Die Distanz. More formal than "Entfernung." "Die Distanz zwischen Hamburg und Berlin betragt 290 km." Used in professional and scientific contexts.
14. **teach:** Entfernung(die) . Fun info: From "entfernen" (to remove/move away). Die Entfernung = distance. More everyday than Distanz. "Die Entfernung zum Hafen" = the distance to the harbor.
15. **teach:** staedtisch . Fun info: "Urban/municipal." From "Stadt" (city). "Stadtische Verkehrsmittel" = municipal transport. The adjective form of Stadt. Hamburg's stadtische character differs sharply from rural Schleswig-Holstein.
16. **teach:** regional . Fun info: Same as English. "Regionale Unterschiede" = regional differences. Pronounced "ray-gee-oh-NAHL." Germany's regional diversity is one of its defining features.
17. **teach:** Passagier(der) . Fun info: From French "passager." Der Passagier. Used for ship, plane, and train passengers. In Switzerland, the platform is called "Perron" (also from French).
18. **teach:** Passagierin(die) . Fun info: Feminine form. Die Passagierin. The -in suffix applies to all person-nouns: Passagier/Passagierin, Fahrer/Fahrerin. Consistent and productive.
19. **teach:** Steward(der) . Fun info: Borrowed from English. Der Steward. Used for male flight attendants and ship stewards. Pronounced more or less like English but with a German R.
20. **teach:** Stewardess(die) . Fun info: Also from English. Die Stewardess. Increasingly replaced by "Flugbegleiterin" (flight companion, feminine) in modern German, but Stewardess persists in everyday speech.
21. **teach:** Laster(der) . Fun info: Short for "Lastkraftwagen" (LKW). "Truck." Der Laster. In formal German: LKW. In everyday speech: Laster. On the Autobahn, Laster have a speed limit of 80 km/h.
22. **teach:** Transport(der) . Fun info: From Latin "transportare." Der Transport. Hamburg's port handles 130 million tons of cargo annually. "Offentlicher Transport" = public transport (though "Nahverkehr" is more common).
23. **teach:** transportieren . Fun info: The verb form. "Die Schiffe transportieren Waren aus aller Welt." Ships transport goods from around the world. Regular verb with the -ieren ending from French/Latin.
24. **teach:** dicht . Fun info: "Dense/thick/tight." "Dichter Nebel" = thick fog. "Dicht besiedelt" = densely populated. Also slang for "drunk" (informal). Hamburg's fog is beruhmt dicht (famously thick).
25. **teach:** Tourismus(der) . Fun info: From French/English "tourism." Der Tourismus. Hamburg sees 15 million overnight stays per year. The -ismus suffix marks abstract concepts, always masculine.

## Lesson 4: "Der Genitiv und die N-Deklination" (Genitive and N-Declension)

**Type:** Grammar deep dive
**Mode:** Bubble. No scene. Grammar instruction.
**Characters:** None (grammar focus)
**id:** deu_r15l4
**board:** true
**Step count:** 26
**New vocabulary (18):** Gegenstand(der), Geschmack(der), Geschwindigkeit(die), Geschwindigkeitsbeschraenkung(die), Reservierung(die), Ruckfahrt(die), Rundfahrt(die), Umleitung(die), Umtausch(der), Vorfahrt(die), Halt(der), Stau(der), Tankstelle(die), Panne(die), Tempo(das), Salon(der), eilen, stoppen
**Embedded variants (2):** (none standalone)
**Grammar:** TAUGHT: Genitive case #12 (practiced with 4+ quiz steps), N-Deklination #13 (der Junge/den Jungen/dem Jungen/des Jungen). PRACTICED: Genitive articles, genitive -(e)s ending, N-Deklination paradigm
**Communicative functions:** Describing ownership, expressing origin/source, talking about speed and traffic, understanding road signs
**Cumulative vocab after: 1,861**

### Step sequence:
1. **intro:** "Der Genitiv und die N-Deklination" . Goals: Master genitive case formation. Learn N-Deklination nouns (der Herr, der Kollege, der Junge). Understand German traffic and driving vocabulary.
2. **teach:** Gegenstand(der) . Fun info: Gegen (against) + Stand (standing). "Object/item." Der Gegenstand. Something that stands against you, that you encounter. "Personliche Gegenstande" = personal belongings.
3. **teach:** Geschmack(der) . Fun info: From "schmecken" (to taste). Der Geschmack = taste/flavor. "Guter Geschmack" means both good taste in food AND good aesthetic sense. The Ge- prefix creates the noun.
4. **teach:** Geschwindigkeit(die) . Fun info: From "geschwind" (swift). Die Geschwindigkeit = speed. A famously long word at 17 letters. On the Autobahn: "Keine Geschwindigkeitsbegrenzung" = no speed limit.
5. **teach:** Geschwindigkeitsbeschraenkung(die) . Fun info: Geschwindigkeit + Beschrankung (restriction). Speed limit. 30 characters. This is the word that makes German famous for long compounds. Posted as road signs: "50" in a red circle.

8. **teach:** pauschal . Fun info: "Flat-rate/all-inclusive." "Pauschalreise" = package tour. From French "forfait." "Pauschal bezahlen" = to pay a flat rate. Common in German travel and business.
9. **teach:** vorder . Fun info: "Front/forward." "Der vordere Teil des Zuges" = the front part of the train. Comparative of "vorn" (in front). "Vorderseite" = front side.
10. **teach:** abwarts . Fun info: "Downward." Ab (away/down) + -warts (toward). "Es geht abwarts" = things are going downhill (literally and figuratively). The -warts suffix indicates direction.
11. **teach:** aufwarts . Fun info: "Upward." Auf (up) + -warts. "Die Karriere geht aufwarts" = the career is going up. Opposite of abwarts. Same directional suffix pattern.
12. **teach:** aussen . Fun info: "Outside." "Von aussen" = from the outside. "Aussenseiter" = outsider. Opposite of "innen" (inside). Hamburg's Aussenalster (Outer Alster lake) is a famous landmark.
13. **teach:** quer . Fun info: "Across/diagonally." "Quer durch die Stadt" = across the city. "Querstrasse" = cross street. Related to English "queer" (originally meaning "oblique").
14. **teach:** rauf . Fun info: Short for "herauf" (up here). Colloquial: "Komm rauf!" = Come up! The prefix rauf- attaches to verbs: raufgehen, raufklettern. Informal register.
15. **teach:** ruckwarts . Fun info: "Backward." Ruck (back) + -warts. "Ruckwarts einparken" = to reverse park. "Ruckwartsgang" = reverse gear. Germans are expected to master ruckwarts einparken for the driving test.
16. **teach:** voraus . Fun info: "Ahead/in advance." "Im Voraus" = in advance. "Voraus bezahlen" = to pay in advance. "Geradeaus" = straight ahead. Essential direction word.
17. **teach:** entlang . Fun info: "Along." "Die Strasse entlang" = along the street. Unusual grammar: comes AFTER the noun with accusative, or before with dative/genitive. "Am Ufer entlang" = along the bank.
18. **teach:** entfernen . Fun info: "To remove/to be distant." Ent- (away) + fernen (from fern = far). "Bitte entfernen Sie den Gegenstand" = Please remove the object. Also: "weit entfernt" = far away.
19. **teach:** fliessen . Fun info: "To flow." "Die Elbe fliesst durch Hamburg." Irregular: fliesst, floss, ist geflossen. Related to English "flow." Hamburg exists because the Elbe fliesst to the North Sea.
20. **teach:** uberqueren . Fun info: Uber (over) + queren (to cross). "To cross." "Die Strasse uberqueren" = to cross the street. Inseparable: "Er uberquert die Strasse." More formal than "uber die Strasse gehen."
21. **teach:** umdrehen . Fun info: Um (around) + drehen (to turn). "To turn around." "Dreh dich um!" = Turn around! Separable. Also reflexive: "sich umdrehen." Verumius dreht sich um und sieht Lukas.
22. **teach:** umtauschen . Fun info: Um (around/exchange) + tauschen (to swap). "To exchange (goods/money)." "Euro in Dollar umtauschen" = to exchange euros for dollars. "Umtausch nur innerhalb von 14 Tagen" = exchange only within 14 days.

29. **teach:** parkieren . Fun info: "To park" (Swiss). In Germany and Austria: "parken." In Switzerland: "parkieren." The -ieren ending follows the French/Latin loan pattern common in Swiss German.
