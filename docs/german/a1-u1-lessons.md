## Lesson 1: "Ankunft am Flughafen" (Arrival at the Airport)

**Type:** Story intro
**Mode:** Cinematic — BER arrivals hall, golden hour light streaming through glass
**Characters:** Verumius (protagonist), Airport announcer (voice only), unnamed Ticket Agent
**Step count:** 20
**New vocabulary (15):** hallo, willkommen, ich, sein, heißen, gut, ja, nein, hier, Deutschland(das), Flughafen(der), Gepäck(das), Ticket(das), Reise(die), danke
**Grammar:** EXPOSED: SVO word order (#16), Präsens irregular sein (#2), personal pronouns nom ich (#11)
**Communicative functions:** #1 introduce self (name — exposure), #3 greet (exposure), #20 react/respond (exposure)
**Cumulative vocab after: 15**

### Step sequence:
1. **intro:** "Willkommen in Deutschland" — Goals: Learn your first German words. Meet Verumius at the airport. Hear greetings and basic responses.
2. **teach:** hallo — Fun info: Used across ALL German-speaking countries. Borrowed into dozens of languages. One of the few German words everyone already knows.
3. **teach:** willkommen — Fun info: Literally "well-come." Same root as English "welcome." Germans put it on doormats, airport signs, and beer garden entrances.
4. **teach:** ich — Fun info: Always lowercase unless it starts a sentence. Unlike English "I," German "ich" is humble. The sound /ç/ does not exist in English — it is like a cat hissing softly.
5. **teach:** sein — Fun info: The most irregular verb in almost every European language. German "sein," English "be," Spanish "ser" — all from the same Proto-Indo-European root *h₁es-.
6. **teach:** heißen — Fun info: Literally means "to be called." Germans never say "my name is" the English way. They say "Ich heiße..." (I am called...). The ß is a uniquely German letter.
7. **teach:** gut — Fun info: Identical to English "good" — both come from Proto-Germanic *gōdaz. Germans say "Alles gut?" the way English speakers say "All good?"
8. **teach:** ja — Fun info: The most recognized German word worldwide after "kindergarten." In casual speech, Germans stretch it: "Jaaa..." means "well, let me think..."
9. **teach:** nein — Fun info: Related to English "no" and Latin "non." Germans sometimes soften it to "nee" in casual speech, especially in northern Germany.
10. **teach:** hier — Fun info: Sounds almost like English "here" and means the same thing. One of those words that prove English and German are siblings.
11. **teach:** Deutschland(das) — Fun info: Germans call their country "Deutschland" (land of the people). The French call it "Allemagne," Finns say "Saksa," and Poles say "Niemcy." No country has more different names in other languages.
12. **teach:** Flughafen(der) — Fun info: Literally "fly-yard" (Flug + Hafen). German loves building long compound words. This is your first taste of that superpower.
13. **teach:** Gepäck(das) — Fun info: From "packen" (to pack). The Ge- prefix turns it into a collective noun: all your packed stuff together. Das Gepäck — neuter, like most Ge- collective nouns.
14. **teach:** Ticket(das) — Fun info: Borrowed directly from English. Germans use both "Ticket" and "Fahrkarte." Using "Ticket" marks you as modern and casual.
15. **teach:** Reise(die) — Fun info: From Old High German "rīsa" (rising, departure). A "Reise" is a journey with intention — not just going somewhere, but going somewhere meaningful.
16. **teach:** danke — Fun info: From "denken" (to think). Thanking someone literally means "I think of you." Same origin as English "thank" from "think."
17. **tip:** "Your first German sounds" — German has three extra letters: ä, ö, ü (vowels with Umlaut dots) and ß (sharp S). You will meet them all. For now: "ei" says "eye," "ie" says "ee," and "ch" is a soft hiss.
18. **mc:** Identify the correct greeting — q: "Verumius walks into the airport. What does the sign say?" opts: [Willkommen, Tschüss, Danke, Nein] ans: Willkommen. hint: "This word is on welcome mats everywhere."
19. **mc:** Basic meaning — q: "Ich heiße Verumius. What did Verumius just say?" opts: [His name, His age, His job, His country] ans: His name. hint: "Heiße relates to being called something."
20. **fb:** Complete the sentence — s: "Verumius sagt: {1}, ich bin hier!" a: ["Hallo"] opts: [Hallo, Nein, Danke, Gut] hint: "How do you greet someone when you arrive?"

## Lesson 2: "Die ersten Wörter" (The First Words)

**Type:** Vocab teach
**Mode:** Bubble — flashcard style, no scene
**Characters:** None (pure vocabulary)
**Step count:** 25
**New vocabulary (25):** der, die, das, ein, eine, bitte, Frau(die), Herr(der), Name(der), Vorname(der), Familienname(der), was, wer, wie, wo, nicht, und, mein, meine, Sprache(die), deutsch, sprechen, kommen, aus, wohnen
**Grammar:** EXPOSED: articles (der/die/das/ein/eine), W-question word order (#17), possessives mein/meine, SVO (#16)
**Communicative functions:** #1 introduce self (name, nationality — taught), #4 ask/answer personal questions (taught)
**Cumulative vocab after: 40**

### Step sequence:
1. **intro:** "Die ersten Wörter" — Goals: Learn German articles (der, die, das). Master question words. Introduce yourself in German.
2. **teach:** der — Fun info: The masculine article. About 40% of German nouns are masculine. If a word feels strong, active, or weather-related, guess "der" — you will be right surprisingly often.
3. **teach:** die — Fun info: Does triple duty: feminine singular ("die Frau"), AND all plurals ("die Männer"), AND the formal "they." The most overworked word in German.
4. **teach:** das — Fun info: The neuter article. Most words starting with Ge- are das. Most diminutives (-chen, -lein) are das. "Das Mädchen" (the girl) is grammatically neuter because of -chen. German grammar does not care about biology.
5. **teach:** ein — Fun info: "A/an" for masculine and neuter. English lost its gender system 800 years ago but kept "a/an." German kept all three genders and the split articles to match.
6. **teach:** eine — Fun info: "A/an" for feminine. "Eine Frau" (a woman), "eine Reise" (a journey). The -e ending is your clue: feminine articles love that -e.
7. **teach:** bitte — Fun info: Means "please" AND "you are welcome" AND "here you go" AND "excuse me?" The Swiss Army knife of German politeness. Context is everything.
8. **teach:** Frau(die) — Fun info: Used as "Mrs./Ms." AND "woman." No separate "Miss" anymore. Even the Chancellor is "Frau Bundeskanzlerin." Frau = respect, regardless of marital status.
9. **teach:** Herr(der) — Fun info: Literally means "lord" or "master." Germans address every adult male as "Herr" + last name. Much more formal than English "Mr."
10. **teach:** Name(der) — Fun info: Identical to English. Both from Latin "nomen." German even has the saying "Nomen est omen" (the name is a sign) — borrowed straight from Latin.
11. **teach:** Vorname(der) — Fun info: "Vor" means "before/front." Your Vorname is the name that comes FIRST. German compound logic: front-name = first name.
12. **teach:** Familienname(der) — Fun info: Family-name. Transparent compound. German last names often reveal ancestors' jobs: Müller (miller), Schmidt (smith), Fischer (fisher).
13. **teach:** was — Fun info: Sounds like English "was" but means "what." This is a false friend. "Was ist das?" is one of the first German phrases most people learn.
14. **teach:** wer — Fun info: Means "who." Looks like English "were" but sounds like "vair." German W is always pronounced as English V.
15. **teach:** wie — Fun info: Means "how." "Wie heißen Sie?" (How are you called?) is the formal way to ask someone's name. Also used for "Wie geht's?" (How's it going?).
16. **teach:** wo — Fun info: Means "where." Only two letters, easy to remember. "Wo ist...?" will be one of your most useful phrases in Germany.
17. **teach:** nicht — Fun info: The German "not." Goes at the end of the clause or before the thing it negates. Word order matters more in German than in English.
18. **teach:** und — Fun info: "And." Same word in dozens of Germanic languages. One of the most frequently used words in any language. Pronounced "oont."
19. **teach:** mein — Fun info: "My" for masculine/neuter nouns. Sounds like English "mine." Same Proto-Germanic root. "Mein Name ist..." is a perfectly valid way to introduce yourself.
20. **teach:** meine — Fun info: "My" for feminine nouns and plurals. The -e ending mirrors "eine." Pattern: feminine things get the -e ending on articles AND possessives.
21. **teach:** Sprache(die) — Fun info: From "sprechen" (to speak). The -e ending is a strong feminine marker. "Muttersprache" (mother tongue) = Mutter + Sprache. Compound magic again.
22. **teach:** deutsch — Fun info: When lowercase, it is an adjective/adverb meaning "German." "Ich spreche deutsch" (I speak German). Capitalized "Deutsch" is the noun (the German language).
23. **teach:** sprechen — Fun info: Cognate with English "speak." One of German's irregular verbs: ich spreche, du sprichst (the e changes to i for du/er/sie). You will see this pattern with many verbs.
24. **teach:** kommen — Fun info: "To come." "Ich komme aus..." (I come from...) is the standard way to state your origin. Regular verb — no tricks.
25. **teach:** aus — Fun info: "From/out of." "Aus Deutschland" = from Germany. Also a prefix that means "off/out": "ausgehen" = go out. One little word, enormous reach.
26. **mc:** Article gender — q: "Which article goes with Name?" opts: [der Name, die Name, das Name, ein Name] ans: der Name. hint: "Name is masculine. Think of the blue article."
27. **mc:** Question word — q: "You want to ask someone's name. Which question word do you need?" opts: [wie, was, wo, wer] ans: wie. hint: "The German way asks HOW you are called."
28. **fb:** Self-introduction — s: "Ich {1} aus Deutschland." a: ["komme"] opts: [komme, spreche, heiße, wohne] hint: "Which verb means to come from a place?"
29. **match:** Pair question words with meanings — pairs: [{nl:"was",en:"what"},{nl:"wer",en:"who"},{nl:"wie",en:"how"},{nl:"wo",en:"where"}]
30. **mc:** Possessive — q: "How do you say 'my name' in German?" opts: [mein Name, meine Name, der Name, ein Name] ans: mein Name. hint: "Name is masculine. Masculine 'my' has no -e ending."

## Lesson 3: "Der falsche Zug" (The Wrong Train)

**Type:** Story development
**Mode:** Bubble — ICE train interior, countryside blurring past the window
**Characters:** Verumius, Dr. Elif Yilmaz (calm, multilingual linguist, 40s, warm authority)
**Step count:** 22
**New vocabulary (18):** herzlich, Entschuldigung(die), Sie (formal pronoun), er, sie (she), es, wir, dein, deine, haben, fragen, antworten, verstehen, sagen, Fahrkarte(die), Koffer(der), Abfahrt(die), Zoll(der)
**Grammar:** TAUGHT: Formal/informal Sie/du (#23), personal pronouns nom er/sie/es/wir (#11). EXPOSED: Präsens irregular haben (#2), V2 rule (#19)
**Communicative functions:** #3 greet and say goodbye — formal (taught), #4 ask/answer personal questions — formal (practiced), #20 react/respond (practiced)
**Cumulative vocab after: 58**

### Step sequence:
1. **intro:** "Der falsche Zug" — Goals: Learn formal vs. informal address. Meet Dr. Yilmaz on the wrong train. Practice greetings and personal questions with a stranger.
2. **teach:** herzlich — Fun info: "Heartfelt/warm." From Herz (heart). "Herzlich willkommen" is the warmest welcome in German — literally "heartfully welcome." Used at weddings, airports, and doorsteps.
3. **teach:** Entschuldigung(die) — Fun info: 16 letters. One of the longest everyday words in German. Literally "un-guilt-ing" (Ent-schuld-igung). Germans love building meaning from parts. This is how you say "excuse me" AND "I am sorry."
4. **teach:** Sie (formal pronoun) — Fun info: Always capitalized when it means "you (formal)." Lowercase "sie" = she or they. The capital letter is the ONLY difference in writing. In speech, context tells you which one. Using Sie with strangers is mandatory until they offer "du."
5. **teach:** er — Fun info: "He." Same as in many Germanic languages. Always lowercase unless starting a sentence. Replaces any masculine noun: der Koffer becomes er.
6. **teach:** sie (she) — Fun info: "She." Lowercase. Replaces any feminine noun: die Fahrkarte becomes sie. Yes, "sie" means both "she" and "they" — context and verb form tell you which.
7. **teach:** es — Fun info: "It." Replaces neuter nouns: das Ticket becomes es. Also used in weather expressions: "Es regnet" (it rains). The word that makes German grammar logical — everything has a pronoun that matches its article.
8. **teach:** wir — Fun info: "We." Sounds like English "veer." The wir-form of verbs always ends in -en, identical to the infinitive. Ich lerne, but wir lernen. This makes wir the easiest pronoun to conjugate for.
9. **teach:** dein — Fun info: "Your" (informal) for masculine/neuter. Mirrors "mein" exactly. Mein Name, dein Name. If someone uses "dein" with you, they consider you a friend or peer.
10. **teach:** deine — Fun info: "Your" (informal) for feminine/plural. Mirrors "meine." Meine Sprache, deine Sprache. The -e pattern for feminine forms is becoming your friend.
11. **teach:** haben — Fun info: "To have." Irregular: ich habe, du hast, er hat. English "have" and German "haben" are the same word separated by 2,000 years. Also used as a helper verb for past tense (later).
12. **teach:** fragen — Fun info: "To ask." Regular verb. "Darf ich fragen?" (May I ask?) is the polite way to begin a question. Related to English "fraught" (originally meant "to carry/seek").
13. **teach:** antworten — Fun info: "To answer." Regular verb. The Ant- prefix means "against/in response to." An Antwort (answer) is literally a "counter-word."
14. **teach:** verstehen — Fun info: "To understand." Ver- + stehen (stand). Literally "to stand before" something mentally. "Ich verstehe" = "I understand." One of the most useful sentences you will ever learn.
15. **teach:** sagen — Fun info: "To say." Regular and reliable. "Sag mal..." (Say/tell me...) is how Germans begin casual requests. Related to English "say" and "saga."
16. **teach:** Fahrkarte(die) — Fun info: "Drive-card" (Fahr + Karte). Your train/bus ticket. Die Fahrkarte — feminine. Germans still say Fahrkarte more than Ticket for public transport. Sounds more official.
17. **teach:** Koffer(der) — Fun info: From Arabic "kufr" (chest/box), which entered German via Italian "coffero." Your suitcase carries a thousand years of trade history in its name.
18. **teach:** Abfahrt(die) — Fun info: "Ab" (away) + "Fahrt" (drive/journey). Departure. You will see this on every train station departure board in Germany. Die Abfahrt — feminine, like most -t ending abstract nouns.
19. **teach:** Zoll(der) — Fun info: Customs. From Latin "teloneum" (toll station). At airports, the green channel means "nichts zu verzollen" (nothing to declare). Also historically means "inch" — a completely different meaning from the same word.
20. **tip:** "Sie vs. du — The invisible wall" — Germany runs on formal address. Use Sie + last name with: strangers, anyone older, officials, service workers. Use du + first name with: friends, family, children, fellow students. Getting it wrong is not a crime, but switching from Sie to du is a social milestone. Dr. Yilmaz uses Sie with Verumius because they just met.
21. **mc:** Formal address — q: "Dr. Yilmaz asks Verumius his name. Which sentence would she use?" opts: [Wie heißen Sie?, Wie heißt du?, Was ist dein Name?, Wer bist du?] ans: Wie heißen Sie?. hint: "Dr. Yilmaz just met Verumius. She is polite and formal."
22. **fb:** Pronoun selection — s: "Dr. Yilmaz ist eine Frau. {1} kommt aus der Türkei." a: ["Sie"] opts: [Sie, Er, Es, Wir] hint: "Which pronoun replaces a feminine noun referring to a woman?"

## Lesson 4: "Wie funktioniert Deutsch?" (How Does German Work?)

**Type:** Grammar
**Mode:** Bubble — learning mode, no scene
**Characters:** None (grammar instruction)
**Step count:** 24
**New vocabulary (12):** lernen, machen, wissen, finden, gehen, auch, oder, aber, in, mit, von, für
**Grammar:** TAUGHT: Präsens regular (#1), SVO word order (#16), W-question word order (#17), Yes/No question order (#18), V2 rule (#19). PRACTICED: all five with 3+ quiz steps each
**Communicative functions:** #1 introduce self (practiced through conjugation), #4 ask/answer personal questions (practiced)
**Cumulative vocab after: 70**

### Step sequence:
1. **intro:** "Wie funktioniert Deutsch?" — Goals: Master regular verb conjugation. Understand German word order rules. Build your first real German sentences.
2. **teach:** lernen — Fun info: "To learn." Regular. Ich lerne, du lernst, er lernt. Cognate with English "learn." In German, you can "learn" a profession: "Bäcker lernen" means to train as a baker.
3. **teach:** machen — Fun info: "To make/do." The most versatile verb in German. "Was machst du?" means both "What are you making?" and "What are you doing?" Regular and reliable.
4. **teach:** wissen — Fun info: "To know (a fact)." Irregular: ich weiß, du weißt. Different from "kennen" (to know a person). English lost this distinction, but German keeps it sharp.
5. **teach:** finden — Fun info: "To find." Also means "to think/consider": "Ich finde das gut" = "I think that's good." Regular conjugation but irregular past tense (later). Same root as English "find."
6. **teach:** gehen — Fun info: "To go (on foot)." Irregular in past tense but regular in present: ich gehe, du gehst. "Wie geht's?" (How's it going?) is Germany's "How are you?"
7. **teach:** auch — Fun info: "Also/too." Placed right after what it modifies. "Ich auch" = "Me too." One of the ten most frequent words in German. Sounds like "owk."
8. **teach:** oder — Fun info: "Or." Sounds like English "odder" without the second d. "Kaffee oder Tee?" (Coffee or tea?) — a question you will hear daily in Germany.
9. **teach:** aber — Fun info: "But." The gentle contrast word. "Gut, aber nicht perfekt." German also has "sondern" (but rather) for stronger corrections — that comes later.
10. **teach:** in — Fun info: Same spelling and meaning as English "in." But German "in" changes based on movement: "in der Stadt" (in the city, stationary) vs. "in die Stadt" (into the city, moving). For now, just use it for location.
11. **teach:** mit — Fun info: "With." Always takes the dative case (you will learn cases later). "Mit Freunden" = "with friends." Also a prefix: "mitkommen" = to come along.
12. **teach:** von — Fun info: "From/of." "Ich bin von hier" = "I am from here." Also used to show possession: "das Buch von Maria" = "Maria's book." Casual alternative to the genitive case.
13. **teach:** für — Fun info: "For." "Für mich" = "for me." The ü sound does not exist in English. Round your lips like saying "oo" but try to say "ee." That is ü.
14. **tip:** "Regular verb conjugation — The -en machine" — German regular verbs follow ONE pattern. Take the infinitive (lernen), remove -en (lern-), add endings: ich lerne, du lernst, er/sie/es lernt, wir lernen, sie/Sie lernen. This covers 80%+ of German verbs. Memorize these five endings and you can conjugate thousands of verbs.
15. **verb_table:** Regular conjugation — title: "Präsens: machen (to do/make)", groups: [{label:"Singular", rows:[{pronoun:"ich",form:"mache",en:"I do"},{pronoun:"du",form:"machst",en:"you do (informal)"},{pronoun:"er/sie/es",form:"macht",en:"he/she/it does"}]},{label:"Plural",rows:[{pronoun:"wir",form:"machen",en:"we do"},{pronoun:"sie/Sie",form:"machen",en:"they/you(formal) do"}]}]
16. **tip:** "Word order — The verb is ALWAYS second" — In a German statement, the conjugated verb is ALWAYS in position 2. "Ich lerne Deutsch" (I learn German). "Heute lerne ich Deutsch" (Today learn I German). Even when something else goes first, the verb stays second. This is the V2 rule. Questions flip: "Lernst du Deutsch?" (Learn you German?) or "Was lernst du?" (What learn you?).
17. **mc:** Conjugation — q: "How do you say 'she learns' in German?" opts: [sie lernt, sie lerne, sie lernen, sie lernst] ans: sie lernt. hint: "Er/sie/es always gets the -t ending."
18. **mc:** Conjugation — q: "Complete: 'Wir _____ Deutsch.'" opts: [lernen, lernt, lerne, lernst] ans: lernen. hint: "The wir-form always matches the infinitive ending."
19. **fb:** V2 word order — s: "Heute {1} ich Deutsch." a: ["lerne"] opts: [lerne, lernen, ich, Deutsch] hint: "The verb must be in position 2 — right after 'Heute.'"
20. **mc:** W-question word order — q: "Which sentence asks 'Where do you live?' correctly?" opts: [Wo wohnst du?, Du wohnst wo?, Wohnst wo du?, Wo du wohnst?] ans: Wo wohnst du?. hint: "W-questions: question word first, then verb, then subject."
21. **mc:** Yes/No question — q: "How do you ask 'Do you speak German?' (informal)?" opts: [Sprichst du deutsch?, Du sprichst deutsch?, Deutsch du sprichst?, Sprichst deutsch du?] ans: Sprichst du deutsch?. hint: "Yes/No questions start with the verb."
22. **fb:** SVO statement — s: "{1} komme aus Deutschland." a: ["Ich"] opts: [Ich, Mein, Du, Gut] hint: "Who is speaking about themselves? The first-person pronoun."
23. **mc:** Conjunction use — q: "Ich lerne Deutsch ___ Englisch." opts: [und, aber, oder, in] ans: und. hint: "Verumius is learning both languages. Which word connects two equal things?"
24. **drag_fill:** Multi-construct — s: "{1} {2} du aus?" blanks: {1:"Woher",2:"kommst"} pool: ["Woher","kommst","kommen","Wo","gehst"] hint: "A W-question about origin. Question word + verb + subject."

## Lesson 5: "Papiere und Zahlen" (Papers and Numbers)

**Type:** Vocab + review
**Mode:** Bubble — passport control / form-filling context
**Characters:** Verumius (filling out forms), border officer (voice only)
**Step count:** 26
**New vocabulary (25):** Adresse(die), Alter(das), Anrede(die), Ausweis(der), Beruf(der), Familienstand(der), Formular(das), Geburtsjahr(das), Geburtsort(der), Geburtstag(der), Pass(der), Namen(der), eins, zwei, drei, vier, fünf, sechs, sieben, acht, neun, zehn, elf, zwölf, sehr
**Grammar:** TAUGHT: Numbers 0-20 first half (#25). PRACTICED: Präsens regular (#1) + W-questions (#17) recycled in quiz steps
**Communicative functions:** #1 introduce self — age, profession (taught), #4 ask/answer personal questions — form-filling (practiced), #11 tell time/dates — exposure through numbers
**Cumulative vocab after: 95**

### Step sequence:
1. **intro:** "Papiere und Zahlen" — Goals: Learn identity document vocabulary. Count from 1-12 in German. Fill out your first German form.
2. **teach:** Adresse(die) — Fun info: Nearly identical to English "address" and French "adresse." Latin "directiare" (to direct) traveled through French into both languages. Die Adresse — feminine.
3. **teach:** Alter(das) — Fun info: "Age." Related to English "old/elder." "Wie alt bist du?" (How old are you?) literally asks "How old are you?" — same logic as English.
4. **teach:** Anrede(die) — Fun info: "Form of address" (Herr/Frau). An- (to) + Rede (speech). How you address someone. On German forms, this field determines whether they write "Sehr geehrter Herr..." or "Sehr geehrte Frau..." in letters to you.
5. **teach:** Ausweis(der) — Fun info: "ID card." Aus- (out) + -weis (show/prove). Something that shows who you are. Every German over 16 must carry a Personalausweis (personal ID card) by law.
6. **teach:** Beruf(der) — Fun info: "Profession." From "berufen" (to be called/appointed). Your Beruf is your calling. Germany takes professional identity very seriously — it is often the second question after your name.
7. **teach:** Familienstand(der) — Fun info: "Marital status." Familie + Stand (status/standing). On forms: ledig (single), verheiratet (married), geschieden (divorced), verwitwet (widowed). Germans put this on official forms everywhere.
8. **teach:** Formular(das) — Fun info: From Latin "formula." Das Formular — neuter. Germany is famous for forms. The joke: "Germans have a form for filling out forms." There is some truth to it.
9. **teach:** Geburtsjahr(das) — Fun info: Geburt (birth) + Jahr (year). Your birth year. German compound nouns read right-to-left for meaning: it is a Jahr (year) of Geburt (birth). Das — neuter, like most compounds ending in -jahr.
10. **teach:** Geburtsort(der) — Fun info: Geburt (birth) + Ort (place). Your birthplace. Der Ort — masculine, so der Geburtsort. The compound inherits the gender of its LAST element. This is a golden rule.
11. **teach:** Geburtstag(der) — Fun info: Geburt (birth) + Tag (day). Birthday. "Alles Gute zum Geburtstag!" is "Happy Birthday!" Germans celebrate the evening BEFORE — the "Vorabend."
12. **teach:** Pass(der) — Fun info: Same as English "passport" (shortened). Der Pass — masculine. A German passport is one of the most powerful in the world — visa-free access to 190+ countries.
13. **teach:** Namen(der) — Fun info: The dative/accusative form of "Name." You will see "Namen" on forms asking you to write your name. Same word, just a case-ending. German nouns sometimes change form — this is your first preview.
14. **teach:** eins — Fun info: "One." The loneliest number in any language. When counting, Germans say "eins." Before a noun, it becomes "ein/eine" (which you already know as the indefinite article!).
15. **teach:** zwei — Fun info: "Two." Sounds like "tsvy." Related to English "two" and "twin." In phone calls, Germans say "zwo" instead of "zwei" to avoid confusion with "drei."
16. **teach:** drei — Fun info: "Three." Sounds like "dry." Same root as English "three," Latin "tres," Russian "tri." One of the most stable words across Indo-European languages.
17. **teach:** vier — Fun info: "Four." Sounds like "fear." Related to English "four." The V is pronounced as F. This catches many English speakers off guard.
18. **teach:** fünf — Fun info: "Five." That ü again. "Fünf" and "five" are the same Proto-Germanic word. Germans use a 24-hour clock, so "fünf Uhr" means 5:00 AM, "siebzehn Uhr" means 5:00 PM.

2. **teach:** Ausland(das) — Fun info: "Foreign land." Aus (out) + Land (land). Das Ausland — everywhere that is not your home country. "Im Ausland leben" (to live abroad) is one of the most common phrases in German expat life.
3. **teach:** Ausländer(der) — Fun info: "Foreigner." Ausland + -er (person from). Der Ausländer — masculine. The word is neutral in meaning but politically loaded. Many Germans prefer "Menschen mit Migrationshintergrund" (people with migration background) in formal contexts.
4. **teach:** ausländisch — Fun info: "Foreign" (adjective). Ausland + -isch. The -isch suffix turns nouns into adjectives: Ausland → ausländisch, Europa → europäisch. You will see this pattern everywhere.
5. **teach:** Deutsche(die) — Fun info: "German woman / German person." As a noun: "die Deutsche" (the German woman), "der Deutsche" (the German man). Unusual: it declines like an adjective, not a regular noun. A quirk of German grammar.
6. **teach:** Europa(das) — Fun info: From Greek mythology: Europa was a Phoenician princess kidnapped by Zeus (as a bull). The continent named after a love story. Das Europa — neuter. Germany is the largest economy in Europe.
7. **teach:** Europäer(der) — Fun info: "European (person)." Europa + -er. Der Europäer. The ä in the middle comes from the Umlaut shift that happens when you add -er to words with a. Same pattern: Amerika → Amerikaner.
8. **teach:** europäisch — Fun info: "European" (adjective). "Die Europäische Union" (EU) is a phrase every German knows. Adjective endings change based on case and gender — for now, just know the base form.
9. **teach:** Heimat(die) — Fun info: "Homeland/home." One of the most emotionally loaded words in German. Not just where you live but where you BELONG. Die Heimat — feminine. There is no exact English equivalent. It carries nostalgia, identity, and belonging.
10. **teach:** international — Fun info: Same spelling in German and English. Borrowed from French. Pronounced with German sounds: "in-ter-nah-tsio-NAHL." The stress falls on the last syllable, unlike English.
11. **teach:** Land(das) — Fun info: "Country/land." Das Land — neuter. Plural: Länder (with Umlaut). "Bundesland" = federal state. Germany has 16 Bundesländer. Same root as English "land."
12. **teach:** Welt(die) — Fun info: "World." From Old High German "weralt" (age of man). Die Welt — feminine. Also Germany's second-largest newspaper. "Weltmeister" (world champion) — Germans love this compound, especially during football season.
13. **teach:** freuen — Fun info: "To be happy/to look forward to." "Ich freue mich" = "I am happy/pleased." Always used with "sich" (reflexive). "Freut mich!" is short for "It pleases me" — the standard response when meeting someone.
14. **teach:** danken — Fun info: "To thank." The verb behind "danke." "Ich danke Ihnen" (I thank you, formal) is more elegant than just "danke." Takes dative: you thank TO someone.
15. **teach:** entschuldigen — Fun info: "To excuse." "Entschuldigen Sie, bitte" = "Excuse me, please." Ent- (un-) + Schuld (guilt) + -igen (verb ending). You are literally "un-guilting" someone. The long word that every tourist needs on day one.
16. **teach:** brauchen — Fun info: "To need." Regular verb. "Ich brauche Hilfe" (I need help). In casual speech, Germans use "brauchen" instead of "müssen" (must): "Du brauchst nicht kommen" = You do not need to come.
17. **teach:** buchstabieren — Fun info: "To spell." Buchstabe (letter, literally "book-staff" from rune staves) + -ieren (verb ending from French). "Können Sie das buchstabieren?" (Can you spell that?) — essential when giving your foreign name.
18. **teach:** kennen — Fun info: "To know (a person/place)." Different from "wissen" (to know a fact). "Ich kenne Berlin" = I know/am familiar with Berlin. "Ich weiß, dass Berlin groß ist" = I know that Berlin is big. Two kinds of knowing.
19. **teach:** gültig — Fun info: "Valid." "Ist Ihr Pass gültig?" (Is your passport valid?). From "gelten" (to be worth). A gültig ticket works. An ungültig (invalid) one does not. You hear this at every ticket gate.
20. **teach:** Flugzeug(das) — Fun info: "Airplane." Flug (flight) + Zeug (stuff/thing). Literally "flight-thing." Das Flugzeug — neuter, like most Zeug-compounds. "Spielzeug" (play-thing = toy), "Werkzeug" (work-thing = tool). German compound logic at its finest.
21. **teach:** bedeuten — Fun info: "To mean." "Was bedeutet das?" (What does that mean?) is the single most useful sentence for a language learner. Be- + deuten (to interpret). You will say this hundreds of times.
22. **tip:** "Germany: more than a country" — Germany is 16 federal states (Bundesländer), each with its own culture, dialect, and identity. A Bavarian and a Hamburger are as different as a Texan and a New Yorker. The food, the humor, the accent — everything changes. Berlin alone has 3.7 million people from 190 countries. Verumius has arrived in one of the most diverse cities in Europe.
