## Lesson 1: "Der erste Morgen" (The First Morning)

**Type:** Story intro
**Mode:** Cinematic -- modern Berlin office building in Mitte, morning light, glass and steel
**Characters:** Verumius (nervous, overdressed), Mia (welcoming, amused)
**Step count:** 22
**New vocabulary (20):** Abteilung(die), Betrieb(der), Besprechung(die), Etage(die), Flur(der), Gang(der), Garderobe(die), Kantine(die), Konferenz(die), Tagesablauf(der), Zusammenarbeit(die), begrüßen, einrichten, ordnen, aufmerksam, gemeinsam, ordentlich, fest, innen, miteinander
**Grammar:** EXPOSED: Passive Prasens #7 (Mia: "Hier wird jeden Morgen Kaffee gemacht"), Relative clauses #10 (narrator: "Der Kollege, der am Empfang sitzt...")
**Communicative functions:** Greet colleagues (exposure), describe workplace (exposure), navigate office building (exposure)
**Cumulative vocab after: 20**

### Step sequence:
1. **intro:** "Der erste Morgen" -- Goals: Arrive at your first German workplace. Learn office vocabulary. Meet your new colleagues with Mia as your guide.
2. **story:** speaker: narrator, mood: excited, art: de-ber-office-exterior -- trg: "Es ist Montagmorgen in Berlin-Mitte. Verumius steht vor einem modernen Burogebaude aus Glas und Stahl. Er tragt einen Anzug. Alle anderen tragen Jeans.", src: "It is Monday morning in Berlin-Mitte. Verumius stands in front of a modern office building of glass and steel. He is wearing a suit. Everyone else is wearing jeans."
3. **story:** speaker: mia, mood: amused, art: de-ber-office-entrance -- trg: "Willkommen im Betrieb! Du bist etwas... overdressed. Aber keine Sorge, das passiert jedem am ersten Tag.", src: "Welcome to the company! You are a bit... overdressed. But no worries, that happens to everyone on the first day."
4. **teach:** Betrieb(der) -- Fun info: From "betreiben" (to operate). A Betrieb is any operating business or company. "Im Betrieb" means at work. Germans distinguish between Firma (legal company), Betrieb (active operation), and Unternehmen (enterprise).
5. **teach:** Abteilung(die) -- Fun info: Ab- (off) + teilen (to divide). A department is literally a "division." Every German company is organized into Abteilungen. Your Abteilung defines your identity at work.
6. **teach:** Etage(die) -- Fun info: Borrowed from French. Germans say "erste Etage" for the first floor ABOVE ground. Ground floor is "Erdgeschoss." So "dritte Etage" = British 3rd floor = American 4th floor. Confusion guaranteed for visitors.
7. **teach:** Flur(der) -- Fun info: The hallway or corridor. Der Flur is where you overhear gossip, bump into the boss, and learn who makes the best coffee. Also means "open field" in old German, but in offices it means the hallway.
8. **teach:** Gang(der) -- Fun info: Both a corridor AND a course of a meal. "Der Gang zum Kopierer" (the walk to the copier) uses the same word as "der erste Gang" (the first course). Context is king.
9. **teach:** Kantine(die) -- Fun info: The company cafeteria. From Latin "cantina." German office canteens are legendary: subsidized hot meals, salad bars, and the social epicenter of every workplace. Currywurst on Fridays is a tradition.
10. **teach:** Garderobe(die) -- Fun info: The cloakroom or wardrobe. From French "garder" (to keep) + "robe" (clothing). In German offices, everyone hangs their coat in the Garderobe before starting work.
11. **teach:** Besprechung(die) -- Fun info: Be- + sprechen (to speak). A meeting. Germans take Besprechungen seriously: there is an agenda, a start time, and (usually) an end time. "Wir haben um 10 eine Besprechung" is a sentence you will hear daily.
12. **teach:** Konferenz(die) -- Fun info: From Latin "conferre" (to bring together). A Konferenz is more formal than a Besprechung. Conference rooms are Konferenzraume. Every floor has at least one.
13. **teach:** Tagesablauf(der) -- Fun info: Tag (day) + Ablauf (sequence/flow). Your daily routine. On your first day, someone always explains the Tagesablauf. Germans love structure: 8:00 start, 12:00 lunch, 17:00 Feierabend.
14. **teach:** Zusammenarbeit(die) -- Fun info: Zusammen (together) + Arbeit (work). Collaboration. Germans value Zusammenarbeit so much they put it in job descriptions, performance reviews, and company values. "Gute Zusammenarbeit" is the highest workplace praise.
15. **teach:** begrüßen -- Fun info: Be- + grüßen (to greet). To welcome or greet formally. "Ich begrüße Sie herzlich" is how department heads start meetings. The be- prefix makes it more thorough than a casual "grüßen."
16. **teach:** einrichten -- Fun info: Ein- (in) + richten (to arrange). To set up or furnish. "Seinen Arbeitsplatz einrichten" means setting up your workspace. Also means to furnish an apartment. Germans take both very seriously.
17. **teach:** ordnen -- Fun info: To organize or sort. "Dateien ordnen" (organize files). Related to "Ordnung" (order). The saying "Ordnung muss sein" (there must be order) is practically the German national motto.
18. **teach:** gemeinsam -- Fun info: Together, jointly. "Gemeinsam sind wir stark" (together we are strong). Stronger than "zusammen" because it implies unity of purpose, not just physical togetherness.
19. **teach:** aufmerksam -- Fun info: Auf- (up) + merksam (noticing). Attentive, observant. "Aufmerksam zuhoren" (to listen attentively) is what your new boss hopes you will do on day one.
20. **story:** speaker: mia, mood: helpful, art: de-ber-office-floor -- trg: "Hier ist unsere Etage. Der Flur fuhrt zur Kantine, und am Ende vom Gang ist der Konferenzraum. Die Besprechung beginnt um zehn.", src: "Here is our floor. The hallway leads to the canteen, and at the end of the corridor is the conference room. The meeting starts at ten."
21. **mc:** Office vocabulary -- q: "Mia says the meeting is in the Konferenzraum. What is a Konferenzraum?" opts: [A conference room, A canteen, A corridor, A cloakroom] ans: A conference room. hint: "Konferenz means conference. Raum means room."
22. **fb:** Complete the sentence -- s: "Die {1} beginnt um zehn Uhr." a: ["Besprechung"] opts: [Besprechung, Kantine, Garderobe, Etage] hint: "Which word means a meeting or discussion?"

## Lesson 2: "Das Büro" (The Office)

**Type:** Vocab teach
**Mode:** Bubble -- office equipment and furnishings
**Characters:** None (pure vocabulary)
**Step count:** 24
**New vocabulary (20):** Bildschirm(der), Festplatte(die), Kabel(das), Kopierer(der), Kopie(die), Monitor(der), Ordner(der), Rechner(der), Regal(das), Steckdose(die), Stecker(der), Tastatur(die), Taste(die), Mappe(die), Kuli(der), Tabelle(die), ausdrucken, kopieren, klicken, tippen
**Grammar:** EXPOSED: Passive Prasens #7 (tip: "Die Datei wird ausgedruckt"), compound nouns (office equipment patterns)
**Communicative functions:** Name office equipment (taught), describe office setup (taught), give/follow workplace instructions (exposure)
**Cumulative vocab after: 40**

### Step sequence:
1. **intro:** "Das Buro" -- Goals: Learn the names of office equipment. Master tech vocabulary for the German workplace. Understand compound noun patterns for office items.
2. **teach:** Bildschirm(der) -- Fun info: Bild (picture) + Schirm (screen/umbrella). Literally "picture-screen." Germans use this for any display: computer monitor, TV screen, phone screen. Das Bild is neuter but der Schirm wins the gender.
3. **teach:** Monitor(der) -- Fun info: From Latin "monere" (to warn/watch). Interchangeable with Bildschirm in tech contexts. Germans use both. Monitor sounds more international, Bildschirm more traditionally German.
4. **teach:** Rechner(der) -- Fun info: From "rechnen" (to calculate). A computer is literally a "calculator." Taschenrechner (pocket-calculator) is a handheld calculator. Before English "Computer" took over, Rechner was the standard word.
5. **teach:** Tastatur(die) -- Fun info: From "Taste" (key/button). A keyboard. German keyboards have QWERTZ layout (not QWERTY). The Y and Z are swapped. Also has dedicated keys for ä, ö, ü, and ß.
6. **teach:** Taste(die) -- Fun info: A key or button. "Drücken Sie die Taste" (press the key). From French "taster" (to touch/feel). Every button on a keyboard, remote, or elevator is a Taste.
7. **teach:** Festplatte(die) -- Fun info: Fest (solid/fixed) + Platte (plate/disk). Hard drive. Literally "fixed disk." "Die Festplatte ist voll" is the IT nightmare sentence every office worker dreads.
8. **teach:** Kabel(das) -- Fun info: From Latin "capulum" (rope). Any cable or cord. "Kabelsalat" (cable salad) is the beautiful German word for tangled cables under your desk. Every office has Kabelsalat.
9. **teach:** Steckdose(die) -- Fun info: Stecken (to plug in) + Dose (socket/tin). A power outlet. German outlets are round (Schuko type). Visitors from the UK or US always need an adapter.
10. **teach:** Stecker(der) -- Fun info: From "stecken" (to plug). A plug. Der Stecker goes into die Steckdose. Simple, logical compound naming. German makes hardware vocabulary self-explanatory.
11. **teach:** Kopierer(der) -- Fun info: From "kopieren" (to copy) + -er (machine suffix). The copy machine. Every German office has one, and every new employee has to learn its quirks. "Der Kopierer ist kaputt" is the most common office complaint.
12. **teach:** Kopie(die) -- Fun info: A copy. From Latin "copia" (abundance). "Eine Kopie machen" (to make a copy). The bureaucratic soul of Germany runs on Kopien of everything.
13. **teach:** Ordner(der) -- Fun info: From "ordnen" (to organize). A binder or folder. Both physical ring binders AND computer folders. Germans keep physical Ordner for everything: taxes, insurance, contracts. The Ordner culture is legendary.
14. **teach:** Regal(das) -- Fun info: A shelf or bookcase. From Latin "regalis" (royal). Bücherregal (bookshelf) and Aktenregal (file shelf) are in every German office. IKEA's Billy is Germany's favorite Regal.
15. **teach:** Mappe(die) -- Fun info: A folder, portfolio, or briefcase. "Aktenmappe" is a document folder. Thinner than an Ordner. Students carry a Schulmappe, professionals carry a Mappe to meetings.
16. **teach:** Kuli(der) -- Fun info: Short for "Kugelschreiber" (ball-point writer). One of Germany's greatest word shortcuts. The full compound has 16 letters. Everyone just says "Kuli." Invented in 1938 by Hungarian László Bíró.
17. **teach:** Tabelle(die) -- Fun info: A table or spreadsheet. From Latin "tabella" (small board). Excel-Tabellen rule German office life. "Ich mache eine Tabelle" means someone is about to organize everything into neat rows and columns.
18. **teach:** ausdrucken -- Fun info: Aus- (out) + drucken (to print/press). To print out. "Können Sie das ausdrucken?" is heard 50 times a day in German offices. Despite digitalization, Germans still print a lot.
19. **teach:** kopieren -- Fun info: To copy. Regular -ieren verb (from Latin/French). "Kopieren Sie bitte diese Unterlagen" (please copy these documents). The -ieren verbs are easy: they always conjugate regularly and the past participle has no ge-.
20. **teach:** klicken -- Fun info: To click. Onomatopoeia borrowed from English. "Klicken Sie auf den Link" (click on the link). German tech vocabulary freely borrows English words and adds German grammar.
21. **teach:** tippen -- Fun info: To type. From English "to tip/tap." "Tippen Sie Ihr Passwort ein" (type in your password). Also means to guess in betting: "Ich tippe auf Bayern" (I bet on Bayern).
22. **mc:** Equipment recognition -- q: "You need to print a document. Which device do you use?" opts: [Kopierer, Rechner, Regal, Ordner] ans: Kopierer. hint: "Which machine makes copies and prints?"
23. **fb:** Workplace instruction -- s: "Bitte {1} Sie die Datei aus." a: ["drucken"] opts: [drucken, klicken, tippen, kopieren] hint: "Which verb means to print something out?"
24. **match:** Office equipment pairs -- pairs: [{trg:"Bildschirm",src:"screen/monitor"},{trg:"Tastatur",src:"keyboard"},{trg:"Festplatte",src:"hard drive"},{trg:"Steckdose",src:"power outlet"},{trg:"Kuli",src:"ballpoint pen"}]

## Lesson 3: "Die Kaffeekuche" (The Coffee Kitchen)

**Type:** Story development + grammar intro
**Mode:** Bubble -- office Kaffeekuche, passive-aggressive sticky notes, coffee machine
**Characters:** Verumius (learning unwritten rules), Mia (coaching), Thomas (birthday colleague)
**Step count:** 24
**New vocabulary (20):** Leiter(der), Leiterin(die), Leitung(die), Mitarbeiterin(die), Hausmeister(der), Sekretärin(die), Dienst(der), Überstunde(die), Terminkalender(der), Wand(die), Teppich(der), Spiegel(der), leiten, reinigen, erledigen, besprechen, anstrengend, einheitlich, technisch, digital
**Grammar:** TAUGHT: Passive Prasens #7 (tip: werden + past participle, "Der Kaffee wird um 8 gemacht"). EXPOSED: Passive Prateritum #8 ("Das Buro wurde letztes Jahr renoviert"), Relative clauses #10 ("Der Kollege, der den letzten Kaffee nimmt...")
**Communicative functions:** Describe workplace rules (taught), discuss colleagues and roles (taught), understand workplace culture (exposure)
**Cumulative vocab after: 60**

### Step sequence:
1. **intro:** "Die Kaffeekuche" -- Goals: Learn colleague vocabulary and job titles. Discover unwritten office rules. Meet the passive voice in workplace context.
2. **story:** speaker: mia, mood: direct, art: de-ber-kaffeekueche -- trg: "So, das hier ist die Kaffeekuche. Hier gelten besondere Regeln. Siehst du die Zettel an der Wand?", src: "So, this is the coffee kitchen. Special rules apply here. Do you see the notes on the wall?"
3. **story:** speaker: narrator, mood: amused, art: de-ber-kaffeekueche-notes -- trg: "An der Wand hangen bunte Zettel: 'Bitte Spulmaschine ausraumen!' 'Milch mit NAMEN beschriften!' 'Der Kaffee wird jeden Morgen frisch gemacht!'", src: "On the wall hang colorful notes: 'Please empty the dishwasher!' 'Label milk with your NAME!' 'The coffee is made fresh every morning!'"
4. **teach:** Leiter(der) -- Fun info: Leader or manager (masculine). From "leiten" (to lead). Also means "ladder" when feminine (die Leiter). Same word, different gender, completely different meaning. Classic German trap.
5. **teach:** Leiterin(die) -- Fun info: Female leader or manager. Leiter + -in. German requires gendered job titles: der Leiter/die Leiterin. Companies often write "Teamleiter/in" to include both.
6. **teach:** Leitung(die) -- Fun info: Leadership, management, or line/pipe. "Unter der Leitung von..." (under the leadership of...). Also: Telefonleitung (phone line), Wasserleitung (water pipe). One word, many pipes.
7. **teach:** Mitarbeiterin(die) -- Fun info: Mit- (with) + Arbeiterin (worker). Female colleague/employee. Germans increasingly use "Mitarbeitende" (gender-neutral) in official contexts. Your Mitarbeiter are your coworkers.
8. **teach:** Hausmeister(der) -- Fun info: Haus (house) + Meister (master). The building caretaker. In German offices and apartment buildings, the Hausmeister is the person who fixes everything, knows everything, and has all the keys.
9. **teach:** Sekretärin(die) -- Fun info: From Latin "secretarius" (confidant). The administrative assistant. Modern German offices increasingly use "Assistenz" or "Office-Management," but Sekretärin remains widely understood.
10. **teach:** Dienst(der) -- Fun info: Service or duty. "Im Dienst" (on duty), "Kundendienst" (customer service). A very formal word. "Dienst ist Dienst und Schnaps ist Schnaps" means work and pleasure do not mix.
11. **teach:** Überstunde(die) -- Fun info: Über- (over) + Stunde (hour). Overtime. Germans track Überstunden meticulously. Many contracts cap them. Working too many Überstunden is seen negatively, not as dedication.
12. **teach:** Terminkalender(der) -- Fun info: Termin (appointment) + Kalender (calendar). Your schedule/planner. Germans live by their Terminkalender. If it is not in the Kalender, it does not exist.
13. **teach:** Wand(die) -- Fun info: Wall (interior). Not to be confused with Mauer (exterior/freestanding wall). Die Berliner Mauer was THE Wall. Die Wand is what you hang pictures on inside.
14. **teach:** reinigen -- Fun info: To clean. More formal than "putzen." "Chemisch reinigen" means dry cleaning. Office kitchens always have a Reinigungsplan (cleaning schedule) on the wall.
15. **teach:** erledigen -- Fun info: To complete, to take care of. "Das ist erledigt" (that is done). One of the most satisfying German sentences. In office culture, erledigen means you finished a task completely.
16. **teach:** besprechen -- Fun info: Be- + sprechen (to speak). To discuss. "Wir müssen das besprechen" (we need to discuss this). More structured than "reden" (to talk). A Besprechung is a besprechen-event.
17. **teach:** leiten -- Fun info: To lead or manage. "Eine Abteilung leiten" (to manage a department). Also "to conduct" (electricity, water). Related to English "lead" through Proto-Germanic.
18. **tip:** "Passive Prasens: werden + Partizip II" -- In English: "Coffee is made." In German: "Kaffee wird gemacht." The structure: subject + werden (conjugated) + past participle (at the end). Werden is the helper verb for ALL passive sentences. ich werde, du wirst, er/sie/es wird, wir werden, sie/Sie werden. The person doing the action often disappears: "Der Kaffee wird gemacht" -- by whom? Nobody knows. The passive loves hiding responsibility. Office signs love the passive because rules apply to everyone.

## Lesson 4: "Grammatik im Buro" (Grammar at the Office)

**Type:** Grammar deep dive
**Mode:** Bubble -- learning mode, no scene
**Characters:** None (grammar instruction)
**Step count:** 26
**New vocabulary (18):** Auftrag(der), Direktor(der), Direktorin(die), Leiter(die), Liste(die), Organisation(die), Stil(der), Loch(das), Decke(die), Kerze(die), bedienen, klären, festlegen, festsetzen, feststehen, festhalten, anstrengend, inner
**Grammar:** TAUGHT: Passive Prateritum #8 (tip + verb_table: wurde + PP). TAUGHT: Relative clauses nom/acc/dat #10 (tip: der/die/das as relative pronouns). PRACTICED: Passive Prasens #7 (3+ quiz steps), Passive Prateritum #8 (3+ quiz steps), Relative clauses #10 (3+ quiz steps)
**Communicative functions:** Describe past events at work (taught), identify and describe people (taught), report workplace procedures (practiced)
**Cumulative vocab after: 78**

### Step sequence:
1. **intro:** "Grammatik im Buro" -- Goals: Master the passive voice in past tense. Learn relative clauses to describe people and things. Combine both structures in workplace contexts.
2. **teach:** Auftrag(der) -- Fun info: An order, task, or assignment. "Im Auftrag von" (on behalf of) appears on every official German letter. From "auftragen" (to assign). Your boss gives you Auftrage.
3. **teach:** Direktor(der) -- Fun info: Director. From Latin "dirigere" (to direct). The top boss. In German schools, the principal is also "der Direktor." More formal than Leiter.
4. **teach:** Direktorin(die) -- Fun info: Female director. Germany has been steadily increasing the number of Direktorinnen in leadership positions. The -in suffix is mandatory when referring to a specific woman.
5. **teach:** Leiter(die) -- Fun info: A ladder. Feminine. Remember: der Leiter = male leader, die Leiter = ladder. "Die Leiter, die an der Wand steht" (the ladder that stands against the wall). Gender changes meaning completely.
6. **teach:** Liste(die) -- Fun info: A list. Same word in many European languages. Germans make Listen for everything: Einkaufsliste (shopping list), Aufgabenliste (task list), Wunschliste (wish list).
7. **teach:** Organisation(die) -- Fun info: Same as English. The -tion ending is always feminine in German and always stressed on the last syllable: Organisa-TION. This rule works for Kommunikation, Information, Situation, and hundreds more.
8. **teach:** bedienen -- Fun info: Be- + dienen (to serve). To operate (a machine) or to serve (customers). "Eine Maschine bedienen" (to operate a machine). "Kunden bedienen" (to serve customers).
9. **teach:** klären -- Fun info: To clarify or settle. From "klar" (clear). "Das mussen wir klaren" (we need to clarify that) is the calm German way of saying "we have a problem."
10. **teach:** festlegen -- Fun info: Fest (firm) + legen (to lay). To determine or establish. "Einen Termin festlegen" (to set a date). The fest- prefix family (festhalten, festsetzen, feststehen, festlegen) all mean making something firm or fixed.
11. **tip:** "Passive Prateritum: wurde + Partizip II" -- Past passive: something WAS done. In English: "The office was built in 2010." In German: "Das Buro wurde 2010 gebaut." Structure: subject + wurde (conjugated) + time/place + past participle (at the end). Wurde is the Prateritum of werden. ich wurde, du wurdest, er/sie/es wurde, wir wurden, sie/Sie wurden. Compare: wird gemacht (IS being made) vs. wurde gemacht (WAS made). The passive Prateritum is standard in written German for reporting past events.

16. **tip:** "Relative clauses: der/die/das as relative pronouns" -- Relative clauses describe a noun. "The colleague WHO sits next to me" = "Der Kollege, DER neben mir sitzt." The relative pronoun matches the GENDER and NUMBER of the noun it refers to, but its CASE depends on its role in the relative clause. Nominative (subject): der/die/das/die. Accusative (object): den/die/das/die. Dative (indirect object): dem/der/dem/denen. The verb goes to the END of the relative clause. Comma before the relative pronoun is mandatory.

6. **teach:** Abonnement(das) -- Fun info: Subscription. From French "abonner." "Ein Zeitungsabonnement" (newspaper subscription). Germans love their Abos (abbreviation). Streaming, gym, magazines: everything runs on Abonnements.
7. **teach:** Anleitung(die) -- Fun info: An- (to) + Leitung (guidance). Instructions or guide. "Schritt-fur-Schritt-Anleitung" (step-by-step guide). Every IKEA furniture comes with a wordless Anleitung.
8. **teach:** Bedienungsanleitung(die) -- Fun info: Bedienung (operation) + Anleitung (instructions). User manual. The longest common compound in daily German office life. Germans actually READ their Bedienungsanleitungen.
9. **teach:** Gebrauchsanweisung(die) -- Fun info: Gebrauch (use) + Anweisung (instruction). Instructions for use. Slightly more formal than Bedienungsanleitung. Found on medicine, cleaning products, and technical equipment.
10. **teach:** Betriebsrat(der) -- Fun info: Betrieb (company) + Rat (council). Works council. A legally mandated employee representation body in German companies with 5+ employees. The Betriebsrat has real power: hiring, firing, working conditions.
11. **teach:** Treppenhaus(das) -- Fun info: Treppe (stairs) + Haus (house). Stairwell. The architectural soul of German apartment buildings. Many Treppenhauser have strict Hausordnung rules about noise and cleaning.
12. **teach:** Dach(das) -- Fun info: Roof. Same root as English "thatch." "Unter einem Dach" (under one roof) means living or working together. "Dachgeschoss" (roof floor) is the attic apartment, very popular in Berlin.
13. **teach:** Wecker(der) -- Fun info: From "wecken" (to wake). Alarm clock. Der Wecker is the enemy of every Monday morning. "Der Wecker klingelt um sechs" (the alarm rings at six) is the most dreaded sentence in German.
14. **teach:** Schlaf(der) -- Fun info: Sleep. Same root as English "sleep." "Guten Schlaf!" is an unusual but warm way to say goodnight. "Schlafzimmer" (bedroom), "Mittagsschlaf" (afternoon nap). Germans value their Schlaf.
15. **teach:** Couch(die) -- Fun info: Borrowed from English/French. Die Couch is feminine in German. "Auf der Couch liegen" (to lie on the couch). After Feierabend, the Couch is the destination.
16. **teach:** Lift(der) -- Fun info: Elevator. From English "lift." More common in Austria and Switzerland. In Germany, "Aufzug" or "Fahrstuhl" are more standard, but Lift is universally understood.
17. **teach:** abwaschen -- Fun info: Ab- (off) + waschen (to wash). To wash dishes. "Den Abwasch machen" (to do the dishes). In the Kaffeekuche, the eternal question: who does the Abwasch?
18. **teach:** anschließen -- Fun info: An- (to/on) + schließen (to close/connect). To connect or plug in. "Den Monitor anschließen" (to connect the monitor). Also: "sich anschließen" (to join/agree with someone).
19. **teach:** ausfallen -- Fun info: Aus- (out) + fallen (to fall). To fail, break down, or be cancelled. "Der Strom fällt aus" (the power goes out). "Die Besprechung fällt aus" (the meeting is cancelled). A versatile word for things going wrong.
20. **teach:** herausfinden -- Fun info: Heraus- (out) + finden (to find). To find out, to discover. "Ich muss herausfinden, wann Feierabend ist" (I need to find out when work ends). Separable: "Ich finde es heraus."
21. **teach:** Mobilität(die) -- Fun info: Mobility. "Elektromobilitat" (electric mobility) is a huge topic in Germany. The -itat ending is always feminine and matches English -ity: Qualitat, Aktivitat, Universitat.
22. **tip:** "Feierabend: the sacred boundary" -- Feierabend literally means "celebration evening." It marks the moment work ends and personal time begins. Germans take this boundary seriously. Sending work emails after Feierabend is frowned upon. Some companies lock email servers after 18:00. The Betriebsrat ensures Feierabend is respected. Uberstunden (overtime) must be tracked, compensated, and minimized. Work-life balance is not a buzzword in Germany. It is a cultural value protected by law.
