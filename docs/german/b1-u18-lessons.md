## Lesson 1: "Unter den Kastanien" (Under the Chestnut Trees)

**Type:** Story intro
**Mode:** Cinematic. Traditional Berlin Biergarten (Prater Garten style). Chestnut trees with fairy lights. Long wooden benches. Self-service area. Summer evening, golden hour light filtering through leaves. Beer steins on tables.
**Characters:** Verumius (happy, summer evening), Lukas (guitar case on back, unusual), Mia (running clothes, came from Lauftreff), Hildi (already seated, reserved spot)
**Step count:** 22
**id:** deu_r18l1
**board:** true
**New vocabulary (18):** Terrasse(die), Treffpunkt(der), Wirt(der), Wirtin(die), Kellner(der), Kellnerin(die), Ober(der), Trinkgeld(das), Grill(der), Scheibe(die), Mahlzeit(die), gemutlich, durstig, hungrig, satt, genießen, spazieren gehen, prost
**Grammar:** EXPOSED: als vs wenn in Hildi's reminiscence ("Als ich jung war, gab es hier nur drei Biergaerten"), Verbs + fixed preps in natural dialogue ("Ich freue mich auf den Abend")
**Communicative functions:** Ordering at a restaurant, expressing hunger/thirst/satisfaction, socializing in informal settings, describing atmosphere
**Cumulative vocab after: 2,321**

### Step sequence:
1. **intro:** "Unter den Kastanien." Goals: Enter the world of the German Biergarten. Learn restaurant and socializing vocabulary. Meet the full cast for a summer evening together.
2. **story:** speaker:narrator, mood:warm. A Berlin Biergarten in late June. Golden light filters through chestnut leaves. Fairy lights are already on. Long wooden benches, the smell of grilled sausages, the clink of beer steins. Hildi sits at a reserved table, arms folded, watching the entrance.
3. **story:** speaker:hildi, mood:pleased. "Hier! Ich habe den besten Tisch reserviert. Unter den alten Kastanien. Als ich jung war, gab es hier nur drei Biergaerten in ganz Berlin."
4. **story:** speaker:verumius, mood:happy. "Es ist wunderschoen hier. So gemutlich. Ich freue mich auf den Abend."
5. **story:** speaker:mia, mood:energetic. "Entschuldigung, ich komme direkt vom Lauftreff. Ich bin so durstig! Und hungrig. Beides."
6. **story:** speaker:narrator, mood:curious. Lukas arrives last. He carries something unusual on his back: a guitar case. Nobody says anything, but Mia raises an eyebrow.
7. **teach:** Terrasse(die) -- Fun info: From French "terrasse." An outdoor dining area. Biergaerten technically have their own traditions separate from a Terrasse, but both mean eating outside. Germans flock outdoors the moment the sun appears.
8. **teach:** Treffpunkt(der) -- Fun info: "Treff" (meeting) + "Punkt" (point). A meeting point. Every German friend group has their Treffpunkt. The Biergarten in summer is the ultimate one.
9. **teach:** Wirt(der) -- Fun info: The innkeeper or pub landlord. From Old High German "wirt" (host). A Biergartenwirt runs the whole operation. Related to English "worth" through the idea of hospitality.
10. **teach:** Wirtin(die) -- Fun info: Female innkeeper. The -in suffix. Many famous Berlin Wirtinnen have run legendary beer gardens for decades. Die Wirtin is also the title of a famous German folk song.
11. **teach:** Kellner(der) -- Fun info: From "Keller" (cellar). A waiter was originally the person who brought drinks up from the cellar. In Bavaria, you might hear "Bedienung!" (Service!) instead of calling the Kellner.
12. **teach:** Kellnerin(die) -- Fun info: Female server. In a traditional Biergarten, the Kellnerin carries multiple one-liter beer steins at once. The record is 29 steins carried at once by a Bavarian Kellnerin.
13. **teach:** Ober(der) -- Fun info: Short for "Oberkellner" (head waiter). An older, more formal word for waiter. "Herr Ober!" is how you call a waiter in a traditional Austrian or South German restaurant.
14. **teach:** Trinkgeld(das) -- Fun info: "Trink" (drink) + "Geld" (money). Tip money was originally money so the server could buy themselves a drink. In Germany, tipping is 5-10%, not the 15-20% common in the US.
15. **teach:** Grill(der) -- Fun info: Same word as English. Germans grill obsessively in summer. "Grillen" (to barbecue) is practically a national sport. A Biergarten often has a Grillstation with Bratwurst and Steaks.
16. **teach:** Scheibe(die) -- Fun info: A slice, disk, or pane. "Eine Scheibe Brot" (a slice of bread). Also "Windschutzscheibe" (windshield). The word covers anything flat and round or flat and thin.
17. **teach:** Mahlzeit(die) -- Fun info: "Mahl" (meal) + "Zeit" (time). Mealtime. Also a greeting: saying "Mahlzeit!" around noon is how German coworkers greet each other at lunch. It is simultaneously a word and a social ritual.
18. **teach:** gemutlich -- Fun info: Possibly the most untranslatable German word. Cozy, comfortable, convivial, warm, relaxed. A Biergarten under chestnut trees on a summer evening is the definition of gemutlich. No single English word captures it.
19. **teach:** durstig -- Fun info: From "Durst" (thirst). Thirsty. "Ich bin durstig" is survival vocabulary in any Biergarten. The -ig suffix turns nouns into adjectives: Durst becomes durstig, Hunger becomes hungrig.
20. **teach:** hungrig -- Fun info: From "Hunger." Hungry. Same pattern as durstig. "Bist du hungrig?" is the question that starts every Biergarten evening. Cognate with English "hungry."
21. **teach:** satt -- Fun info: Full, satisfied (after eating). "Ich bin satt" means you have had enough. The opposite of hungrig. Germans say "satt" not "voll" for food fullness. "Voll" means drunk.
22. **teach:** genießen -- Fun info: To enjoy, to savor. An irregular strong verb (genoss, genossen). "Genieße den Abend!" (Enjoy the evening!). From the same root as "Genuss" (pleasure/enjoyment). A word for conscious, deliberate enjoyment.
23. **teach:** spazieren gehen -- Fun info: To go for a walk. A separable verb phrase. "Wir gehen spazieren" (We go for a walk). From Italian "spaziare" (to take space). The German evening walk (Spaziergang) is a cultural institution.
24. **teach:** prost -- Fun info: From Latin "prosit" (may it be beneficial). The universal German toast. You MUST make eye contact when clinking glasses. Not doing so supposedly brings seven years of bad luck. "Prost!" then eye contact, then drink.
25. **mc:** Vocabulary check. q: "Everyone has arrived at the Biergarten. Mia says she is very thirsty. Which word describes her state?" opts: [durstig, hungrig, satt, gemutlich] ans: durstig. hint: "This word comes from Durst and describes wanting something to drink."
26. **mc:** Cultural knowledge. q: "What does 'Mahlzeit!' mean when a coworker says it at noon?" opts: [It is a lunch greeting, It means goodbye, It means thank you, It is an apology] ans: It is a lunch greeting. hint: "Mahl means meal, Zeit means time. Think about what happens at noon."
27. **fb:** Complete the dialogue. s: "Lukas, moechtest du etwas trinken? -- Ja, ich bin sehr {1}!" a: ["durstig"] opts: [durstig, hungrig, satt, gemutlich] hint: "Lukas wants something to drink. Which adjective describes being thirsty?"

## Lesson 2: "Brotzeit und Bratwurst" (Snack Time and Bratwurst)

**Type:** Vocab teach
**Mode:** Bubble. No scene. Pure food and kitchen vocabulary.
**Characters:** None (pure vocabulary)
**Step count:** 22
**id:** deu_r18l2
**board:** true
**New vocabulary (18):** Pfanne(die), Kanne(die), Schuessel(die), Dose(die), Tuete(die), Zutat(die), Zwiebel(die), Pfeffer(der), Mehl(das), Fett(das), Pilz(der), Pflaume(die), Aprikose(die), Frucht(die), Gebaeck(das), Picknick(das), roh, gar
**Grammar:** EXPOSED: Verbs + fixed preps in example sentences ("Das Gebaeck besteht aus Mehl und Zucker"), als vs wenn in food memory contexts ("Wenn ich Pflaumen sehe, denke ich an meine Oma")
**Communicative functions:** Naming kitchen equipment, describing ingredients, discussing food preparation states
**Cumulative vocab after: 2,339**

### Step sequence:
1. **intro:** "Brotzeit und Bratwurst." Goals: Learn essential food and kitchen vocabulary. Name ingredients and cooking equipment. Describe whether food is cooked or raw.
2. **teach:** Pfanne(die) -- Fun info: A pan or skillet. From Latin "patina." "Bratpfanne" (frying pan) is where Bratwurst gets its sizzle. Die Pfanne is feminine. "Etwas auf die Pfanne hauen" (to slap something on the pan) is slang for doing something boldly.
3. **teach:** Kanne(die) -- Fun info: A jug, pot, or pitcher. "Kaffeekanne" (coffee pot), "Teekanne" (teapot), "Giesskanne" (watering can). The -kanne compounds cover every kind of pouring vessel. Die Kanne is always feminine.
4. **teach:** Schuessel(die) -- Fun info: A bowl. From "schuetten" (to pour). A Schuessel holds salads, soups, or Kartoffelsalat at the Biergarten. Not to be confused with "Schluessel" (key). One letter difference, completely different objects.
5. **teach:** Dose(die) -- Fun info: A can or tin. "Konservendose" (canned food). In many German regions, Dose also means a small container or box. "Dosenbier" (canned beer) is the budget option at any German park gathering.
6. **teach:** Tuete(die) -- Fun info: A bag (paper or plastic). "Eine Tuete Chips" (a bag of chips). In northern Germany, Tuete is universal. In southern Germany and Austria, they say "Sackerl" or "Sack." Regional vocabulary at its finest.
7. **teach:** Zutat(die) -- Fun info: "Zu" (to/added) + "Tat" (deed/action). An ingredient, literally something "added to the deed" of cooking. "Zutaten" (plural) is what every recipe lists first. Die Zutat is feminine.
8. **teach:** Zwiebel(die) -- Fun info: From Latin "cepulla" (small onion). The Zwiebel is in nearly every German dish. "Zwiebelkuchen" (onion tart) paired with Federweisser (young wine) is an autumn tradition.
9. **teach:** Pfeffer(der) -- Fun info: From Latin "piper," which came from Sanskrit. Same root as English "pepper." "Geh dahin, wo der Pfeffer waechst!" (Go where the pepper grows!) means "Get lost!" because pepper came from far away.
10. **teach:** Mehl(das) -- Fun info: Flour. From Old High German "melo" (ground grain). Related to English "meal" (ground grain) and "mill." Das Mehl is neuter. German baking depends on precise Mehl types: Type 405 (fine), 550, 1050 (coarse).
11. **teach:** Fett(das) -- Fun info: Fat or grease. Same root as English "fat." "Fettarm" (low-fat), "fettfrei" (fat-free). Das Fett is neuter. "Ins Fettnaepfchen treten" (to step in the fat pot) means to make a social blunder.
12. **teach:** Pilz(der) -- Fun info: A mushroom. From Latin "pileus" (cap). Germans forage for Pilze in forests every autumn. "Pilzsammeln" (mushroom picking) is a beloved tradition but dangerous without expertise. Some Pilze are deadly.
13. **teach:** Pflaume(die) -- Fun info: A plum. From Latin "prunum" via Germanic transformation. "Pflaumenkuchen" (plum cake) is a classic late-summer dessert. In slang, "Du Pflaume!" means "You dolt!" but gently.
14. **teach:** Aprikose(die) -- Fun info: From Arabic "al-barquq" through Latin. An apricot. In Austria, they say "Marille" instead of Aprikose. Same fruit, different word depending on which side of the border you stand.
15. **teach:** Frucht(die) -- Fun info: Fruit. From Latin "fructus." "Fruechtetee" (fruit tea) is what Germans drink when they do not want caffeine. Die Frucht is the formal word. Casual German uses "Obst" for fresh fruit.
16. **teach:** Gebaeck(das) -- Fun info: From "backen" (to bake). The Ge- prefix makes it collective: all baked goods together. Cookies, pastries, bread rolls. Das Gebaeck is neuter, like most Ge- collective nouns.
17. **teach:** Picknick(das) -- Fun info: Borrowed from French "pique-nique." Das Picknick is neuter. Germans love a Picknick in the park: blanket, bread, cheese, wine. The Biergarten is essentially a permanent communal Picknick.
18. **teach:** roh -- Fun info: Raw, uncooked. "Rohes Fleisch" (raw meat), "Rohkost" (raw vegetables/health food). Related to English "raw" through Proto-Germanic. Germans eat some things raw that surprise foreigners: "Mett" (raw minced pork) is a real thing.
19. **teach:** gar -- Fun info: Cooked, done (as an adjective). "Das Fleisch ist gar" (The meat is done). Also an adverb meaning "at all" or "even": "gar nicht" (not at all). Two completely different meanings in one word.
20. **mc:** Kitchen equipment. q: "You need to fry onions. Which kitchen item do you use?" opts: [Pfanne, Kanne, Schuessel, Dose] ans: Pfanne. hint: "This item comes from Latin 'patina' and is used for frying."
21. **fb:** Ingredient knowledge. s: "Fuer den Kuchen brauchen wir {1}, Eier und Zucker." a: ["Mehl"] opts: [Mehl, Fett, Pfeffer, Pilz] hint: "The white powder that forms the base of all baking."
22. **match:** Food states and items. pairs: [{trg:"roh",src:"raw/uncooked"},{trg:"gar",src:"cooked/done"},{trg:"Zutat",src:"ingredient"},{trg:"Frucht",src:"fruit"}]
23. **mc:** Regional vocabulary. q: "In Austria, 'Aprikose' is called..." opts: [Marille, Pflaume, Frucht, Zwiebel] ans: Marille. hint: "This is the Austrian word for the same fruit. It comes up later in this unit."
24. **fb:** Complete the compound. s: "Zum Fruehstueck esse ich gerne frisches {1} vom Baecker." a: ["Gebaeck"] opts: [Gebaeck, Picknick, Mehl, Fett] hint: "The collective noun for all baked goods, with the Ge- prefix."

## Lesson 3: "Die Spargel-Panik" (The Asparagus Panic)

**Type:** Story development + grammar intro
**Mode:** Bubble. Biergarten table, beer steins, plate of white asparagus. Hildi's phone propped against a beer stein with Heinrich on speakerphone. Summer evening light.
**Characters:** Heinrich (speakerphone, insistent), Hildi (holding phone, amused), Verumius (confused about asparagus deadline), Lukas (laughing)
**Step count:** 22
**id:** deu_r18l3
**board:** true
**New vocabulary (17):** Saison(die), Ernte(die), Quittung(die), Rabatt(der), Sonderangebot(das), Schlange(die), Backofen(der), Nahrungsmittel(das), Ernaehrung(die), Diaet(die), Vitamin(das), reif, haltbar, vegetarisch, zubereiten, mischen, spuelen
**Grammar:** TAUGHT: #19 als vs wenn. Tip card explaining the difference: als for single past events, wenn for repeated/habitual/conditional. EXPOSED: #22 Verbs + fixed preps through Heinrich's speech ("Ich bestehe darauf, dass ihr Spargel bestellt!")
**Communicative functions:** Discussing seasonal food, expressing urgency, debating preferences, understanding cultural food traditions
**Cumulative vocab after: 2,356**

### Step sequence:
1. **intro:** "Die Spargel-Panik." Goals: Understand the German obsession with Spargelzeit. Learn the difference between 'als' (one-time past) and 'wenn' (repeated/conditional). Pick up food shopping and nutrition vocabulary.
2. **story:** speaker:narrator, mood:amused. Hildi's phone buzzes. She puts it on speaker. Heinrich's voice fills the Biergarten table. He sounds agitated.
3. **story:** speaker:heinrich, mood:urgent. "Es ist der zwanzigste Juni! Spargelzeit endet am vierundzwanzigsten! Habt ihr schon Spargel bestellt? Ich bestehe darauf!"
4. **story:** speaker:verumius, mood:confused. "Warum ist Spargel so dringend? Es ist doch nur Gemuese..."
5. **story:** speaker:hildi, mood:explaining. "Als ich ein Kind war, haben wir jedes Jahr auf den ersten Spargel gewartet. Wenn die Saison endet, gibt es keinen mehr. Bis naechstes Jahr."
6. **story:** speaker:lukas, mood:laughing. "Heinrich hat recht. Wenn man den letzten Spargel verpasst, ist das eine nationale Tragoedie."
7. **teach:** Saison(die) -- Fun info: From French "saison." Season, particularly for food or sports. Spargelzeit is Germany's most famous food Saison: mid-April to June 24. Germans eat 1.7 kg of Spargel per person per year.
8. **teach:** Ernte(die) -- Fun info: Harvest. From "ernten" (to harvest). "Erntezeit" (harvest time) is when German farmers are busiest. Die Ernte is feminine. The word carries weight in a country where seasonal eating is still important.
9. **teach:** Quittung(die) -- Fun info: A receipt. From Latin "quietare" (to settle). "Kann ich die Quittung haben?" (Can I have the receipt?) is essential shopping vocabulary. Germans keep Quittungen for everything, especially for tax returns.
10. **teach:** Rabatt(der) -- Fun info: From Italian "rabatto" (discount). A discount or reduction. "Sonderangebot" (special offer) and "Rabatt" are the words that make German shoppers happy. Der Rabatt is masculine.
11. **teach:** Sonderangebot(das) -- Fun info: "Sonder" (special) + "Angebot" (offer). A special deal. German supermarkets advertise Sonderangebote in weekly flyers. Das Sonderangebot is neuter because Angebot is neuter. Compound gender rule.
12. **teach:** Schlange(die) -- Fun info: A snake AND a queue. "Schlange stehen" (to stand in line) makes the queue a snake. Germans queue precisely but not always patiently. At the Biergarten self-service counter, the Schlange can get long.
13. **teach:** Backofen(der) -- Fun info: "Back" (bake) + "Ofen" (oven). Der Backofen. Every German kitchen has one. "Den Backofen auf 180 Grad vorheizen" (Preheat the oven to 180 degrees) begins every German recipe. Celsius, naturally.
14. **teach:** Nahrungsmittel(das) -- Fun info: "Nahrung" (nourishment) + "Mittel" (means/agent). Foodstuff. The formal, official word for food. You see it on packaging and in regulations. More technical than "Essen" (food/eating).
15. **teach:** Ernaehrung(die) -- Fun info: From "ernaehren" (to nourish). Nutrition or diet (in the general sense). "Gesunde Ernaehrung" (healthy nutrition) is a topic Germans take seriously. Die Ernaehrung covers your entire relationship with food.
16. **teach:** Diaet(die) -- Fun info: From Greek "diaita" (way of life). A diet. "Diaet machen" (to be on a diet). In German, Diaet can also mean a special medical diet, not just weight loss. Politicians' salaries are also called "Diaeten."
17. **teach:** Vitamin(das) -- Fun info: Coined in 1912 from Latin "vita" (life) + "amine." Das Vitamin is neuter. Germans are vitamin-conscious. "Vitamin C" is pronounced "Vitamin Tsay." The health-food market in Germany is enormous.
18. **teach:** reif -- Fun info: Ripe, mature. "Der Apfel ist reif" (The apple is ripe). Also used figuratively: "reif fuer etwas sein" (to be ready for something). "Ich bin reif fuer den Urlaub" (I am ready for vacation).
19. **teach:** haltbar -- Fun info: "Halt" (hold) + "-bar" (able). Durable, having a shelf life. "Mindestens haltbar bis..." (Best before...) appears on every German food package. The -bar suffix works like English "-able."
20. **teach:** vegetarisch -- Fun info: From Latin "vegetare" (to enliven). Vegetarian. Germany has one of the highest rates of vegetarianism in Europe. Berlin alone has over 60 fully vegetarian restaurants. "Gibt es etwas Vegetarisches?" is a common Biergarten question.
21. **teach:** zubereiten -- Fun info: "Zu" (to) + "bereiten" (to prepare). To prepare food. More specific than "vorbereiten" (general preparation). You "bereitest ein Essen zu" (prepare a meal). Separable: "Ich bereite das Essen zu."
22. **teach:** mischen -- Fun info: To mix. Regular verb. "Einen Salat mischen" (to toss a salad), "Karten mischen" (to shuffle cards). Related to English "mix" through Latin "miscere."
23. **teach:** spuelen -- Fun info: To rinse or wash (dishes). "Geschirr spuelen" (to wash dishes). Also "die Toilette spuelen" (to flush the toilet). The Spuelmaschine is the dishwasher. One verb, two very different cleaning contexts.
24. **tip:** "als vs wenn: Two words for 'when'." German has two words where English has one. ALS = one specific moment in the past. "Als ich ein Kind war..." (When I was a child...). WENN = every time, repeatedly, or hypothetically. "Wenn ich Spargel esse..." (When/whenever I eat asparagus...). Memory trick: ALS = ONCE in the past. WENN = WHENEVER or IF. Heinrich says "Als ich jung war" (one specific past). Hildi says "Wenn die Saison endet" (every year, repeated).

## Lesson 4: "Worauf freust du dich?" (What Are You Looking Forward To?)

**Type:** Grammar deep dive
**Mode:** Bubble. No scene. Grammar instruction with contextual examples from the Biergarten story.
**Characters:** None (grammar instruction)
**Step count:** 24
**id:** deu_r18l4
**board:** true
**New vocabulary (16):** Konsum(der), Fluesstigkeit(die), Alkohol(der), Kakao(der), Serviceangestellte(die), Vermietung(die), Vermittlung(die), Eroeffnung(die), bio, biologisch, mager, spitz, eroeffnen, konsumieren, loesen, heben
**Grammar:** TAUGHT: #22 Verbs + fixed prepositions (sich freuen auf/ueber, warten auf, denken an, bestehen auf, sich interessieren fuer). Verb table with common verb+prep combinations. TAUGHT: #23 da-/wo- compounds (darauf, darueber, daran, dafuer, worauf, worueber, woran, wofuer). Tip card explaining when to use da- vs wo- compounds.
**Communicative functions:** Asking about preferences and interests, expressing anticipation, referring to previously mentioned things with da-compounds, forming questions about prepositional objects with wo-compounds
**Cumulative vocab after: 2,372**

### Step sequence:
1. **intro:** "Worauf freust du dich?" Goals: Master verbs with fixed prepositions. Learn da-compounds (darauf, darueber) and wo-compounds (worauf, worueber). Use these to talk about feelings, interests, and plans.
2. **teach:** Konsum(der) -- Fun info: From Latin "consumere." Consumption. "Konsum" covers everything from food to media. "Konsumgesellschaft" (consumer society) is a word Germans use critically. Der Konsum is masculine.
3. **teach:** Fluessigkeit(die) -- Fun info: "Fluessig" (liquid) + "-keit" (noun suffix). Liquid, fluid. "Viel Fluessigkeit trinken" (drink plenty of fluids) is standard German health advice. The -keit suffix always creates feminine nouns.
4. **teach:** Alkohol(der) -- Fun info: From Arabic "al-kuhl" (the kohl/essence). Germany has some of the most relaxed alcohol laws in Europe. Beer from age 16, spirits from 18. The Biergarten is the temple of responsible Alkohol consumption.
5. **teach:** Kakao(der) -- Fun info: From Nahuatl "cacahuatl" via Spanish. Hot chocolate. "Jemanden durch den Kakao ziehen" (to pull someone through the cocoa) means to make fun of someone. Der Kakao is masculine.
6. **teach:** Serviceangestellte(die) -- Fun info: "Service" + "Angestellte" (employee). A service employee. The gender-neutral alternative to Kellner/Kellnerin. Die Serviceangestellte for women, der Serviceangestellte for men. Modern German hospitality vocabulary.
7. **teach:** Vermietung(die) -- Fun info: From "vermieten" (to rent out). Rental or letting. "Zimmervermietung" (room rental). Die Vermietung is feminine because of -ung. You see it on signs everywhere: "Ferienwohnung, Vermietung ab Juni."
8. **teach:** Vermittlung(die) -- Fun info: From "vermitteln" (to mediate/arrange). Mediation, placement, or brokering. "Arbeitsvermittlung" (job placement), "Zimmervermittlung" (accommodation service). The -ung ending makes it feminine.
9. **teach:** Eroeffnung(die) -- Fun info: From "eroeffnen" (to open). An opening, inauguration. "Die Eroeffnung des Biergartens" (the opening of the beer garden). Die Eroeffnung is a formal event. Grand openings in Germany involve speeches and ribbon-cutting.
10. **teach:** bio -- Fun info: Short for "biologisch." Organic. "Bio-Bratwurst," "Bio-Bier," "Bio-Gemuese." Germany is the largest organic food market in Europe. The "Bio" label is legally protected and strictly regulated.
11. **teach:** biologisch -- Fun info: From Greek "bios" (life) + "logos" (study). Biological, but in food context it means organic. "Biologische Landwirtschaft" (organic farming). Longer form of "bio," used in formal or written contexts.
12. **teach:** mager -- Fun info: Lean, thin, low-fat. "Magermilch" (skim milk), "mageres Fleisch" (lean meat). Also used for people: "Er ist ziemlich mager" (He is quite thin). Not a compliment when applied to food portions.

7. **teach:** Metzger(der) -- Fun info: A butcher. Standard southern German. Northern Germans say "Fleischer," Austrians say "Fleischhauer." Three words for one trade. Der Metzger comes from Latin "macellarius" (slaughterer). Every region defends its word.
8. **teach:** ernaehren -- Fun info: To nourish, to feed. Reflexive: "sich ernaehren" (to eat, to sustain oneself). "Sich gesund ernaehren" (to eat healthily). From "Nahrung" (nourishment). The er- prefix makes it about achieving nourishment.
9. **teach:** fressen -- Fun info: To eat (for animals). "Der Hund frisst" (The dog eats). Using "fressen" for a person is rude: "Er frisst wie ein Schwein" (He eats like a pig). Humans "essen," animals "fressen." The distinction matters.
10. **teach:** fuettern -- Fun info: To feed (an animal or baby). "Die Enten fuettern" (to feed the ducks). From "Futter" (animal food/feed). Also slang: "Ich muss meinen Computer fuettern" (I need to feed data into my computer).
11. **teach:** grillieren -- Fun info: Swiss German for "grillen" (to barbecue). The -ieren suffix shows French influence. In Switzerland: "Wir grillieren am See." In Germany: "Wir grillen im Park." Same smoke, different verb.
12. **teach:** naehem -- Fun info: To sew. "Eine Naehmaschine" (sewing machine). From Old High German "najen." Related to English "needle" through the same root. "Mit heißer Nadel genaehrt" (sewn with a hot needle) means done hastily.
13. **teach:** nuetzen -- Fun info: To be useful, to use. "Das nuetzt nichts" (That is no use). Southern German and Austrian variant of "nutzen." Both are correct. The ue vs u distinction is regional, not grammatical.
14. **teach:** nutzen -- Fun info: To use, to make use of. "Die Gelegenheit nutzen" (to seize the opportunity). Standard German variant. "Nutzen" as a noun (der Nutzen) means benefit. Verb and noun share the same form.
15. **teach:** runterwerfen -- Fun info: "Runter" (down) + "werfen" (to throw). To throw down. Separable: "Wirf das nicht runter!" (Do not throw that down!). Colloquial contraction of "herunterwerfen." Spoken German loves shortening.
16. **teach:** verpflegen -- Fun info: From "Pflege" (care) + ver- prefix. To cater, to provide food and care. "Die Gaeste verpflegen" (to cater for the guests). "Verpflegung" (catering/provisions) appears on conference schedules.
