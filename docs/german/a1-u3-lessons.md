## L1: "Wohnung frei" — Story Intro
**Type**: Story intro (cinematic) | **Mode**: Cinematic | **Steps**: 14
**Characters**: Verumius, Lena (phone voice), Frau Hildegard "Hildi" Schreiber (introduced)

### New Vocabulary (18 words)
- **housing (6)**: Wohnung(die), Miete(die), Nachbar(der), Schlüssel(der), Stock(der), Vermieter(der)
- **home (5)**: Haus(das), Zimmer(das), Balkon(der), Fenster(das), Flur(der)
- **verbs (4)**: mieten, planen, kosten, brauchen
- **other (2)**: Anfang(der), Ende(das)
- **housing (1)**: Apartment(das)

### Grammar: Recycled #1 Prasens, #17 W-questions (Was kostet...? Wie viele Zimmer?)
### Functions: #6 Describe places (exposure), #12 State prices (exposure)

### Step Sequence
1. `intro` — Unit 3 intro: "Die Wohnungssuche". Goals: family, home, furniture, apartment hunting with Hildi.
2. `story` — CINEMATIC: Prenzlauer Berg street. Verumius staring at apartment listings taped to a lamppost. Rain starts. One listing circled in red: "3 Zimmer, Balkon, Prenzlauer Berg. 650 Euro warm." He photographs it.
3. `teach` — die Wohnung (the apartment). Fun: Germans say "warm" rent (Warmmiete) includes heating. "Cold" rent (Kaltmiete) does not. You WILL be asked which one.
4. `teach` — das Haus (the house). Fun: A "Mehrfamilienhaus" is a multi-family house. Most Germans rent, not buy. Homeownership is ~50%, lowest in Europe.
5. `teach` — das Zimmer (the room). Fun: "3-Zimmer-Wohnung" means 3 rooms PLUS kitchen and bathroom. Kitchen and bath don't count as Zimmer.
6. `teach` — mieten (to rent). Fun: "Mieter" = tenant, "Vermieter" = landlord. The "ver-" prefix flips the meaning, like "buy" vs "sell."
7. `teach` — die Miete (the rent). Fun: Average Berlin rent has doubled since 2010. The "Mietpreisbremse" (rent brake) tries to slow it down. Good luck.
8. `teach` — kosten (to cost). Fun: Germans ask "Was kostet das?" more than "Wie viel kostet das?" Both work, but the short version is more natural.
9. `teach` — brauchen (to need). Fun: In spoken German, "brauchen" sometimes replaces "mussen" with "nicht": "Du brauchst nicht kommen" = you don't need to come.
10. `teach` — das Apartment / der Balkon / das Fenster / der Flur. Fun: Apartment is borrowed from French. Balkon too. Fenster comes from Latin "fenestra." German loves borrowing.
11. `teach` — der Schlüssel (the key) / der Stock (the floor/story). Fun: "Erdgeschoss" = ground floor (floor 0). "Erster Stock" = what Americans call the 2nd floor. This WILL confuse you.
12. `teach` — der Vermieter (the landlord) / der Nachbar (the neighbor). Fun: "Nachbar" literally = "near-dweller." Your Nachbar will know your schedule better than you do.
13. `teach` — planen / der Anfang / das Ende. Fun: "Aller Anfang ist schwer" = every beginning is hard. A German proverb you'll hear constantly.
14. `story` — CINEMATIC: Verumius calls the number. A woman's voice, precise and clipped: "Schreiber. Besichtigung morgen, 14 Uhr. Pünktlich." Click. He stares at the phone. Lena texts: "Hildi Schreiber? Viel Glück. Du brauchst es." Fade to title card: THE 14-PAGE HAUSORDNUNG.

### Grammar: #12 Possessive articles (exposure — mein/dein/sein only, nominative)
### Functions: #2 Introduce others (practice), #5 Describe people (practice)

### Step Sequence
1. `intro` — Lesson intro: Meet the family. Goals: all family members, extended family, relationship status, possessives mein/dein/sein.
2. `story` — BUBBLE: Verumius video-calling Lena night before the viewing. She asks about his "Selbstauskunft" (tenant self-disclosure form). "Familienstand?" — "Uh..."
3. `teach` — die Familie (the family). Fun: "Familienstand" on German forms means marital status. Options: ledig, verheiratet, geschieden. Verumius checks "ledig" with relief. A: "Was ist dein Familienstand?" / B: "Ledig. Ich bin ledig."
4. `teach` — der Vater (the father) / die Mutter (the mother). Fun: Informal: Papa/Mama. "Mutti" is what Angela Merkel was nicknamed. Every German kid says "Mama" first. A: "Wer ist das?" / B: "Das ist mein Vater."
5. `teach` — der Bruder (the brother) / die Schwester (the sister). Fun: "Bruder" and "brother" share a 5,000-year-old Proto-Indo-European root. Some words survive everything. A: "Hast du Geschwister?" / B: "Ja, einen Bruder und eine Schwester."
6. `teach` — der Sohn (the son) / die Tochter (the daughter). Fun: "Tochter" is cognate with English "daughter." The "T" and "D" swap is a regular Germanic sound shift. A: "Ist das dein Sohn?" / B: "Nein, das ist meine Tochter."
7. `teach` — das Kind (the child) / das Baby (the baby). Fun: "Kindergarten" is German's most famous export word. It means "children's garden." English borrowed it unchanged. A: "Wie viele Kinder hast du?" / B: "Zwei Kinder und ein Baby."
8. `teach` — das Mädchen (the girl) / **der Junge** (the boy). Fun: Mädchen is DAS (neuter!), not die, because the -chen suffix ALWAYS makes a word neuter. Junge is DER. "Das Mädchen und der Junge spielen." A: "Wer spielt im Garten?" / B: "Der Junge und das Mädchen."
9. `teach` — der Mann (the man/husband) / **die Ehefrau** (the wife) / **der Ehemann** (the husband). Fun: "Mann" means both "man" and "husband." "Ehefrau" and "Ehemann" are the formal terms. On forms you'll see "Ehepartner." A: "Mein Ehemann heißt Thomas." / B: "Und meine Ehefrau heißt Anna."
10. `teach` — die Eltern (the parents, pl) / die Geschwister (the siblings, pl). Fun: Both words exist ONLY in plural. There is no "ein Elter" or "ein Geschwister" in normal German. A: "Wo sind deine Eltern?" / B: "Meine Eltern wohnen in Hamburg."
11. `teach` — der Enkel (the grandchild) / die/der Erwachsene (the adult). Fun: "Erwachsene" changes article by gender: der Erwachsene (male), die Erwachsene (female). An adjective-noun. A: "Ist das ein Kind?" / B: "Nein, das ist ein Erwachsener."
12. `teach` — **der Großvater** (the grandfather) / **die Großmutter** (the grandmother). Fun: "Groß" = big/grand. Großvater = grand-father. The pattern is transparent. In English it's the same: grand + parent. A: "Mein Großvater ist 80 Jahre alt." / B: "Und deine Großmutter?"
13. `teach` — **der Opa** (grandpa) / **die Oma** (grandma). Fun: Opa and Oma are what German kids actually say. "Großvater" is formal, "Opa" is everyday. Every German kid has an Oma who bakes Kuchen. A: "Mein Opa kocht gern." / B: "Meine Oma auch!"
14. `teach` — **die Großeltern** (the grandparents, pl). Fun: Like "Eltern," "Großeltern" exists only in plural. "Meine Großeltern wohnen auf dem Land" is a classic German sentence. A: "Besuchst du deine Großeltern?" / B: "Ja, jedes Wochenende."
15. `teach` — **die Freundin** (the girlfriend/female friend). Fun: "Freundin" means both "female friend" and "girlfriend." Context matters. "Meine Freundin" with possessive usually means girlfriend. "Eine Freundin" usually means a female friend. A: "Ist das deine Freundin?" / B: "Nein, das ist eine Freundin."
16. `teach` — **die Partnerin** (the female partner) / **die Verwandte** (the female relative). Fun: "Partnerin" is gender-neutral in usage for any romantic partner. "Verwandte" covers all relatives beyond the nuclear family: cousins, aunts, uncles. A: "Wer kommt zum Essen?" / B: "Meine Partnerin und eine Verwandte."
17. `teach` — **die Hausfrau** (the housewife) / **der Hausmann** (the househusband). Fun: "Hausmann" has become more common in modern Germany. "Haus" + "Frau/Mann" is as transparent as it gets. Hildi would approve of a good Hausmann. A: "Was macht dein Mann?" / B: "Er ist Hausmann. Er kocht und putzt."
18. `teach` — ledig (single) / verheiratet (married) / geschieden (divorced). Fun: On every German form. "Verwitwet" (widowed) is the fourth option. Germans document EVERYTHING. A: "Bist du verheiratet?" / B: "Nein, ich bin ledig."
19. `teach` — **geboren** (born) / **jung** (young). Fun: "Ich bin 1995 geboren" = I was born in 1995. "Jung" is the opposite of "alt." "Jung und frei" is a very German feeling. A: "Wann bist du geboren?" / B: "Ich bin 1998 geboren."
20. `teach` — **männlich** (male) / **weiblich** (female). Fun: On EVERY German form. "Geschlecht: männlich/weiblich/divers." Since 2018, Germany recognizes a third option: "divers." A: "Was steht auf dem Formular?" / B: "Geschlecht: männlich oder weiblich."
21. `teach` — **die Jugendliche** (the teenager/young person). Fun: "Jugendliche" = people roughly 14-18. "Jugendherberge" = youth hostel. Like "Erwachsene," it's an adjective-noun that changes article by gender. A: "Wer wohnt hier?" / B: "Erwachsene und Jugendliche."
22. `teach` — die/der Bekannte (the acquaintance) / der Partner (the partner). Fun: "Bekannte" is another adjective-noun like Erwachsene. Germans distinguish sharply between Freund and Bekannte. A: "Ist das dein Freund?" / B: "Nein, nur ein Bekannter."
23. `tip` — Possessive articles intro: mein (my), dein (your), sein (his). They follow the ein-pattern: mein Bruder, meine Schwester, mein Kind. The ending changes with gender, just like ein/eine/ein.
24. `teach` — mein/meine (my). Fun: "Mein" for der/das nouns, "meine" for die nouns and all plurals. Same pattern as ein/eine. A: "Mein Bruder wohnt in Berlin." / B: "Meine Schwester auch."
25. `teach` — dein/deine (your, informal). Fun: "Dein" follows the exact same pattern as "mein." Learn one, you know both. A: "Ist das dein Hund?" / B: "Ja, das ist mein Hund."
26. `teach` — sein/seine (his). Fun: "Sein" also means "to be." Context separates them. "Sein Bruder ist nett" = His brother is nice. Two "sein" meanings in one sentence. A: "Sein Opa ist nett." / B: "Seine Oma auch."
27. `mc` — "Meine _____ heißt Anna." opts: [Bruder, Schwester, Sohn, Vater] ans: Schwester. Hint: "Meine" signals die-noun. Anna is a woman's name.
28. `mc` — "Das ist ___ Sohn." (his) opts: [mein, dein, sein, meine] ans: sein. Hint: "his" = which possessive? Der Sohn is masculine.
29. `mc` — "Mein _____ und meine Großmutter sind meine Großeltern." opts: [Großvater, Opa, Bruder, Enkel] ans: Großvater. Hint: The male grandparent paired with Großmutter. The formal word.
30. `fb` — "Mein {1} und meine Schwester sind Geschwister." a: Bruder, opts: [Bruder, Mutter, Tochter, Kind]. Hint: Male sibling paired with Schwester.
31. `mc` — "Der _____ spielt mit dem Mädchen." opts: [Junge, Mann, Opa, Enkel] ans: Junge. Hint: The boy. Male counterpart to das Mädchen.
32. `mc` — "Auf dem Formular steht: Geschlecht _____." opts: [männlich, ledig, geboren, jung] ans: männlich. Hint: The form asks about gender, not marital status or age.
33. `match` — Extended family pairs: [{nl:"der Opa",en:"grandpa"},{nl:"die Oma",en:"grandma"},{nl:"die Ehefrau",en:"the wife"},{nl:"der Junge",en:"the boy"},{nl:"die Verwandte",en:"the relative"}]
34. `fb` — "Meine Oma ist 1940 {1}." a: geboren, opts: [geboren, jung, alt, ledig]. Hint: She came into the world in 1940. Which word means "born"?
35. `mc` — "Verumius ist _____." opts: [verheiratet, geschieden, ledig, Enkel] ans: ledig. Hint: Not married, not divorced. What's left?

### New Vocabulary (26 words)

- **prepositions (3)**: hinter, zwischen, neben

- **verbs (2)**: erklären, empfehlen

- **ADDED other (1)**: alleine

Stock already taught L1 (SKIP). Total new = 26.

### Grammar: Recycled #6 Nominative, #8/#9 Articles, #13 Gender. Prepositions hinter/zwischen/neben (exposure).
### Functions: #6 Describe places and things (MUST practice)

### Step Sequence
1. `intro` — Lesson intro: The apartment viewing. Goals: rooms, furniture, spatial prepositions, describing a home.
2. `story` — BUBBLE: Verumius arrives at 13:58. Hildi opens the door at exactly 14:00. She is 70, silver hair in a bun, reading glasses on a chain, wearing a pressed blouse. "Herr Verumius? Schreiber. Pünktlich. Gut." She turns. He follows.
3. `teach` — die Küche (the kitchen). Fun: "Küche" comes from Latin "coquina" (cooking place). The German kitchen is sacred. NEVER leave dishes in the sink. Hildi will know. A: "Wo ist die Küche?" / B: "Hier links."
4. `teach` — das Wohnzimmer (the living room) / das Schlafzimmer (the bedroom). Fun: German room names are transparent: Wohn(living)+Zimmer(room), Schlaf(sleep)+Zimmer. Once you know "Zimmer," you can decode any room. A: "Das Wohnzimmer ist groß." / B: "Und das Schlafzimmer?"
5. `teach` — das Bad (the bathroom) / die Dusche (the shower). Fun: "Badezimmer" is formal, "Bad" is everyday. Germans shower in the morning. Evening showers are considered unusual. A: "Hat das Bad eine Dusche?" / B: "Ja, die Dusche ist neu."
6. `teach` — **die Toilette** (the toilet/restroom). Fun: "Toilette" is borrowed from French. In many German apartments, the Toilette is a separate tiny room from the Bad. Ask "Wo ist die Toilette?" and you might get a different room than "Wo ist das Bad?" A: "Wo ist die Toilette?" / B: "Neben dem Bad."
7. `teach` — der Boden (the floor) / das Dach (the roof) / der Garten (the garden). Fun: "Dachgarten" = rooftop garden. German compounds just stack: Dach + Garten. You can build words forever. A: "Der Boden ist neu." / B: "Und das Dach?"
8. `teach` — **die Treppe** (the stairs/staircase). Fun: "Treppenhaus" = stairwell. German buildings often have no elevator, just a Treppe. Hildi's building: 4th floor, no Aufzug. Legs of steel required. A: "Gibt es einen Aufzug?" / B: "Nein, nur die Treppe."
9. `teach` — **der Raum** (the room/space). Fun: "Raum" is more formal than "Zimmer." "Wohnraum" = living space. "Weltraum" = outer space. One word, from a room to the cosmos. A: "Wie viele Räume hat die Wohnung?" / B: "Drei Räume und eine Küche."
10. `teach` — der Kühlschrank (the fridge). Fun: Literally "cool-cabinet." German compound nouns are descriptions disguised as words. Handschuh (hand-shoe) = glove. Staubsauger (dust-sucker) = vacuum. A: "Funktioniert der Kühlschrank?" / B: "Ja, er ist neu."
11. `teach` — die Lampe / der Stuhl / der Tisch. Fun: When you move into a German apartment, it often has NO lamps, no kitchen cabinets, sometimes no floor. You bring EVERYTHING. A: "Ist die Lampe dabei?" / B: "Nein, Sie brauchen eine Lampe."
12. `teach` — die Möbel (the furniture, pl). Fun: "Möbel" is always plural in German. There is no singular "Möbel" for one piece. You say "ein Möbelstück" (a furniture-piece). A: "Sind Möbel dabei?" / B: "Nein, keine Möbel."
13. `teach` — **das Appartement** (the apartment, formal). Fun: "Appartement" is the French-spelling variant of "Apartment." Both exist in German. "Appartement" sounds fancier, often used for smaller furnished units or holiday rentals. A: "Ist das ein Appartement?" / B: "Nein, eine Wohnung. Drei Zimmer."
14. `teach` — **die Ordnung** (the order/tidiness). Fun: "Ordnung muss sein!" = there must be order! The most German sentence ever. Hildi's Hausordnung is literally "house-order." Germans have Ordnung for everything. A: "Die Ordnung ist wichtig." / B: "Ja, Ordnung muss sein!"
15. `teach` — **die Reparatur** (the repair). Fun: "Reparatur" comes from Latin "reparare." If something breaks, you call the Vermieter for a Reparatur. Hildi handles all Reparaturen herself. Nothing stays broken in her building. A: "Der Kühlschrank ist kaputt." / B: "Ich mache die Reparatur."
16. `teach` — **alleine** (alone, variant of allein). Fun: "Allein" and "alleine" mean the same thing. "Alleine" is slightly more colloquial. "Wohnst du alleine?" is what every German landlord asks to gauge how many people use the apartment. A: "Wohnst du alleine?" / B: "Ja, ich wohne alleine."
17. `story` — BUBBLE: Hildi walks him through each room. "Das Wohnzimmer. 28 Quadratmeter." She measures with her eyes. "Die Küche. Der Kühlschrank funktioniert. Der Boden ist neu." Everything stated like a legal fact. She opens the Toilette door: "Sauber. Immer." The Treppe creaks. "Vierter Stock. Keine Reparatur nötig."
18. `teach` — die Nebenkosten (the utility costs, pl). Fun: "Nebenkosten" = the costs BESIDE the rent: heating, water, trash, building insurance. The "zweite Miete" (second rent). Always ask how much. A: "Wie hoch sind die Nebenkosten?" / B: "200 Euro im Monat."
19. `teach` — erklären (to explain) / empfehlen (to recommend). Fun: Hildi erklärt everything. In extreme detail. Whether you asked or not. A: "Können Sie das erklären?" / B: "Natürlich. Ich erkläre alles."
20. `teach` — hinter (behind) / zwischen (between) / neben (next to). Fun: These prepositions answer "wo?" (where). "Der Stuhl steht neben dem Tisch." Spatial prepositions are essential for describing rooms. A: "Wo ist der Schrank?" / B: "Neben dem Fenster."
21. `tip` — Spatial prepositions: hinter (behind), zwischen (between), neben (next to). These combine with articles: "hinter dem Schrank" (behind the wardrobe), "zwischen der Küche und dem Bad" (between the kitchen and bathroom). Full dative case comes in A2. For now, learn the positions.
22. `mc` — "Der Kühlschrank steht in der _____." opts: [Küche, Schlafzimmer, Garten, Dach] ans: Küche. Hint: Where do you keep food cold?
23. `fb` — "Das Wohnzimmer ist {1} der Küche und dem Schlafzimmer." a: zwischen, opts: [zwischen, hinter, neben, unter]. Hint: The living room is in the middle of two other rooms.
24. `mc` — "Wo ist die Toilette? _____ dem Bad." opts: [Neben, Hinter, Zwischen, Auf] ans: Neben. Hint: The toilet room is right next to the bathroom.
25. `match` — Room pairs: [{nl:"die Küche",en:"the kitchen"},{nl:"das Bad",en:"the bathroom"},{nl:"die Treppe",en:"the stairs"},{nl:"die Toilette",en:"the restroom"},{nl:"der Raum",en:"the room/space"}]
26. `mc` — "Die Möbel _____ neu." opts: [ist, sind, hat, bist] ans: sind. Hint: Möbel is always plural. Plural verb form needed.

### New Vocabulary (26 words)

- **ADDED home-verbs (3)**: anmachen, ausmachen, baden
- **verbs (6)**: funktionieren, gehören, reparieren, renovieren, unterschreiben, wünschen
- **ADDED verbs (2)**: anziehen, aussehen
- **ADDED housing (1)**: vermieten
- **possessives (4)**: ihr, ihre, unser, unsere, seine [completing the 6-set]
- **other (2)**: Menge(die), Rest(der)
- **affix (1)**: Liebling-
- **ADDED affix (1)**: weiter-

Total new: 5 + 3 + 6 + 2 + 1 + 4 + 2 + 1 + 1 + 1 + 1 = 27. Seine partially covered in L2, counted here = 26 net new words.

### Grammar: #4 Separable verbs (CORE — 3+ quiz steps), #12 Possessive articles continued (ihr/unser + full paradigm)
### Functions: #6 Describe places (practice via verbs), #15 Fill in forms (exposure — lease vocabulary)

### Step Sequence
1. `intro` — Lesson intro: Separable verbs and possessives. Goals: understand prefix separation, conjugate separable verbs, use all possessive articles.
2. `tip` — SEPARABLE VERBS: Some German verbs split in two. "aufräumen" (to tidy up) becomes "Ich räume auf." The prefix (auf-) goes to the END of the sentence. Think of it as a boomerang: the prefix leaves but always comes back at the end. Common separable prefixes: auf-, ein-, um-, mit-, an-, ab-, aus-.
3. `teach` — aufräumen (to tidy up). Fun: Hildi's favorite verb. "Räumen Sie bitte auf!" The "auf" flies to the end. Literally "up-clearing." German apartments must be aufgeräumt at all times. Hildi checks. A: "Räumst du auf?" / B: "Ja, ich räume gerade auf."
4. `teach` — einziehen (to move in). Fun: "Ich ziehe ein" = I'm moving in. The "ein" (in) separates. The opposite: "ausziehen" (to move out). Both separate the same way. A: "Wann ziehst du ein?" / B: "Ich ziehe morgen ein."
5. `teach` — umziehen (to move/relocate). Fun: "Umziehen" has TWO meanings depending on stress: UMziehen (separable) = to relocate. umZIEHEN (inseparable) = to change clothes. Stress matters! A: "Wir ziehen nächste Woche um." / B: "Wohin zieht ihr um?"
6. `teach` — **anmachen** (to turn on/switch on) / **ausmachen** (to turn off/switch off). Fun: "Mach das Licht an!" = turn the light on. "Mach das Licht aus!" = turn it off. "An" and "aus" are opposites. Both separable. Hildi's Hausordnung has rules about when to anmachen and ausmachen. A: "Machst du das Licht an?" / B: "Nein, ich mache es aus."
7. `teach` — **anziehen** (to put on/get dressed) / **aussehen** (to look/appear). Fun: "Ich ziehe mich an" = I get dressed. "Das sieht gut aus" = that looks good. Both separable. "Anziehen" also means "to attract" (einen Magneten anziehen). A: "Was ziehst du an?" / B: "Das sieht gut aus!"
8. `teach` — putzen (to clean) / waschen (to wash) / **baden** (to bathe). Fun: "Putzen" is surface cleaning (floors, windows). "Waschen" is water cleaning (clothes, hands). "Baden" is soaking in a tub. Hildi expects all three, regularly. A: "Ich wasche die Kleidung." / B: "Und ich bade das Kind."
9. `teach` — funktionieren (to function/work). Fun: "Der Kühlschrank funktioniert" = the fridge works. Germans love this verb for machines and systems. If something "funktioniert nicht," prepare for a 3-week repair wait. A: "Funktioniert die Lampe?" / B: "Nein, sie funktioniert nicht."
