## Lesson 1: "Lampenfieber" (Stage Fright)

**Type:** Story intro
**Mode:** Cinematic. Verumius's apartment, evening before the presentation. Laptop with slides glowing. He practices in front of the mirror, gesturing too much, then not enough. Evening anxiety.
**Characters:** Verumius alone (nervous, rehearsing, not satisfied)
**Step count:** 22
**id:** deu_r27l1
**board:** true
**New vocabulary (18):** Praesentation(die), Vortrag(der), Publikum(das), Fachvortrag(der), Folie(die), Gliederung(die)[V1], Hauptteil(der), Schlusswort(das), Aufbau(der), Ablauf(der), Vorbereitung(die), Kernaussage(die), ueberzeugend, anschaulich, strukturiert, praesentieren, vortragen, einleiten
**Grammar:** EXPOSED: Konsekutivsaetze in Verumius's self-talk ("Er war so nervoes, dass er den Text vergass"). Konditionalsaetze in planning ("Falls sie Fragen stellen..."). Natural exposure only.
**Communicative functions:** Describing presentations, expressing nervousness, planning formal speech structure, naming presentation components
**Cumulative vocab after: ~3,218**

### Step sequence:
1. **intro:** "Lampenfieber." Goals: Enter the world of German professional presentations. Learn core presentation vocabulary. Understand what makes a German Vortrag different from casual speaking.
2. **story:** speaker:narrator, mood:tense. Verumius's apartment, late evening. The laptop screen glows with half-finished slides. A mirror reflects his rehearsal attempts. He gestures too broadly, then stands rigid. Neither feels right.
3. **story:** speaker:verumius, mood:nervous. "Morgen ist der Vortrag. Meine erste Praesentation auf Deutsch. Vor dem ganzen Team. Was, wenn ich alles vergesse?"
4. **story:** speaker:verumius, mood:thinking. "Ich brauche eine klare Gliederung. Einleitung, Hauptteil, Schluss. Und eine Kernaussage, die ueberzeugt."
5. **story:** speaker:narrator, mood:warm. He opens a new document. Types: "Projektbericht Q2." Deletes it. Types: "Wie roemische Aquaedukte unser Projektmanagement erklaeren." Pauses. Smiles.
6. **teach:** Praesentation(die) -- Fun info: From Latin "praesentare" (to present/show). Die Praesentation is the formal word for a presentation in German business culture. Germans expect structured, well-prepared Praesentationen. "Tod durch PowerPoint" (death by PowerPoint) is a German office joke too.
7. **teach:** Vortrag(der) -- Fun info: "Vor" (before/in front of) + "Trag" (carrying). You "carry" your ideas before an audience. Der Vortrag is more formal than Praesentation and implies expertise. "Einen Vortrag halten" (to give a presentation) is the standard collocate.
8. **teach:** Publikum(das) -- Fun info: From Latin "publicum" (the public). Das Publikum means audience. Same root as English "public." In German, your Publikum can be a theater audience, a lecture hall, or your colleagues at the Monday meeting.
9. **teach:** Fachvortrag(der) -- Fun info: "Fach" (subject/field) + "Vortrag." A specialist presentation. Der Fachvortrag implies deep expertise. At German conferences, a Fachvortrag is expected to go into technical detail. General audiences get a "Ueberblicksvortrag" instead.
10. **teach:** Folie(die) -- Fun info: From Latin "folium" (leaf). Originally meant a transparency sheet for overhead projectors. Die Folie now means a presentation slide. "Naechste Folie, bitte" (Next slide, please) is heard in every German meeting room.
11. **teach:** Gliederung(die)[V1] -- Fun info: From "gliedern" (to structure/divide into parts). An outline, a structure. Die Gliederung is sacred in German academic and professional culture. No thesis, no Vortrag, no report ships without a Gliederung. "Glied" means limb. A Gliederung is the skeleton.
12. **teach:** Hauptteil(der) -- Fun info: "Haupt" (main/head) + "Teil" (part). The main body of a presentation or text. Der Hauptteil. German Vortraege follow a strict three-part structure: Einleitung (introduction), Hauptteil (main body), Schluss (conclusion). No exceptions.
13. **teach:** Schlusswort(das) -- Fun info: "Schluss" (end/conclusion) + "Wort" (word). Concluding remarks. Das Schlusswort. In German presentations, the Schlusswort is where you summarize, thank the audience, and open the floor for questions. A strong Schlusswort leaves a lasting impression.
14. **teach:** Aufbau(der) -- Fun info: "Auf" (up) + "Bau" (building/construction). Structure, organization. Der Aufbau. How something is built up. "Der Aufbau des Vortrags" (the structure of the presentation) is what every German coach asks about first.
15. **teach:** Ablauf(der) -- Fun info: "Ab" (off/away) + "Lauf" (run/course). The sequence, the procedure. Der Ablauf. "Der Ablauf der Veranstaltung" (the schedule of the event). How things run from start to finish. Germans love a clear Ablauf.
16. **teach:** Vorbereitung(die) -- Fun info: "Vor" (before) + "Bereitung" (preparation). Preparation. Die Vorbereitung. Germans are legendary for Vorbereitung. An unprepared presentation is a professional sin. The compound tells you: do it BEFORE the event.
17. **teach:** Kernaussage(die) -- Fun info: "Kern" (core/kernel) + "Aussage" (statement). The key message. Die Kernaussage. Every German Vortrag must have one clear Kernaussage. If your audience remembers one thing, it should be this. The kernel inside the shell of details.
18. **teach:** ueberzeugend -- Fun info: "Ueber" (over) + "zeugend" (generating/witnessing). Convincing. Present participle of ueberzeugen. "Ein ueberzeugender Vortrag" is one that "generates belief over" the audience. The highest compliment for a presentation in German.
19. **teach:** anschaulich -- Fun info: "An" (at) + "schaulich" (viewable). Vivid, illustrative. "Anschaulich erklaeren" means to explain so people can "look at" the idea. German teachers prize anschauliche Beispiele (vivid examples). Making the abstract visible.
20. **teach:** strukturiert -- Fun info: From Latin "structura." Structured, organized. "Klar strukturiert" (clearly structured) is the gold standard for German presentations. Past participle used as adjective. German professional culture worships Struktur.
21. **teach:** praesentieren -- Fun info: From Latin "praesentare." To present. Not separable. "Ich praesentiere die Ergebnisse" (I present the results). More formal than "zeigen" (to show). The professional verb for showing work.
22. **teach:** vortragen -- Fun info: "Vor" (before) + "tragen" (to carry). To present, to deliver a speech. Separable: "Ich trage meine Ideen vor." Strong verb: vortragen/trug vor/vorgetragen. More formal than praesentieren and implies oral delivery.
23. **teach:** einleiten -- Fun info: "Ein" (in/into) + "leiten" (to lead/guide). To introduce, to open. Separable: "Ich leite den Vortrag ein." You "lead into" the topic. Die Einleitung (introduction) is the noun form. The first step of the sacred three-part structure.
24. **mc:** Core vocabulary. q: "Verumius needs to organize his presentation into three parts. What is the German word for the main body?" opts: [Hauptteil, Einleitung, Schlusswort, Gliederung] ans: Hauptteil. hint: "The 'main part' of any German presentation, between introduction and conclusion."
25. **fb:** Complete the sentence. s: "Ein guter Vortrag braucht eine klare {1}." a: ["Kernaussage"] opts: [Kernaussage, Folie, Ablauf, Praesentation] hint: "The 'core statement' that your audience should remember above all else."
26. **mc:** Meaning check. q: "Verumius wants his presentation to be 'ueberzeugend.' What does this mean?" opts: [convincing, structured, vivid, formal] ans: convincing. hint: "The word literally means 'generating belief over' someone. The highest praise for a Vortrag."

## Lesson 2: "Fachbegriffe und Formeln" (Technical Terms and Formulas)

**Type:** Vocab teach
**Mode:** Bubble. No scene. Pure vocabulary.
**Characters:** None (pure vocabulary)
**Step count:** 24
**id:** deu_r27l2

**New vocabulary (20):** Flipchart(das), Handout(das), Bildschirmpraesentation(die), Diagramm(das), Uebersicht(die), Ueberleitung(die), Uebergang(der)[V1], Formulierung(die), Zuhoerer(der), Zuhoererin(die), Referent(der), Referentin(die), Moderator(der), Moderatorin(die), ausfuehrlich, praegnant, verstaendlich, konkret, professionell, kompetent
**Grammar:** EXPOSED: Finalsaetze in example sentences ("Er nutzt ein Diagramm, damit das Publikum die Daten versteht"). Natural exposure through teach card examples.
**Communicative functions:** Naming presentation tools and roles, describing speaker qualities, using professional register vocabulary
**Cumulative vocab after: ~3,238**

### Step sequence:
1. **intro:** "Fachbegriffe und Formeln." Goals: Learn words for presentation tools and equipment. Master the vocabulary for speaker roles. Describe professional qualities in German.
2. **teach:** Flipchart(das) -- Fun info: English loanword. Das Flipchart. Despite PowerPoint dominance, many German workshops still love the Flipchart. "Etwas am Flipchart festhalten" (to capture something on the flipchart) is workshop culture at its finest. The neuter article because it is a foreign word.
3. **teach:** Handout(das) -- Fun info: Another English loanword. Das Handout. In German academia, a Handout is expected at every seminar presentation. It summarizes key points on one page. Professors judge your Handout as seriously as your Vortrag.
4. **teach:** Bildschirmpraesentation(die) -- Fun info: "Bildschirm" (screen, literally "picture-screen") + "Praesentation." A screen presentation, i.e. a slideshow. Die Bildschirmpraesentation. At 24 letters, this compound demonstrates why Germans love abbreviations. Most just say "Praesentation."
5. **teach:** Diagramm(das) -- Fun info: From Greek "diagramma" (figure drawn with lines). Das Diagramm. A chart or diagram. "Kreisdiagramm" (pie chart), "Balkendiagramm" (bar chart), "Liniendiagramm" (line chart). Germans love data visualizations in Vortraege.
6. **teach:** Uebersicht(die) -- Fun info: "Ueber" (over) + "Sicht" (view/sight). An overview. Die Uebersicht. Literally a "view from above." "Ich gebe Ihnen eine Uebersicht" (I will give you an overview) is how many German presentations begin.
7. **teach:** Ueberleitung(die) -- Fun info: "Ueber" (over) + "Leitung" (leading/guidance). A transition. Die Ueberleitung. The art of smoothly moving from one topic to the next. "Eine elegante Ueberleitung" separates good speakers from great ones.
8. **teach:** Uebergang(der)[V1] -- Fun info: "Ueber" (over) + "Gang" (walk/passage). A transition, a passage. Der Uebergang. While Ueberleitung is about guiding, Uebergang is about the passage itself. "Der Uebergang zum naechsten Thema" (the transition to the next topic).
9. **teach:** Formulierung(die) -- Fun info: From "formulieren" (to formulate). Wording, phrasing. Die Formulierung. "Eine glueckliche Formulierung" (a felicitous phrasing) can make or break a presentation. German business culture values precise Formulierungen.
10. **teach:** Zuhoerer(der) -- Fun info: "Zu" (to) + "Hoerer" (listener). A listener, an audience member. Der Zuhoerer. "Die Zuhoerer fesseln" (to captivate the listeners) is every speaker's goal. Active listening: "zuhoeren" is more attentive than just "hoeren."
11. **teach:** Zuhoererin(die) -- Fun info: Zuhoerer + "-in." Die Zuhoererin. A female listener. The standard feminine form. In modern German, many speakers use "Zuhoererinnen und Zuhoerer" to address their full audience inclusively.
12. **teach:** Referent(der) -- Fun info: From Latin "referens" (one who reports). A speaker, a presenter. Der Referent. More formal than "Sprecher." At German conferences, the Referent is the expert who delivers the Fachvortrag. N-Deklination: Referent, Referenten.
13. **teach:** Referentin(die) -- Fun info: Referent + "-in." Die Referentin. A female speaker/presenter. "Die Referentin hat ueberzeugend vorgetragen" (The speaker presented convincingly) is high praise at a German conference.
14. **teach:** Moderator(der) -- Fun info: From Latin "moderator" (one who regulates). Der Moderator. The person who guides a discussion or event. In German TV, the Moderator is the host. In meetings, the Moderator keeps things on track and on time.
15. **teach:** Moderatorin(die) -- Fun info: Moderator + "-in." Die Moderatorin. A female moderator/host. German conference culture always credits the Moderatorin by name. "Vielen Dank an unsere Moderatorin" is a standard closing line.
16. **teach:** ausfuehrlich -- Fun info: "Aus" (out) + "fuehrlich" (leading). Detailed, thorough. "Ausfuehrlich erklaeren" (to explain in detail). The opposite of "kurz" (brief). German academic culture values ausfuehrliche Darstellungen (detailed accounts).
17. **teach:** praegnant -- Fun info: From Latin "praegnans" (weighty/meaningful). Concise and to the point. False friend alert: "praegnant" in German does NOT mean pregnant. It means pithy, succinct. "Eine praegnante Formulierung" is brief but powerful.
18. **teach:** verstaendlich -- Fun info: From "verstehen" (to understand) + "-lich." Understandable, comprehensible. "Klar und verstaendlich" (clear and understandable) is what every audience wants. "Allgemeinverstaendlich" means accessible to a general audience.
19. **teach:** konkret -- Fun info: From Latin "concretus" (solid/dense). Concrete, specific. "Koennen Sie das konkret beschreiben?" (Can you describe that concretely?) is what Germans ask when you are too vague. The opposite of "abstrakt."
20. **teach:** professionell -- Fun info: From French "professionnel." Professional. Stress on the last syllable: pro-fess-io-NELL. "Eine professionelle Praesentation" is the minimum expectation in German business culture. Not a compliment but a baseline.
21. **teach:** kompetent -- Fun info: From Latin "competens" (suitable). Competent, knowledgeable. "Fachlich kompetent" (professionally competent) is how German colleagues earn respect. "Kompetenz" (competence) is the noun. Germans value demonstrated Kompetenz over charisma.
22. **tip:** "The German Vortrag structure." Every German Vortrag follows three parts: Einleitung (introduction, ~10%), Hauptteil (main body, ~80%), Schluss (conclusion, ~10%). The Einleitung greets, introduces the topic, and states the Kernaussage. The Hauptteil develops arguments with evidence. The Schluss summarizes, thanks, and opens for questions. Deviating from this structure marks you as unprepared.

25. **fb:** Complete the sentence. s: "Die Referentin erklaerte das Thema sehr {1}." a: ["anschaulich"] opts: [anschaulich, praegnant, ausfuehrlich, professionell] hint: "She explained it so vividly that people could 'see' the idea. Making the abstract visible."

This means..." opts: [concise and to the point, too long, needs more detail, excellent] ans: concise and to the point. hint: "This is a false friend. In German it means pithy and succinct, not what it sounds like in English."

**Type:** Story development + grammar intro
**Mode:** Bubble. Office meeting room, early morning. Mia coaching Verumius through his script with a red pen. Direct feedback.
**Characters:** Mia (direct/coaching, red pen, supportive but firm), Verumius (thinking, absorbing feedback)
**Step count:** 24
**id:** deu_r27l3
**board:** true
**New vocabulary (20):** Tagung(die), Veranstaltung(die), Sitzung(die), Besprechung(die), Konferenzraum(der), Tagesordnung(die), Tagesordnungspunkt(der), Rahmenprogramm(das), Redezeit(die), Redebeitrag(der), demnach, daraufhin, erstens, ferner, schliesslich, darstellen, hervorheben, aufzeigen, skizzieren, hinweisen
**Grammar:** TAUGHT: #12 Konsekutivsaetze (sodass, so... dass, infolgedessen, folglich, demnach). Tip card explaining consecutive clauses. Verb table for clause patterns. EXPOSED: #14 Konditionalsaetze advanced in Mia's coaching ("Sofern du die Daten klar praesentierst...").
**Communicative functions:** Describing meetings and conferences, using discourse markers, expressing consequences and results, structuring arguments
**Cumulative vocab after: ~3,258**

### Step sequence:
1. **intro:** "So klar, dass alle nicken." Goals: Learn Konsekutivsaetze (consecutive/result clauses). Master meeting and conference vocabulary. Use discourse markers to structure arguments.
2. **story:** speaker:narrator, mood:focused. Office meeting room, 7:30 AM. Mia is already there, red pen in hand. Verumius's script is spread across the table. She has circled entire paragraphs.
3. **story:** speaker:mia, mood:direct. "Dein Vortrag ist zu lang. Die Zuhoerer verlieren die Geduld. Du musst so klar formulieren, dass jeder den Punkt sofort versteht."
4. **story:** speaker:verumius, mood:thinking. "Also: erstens das Problem, ferner die Analyse, schliesslich die Loesung?"
5. **story:** speaker:mia, mood:approving. "Genau. Und die Ueberleitung muss so praegnant sein, dass niemand den Faden verliert. Demnach: weniger Folien, mehr Struktur."
6. **teach:** Tagung(die) -- Fun info: From "tagen" (to meet in session). A conference, a convention. Die Tagung. More academic than "Konferenz." German universities host Tagungen where researchers present Fachvortraege. "Jahrestagung" (annual conference) is the big one.
7. **teach:** Veranstaltung(die) -- Fun info: From "veranstalten" (to organize). An event. Die Veranstaltung. The umbrella word for any organized gathering: conference, concert, workshop, lecture. "Veranstaltungsort" (venue) and "Veranstalter" (organizer) derive from it.
8. **teach:** Sitzung(die) -- Fun info: From "sitzen" (to sit). A meeting, a session. Die Sitzung. Literally "a sitting." German committee culture runs on Sitzungen. "Vorstandssitzung" (board meeting), "Ausschusssitzung" (committee meeting). Everyone sits. Everyone talks.
9. **teach:** Besprechung(die) -- Fun info: From "besprechen" (to discuss). A meeting, a discussion. Die Besprechung. More informal than Sitzung. "Team-Besprechung" is the standard German office meeting. "Besprechungszimmer" (meeting room) is on every floor.
10. **teach:** Konferenzraum(der) -- Fun info: "Konferenz" (conference) + "Raum" (room). A conference room. Der Konferenzraum. Where decisions are made in German offices. Always has a projector, a whiteboard, and water glasses. Booking one is a competitive sport.
11. **teach:** Tagesordnung(die) -- Fun info: "Tages" (of the day) + "Ordnung" (order). An agenda. Die Tagesordnung. Sacred in German meeting culture. No meeting starts without one. "Gibt es noch Punkte zur Tagesordnung?" (Are there more items on the agenda?) signals the meeting is ending.
12. **teach:** Tagesordnungspunkt(der) -- Fun info: Tagesordnung + "Punkt" (point/item). An agenda item. Der Tagesordnungspunkt. Abbreviated TOP. "TOP 3: Projektbericht" is how German agendas are structured. Each TOP gets a time slot. Germans take this seriously.
13. **teach:** Rahmenprogramm(das) -- Fun info: "Rahmen" (frame) + "Programm." A supporting program, side events. Das Rahmenprogramm. At German conferences, the Rahmenprogramm includes dinners, excursions, and networking. The frame around the main content.
14. **teach:** Redezeit(die) -- Fun info: "Rede" (speech) + "Zeit" (time). Speaking time. Die Redezeit. At German conferences and parliament, Redezeit is strictly enforced. Going over your Redezeit is a serious breach of Ordnung. The Moderator will cut you off.
15. **teach:** Redebeitrag(der) -- Fun info: "Rede" (speech) + "Beitrag" (contribution). A spoken contribution. Der Redebeitrag. Your turn to speak in a meeting or debate. "Vielen Dank fuer Ihren Redebeitrag" (Thank you for your contribution) is the polite acknowledgment.
16. **teach:** demnach -- Fun info: "Dem" (that) + "nach" (after/according to). Consequently, accordingly. An adverb that signals a logical conclusion. "Die Zahlen sind gut, demnach koennen wir fortfahren" (The numbers are good, accordingly we can proceed). Formal and precise.
17. **teach:** daraufhin -- Fun info: "Darauf" (thereupon) + "hin" (toward). As a result, thereupon. "Er praesentierte die Daten, daraufhin aenderte das Team den Plan." Something happens, and daraufhin the consequence follows. More narrative than demnach.
18. **teach:** erstens -- Fun info: "Erst" (first) + "-ens" (adverb suffix). Firstly. "Erstens..., zweitens..., drittens..." is the German numbered argument structure. Essential for structured Vortraege. The -ens ending creates ordinal adverbs.
19. **teach:** ferner -- Fun info: From "fern" (far/distant). Furthermore, moreover. Formal written register. "Ferner moechte ich darauf hinweisen..." (Furthermore, I would like to point out...). Elegant and slightly old-fashioned. Perfect for academic Vortraege.
20. **teach:** schliesslich -- Fun info: From "schliessen" (to close). Finally, ultimately. "Schliesslich kommen wir zum letzten Punkt" (Finally, we come to the last point). Marks the end of an argument chain. Also means "after all": "Schliesslich bin ich der Experte" (After all, I am the expert).
21. **teach:** darstellen -- Fun info: "Dar" (there/forth) + "stellen" (to place). To present, to depict, to represent. Separable: "Ich stelle die Ergebnisse dar." "Die Situation anders darstellen" (to present the situation differently). The formal word for showing data.
22. **teach:** hervorheben -- Fun info: "Hervor" (forth) + "heben" (to lift). To emphasize, to highlight. Separable: "Ich hebe die Kernaussage hervor." Lifting something forth from the background. Strong verb: hervorheben/hob hervor/hervorgehoben.
23. **teach:** aufzeigen -- Fun info: "Auf" (up) + "zeigen" (to show). To point out, to demonstrate. Separable: "Die Studie zeigt Probleme auf." More formal than "zeigen" alone. "Aufzeigen" implies revealing something that was not obvious.
24. **teach:** skizzieren -- Fun info: From Italian "schizzo" (sketch). To sketch, to outline. "Ich skizziere kurz den Ablauf" (I will briefly outline the procedure). Not separable. Used when giving a quick overview without full detail.
25. **teach:** hinweisen -- Fun info: "Hin" (toward) + "weisen" (to point). To point out, to draw attention to. Separable: "Ich weise auf ein Problem hin." "Darauf hinweisen" (to point out) is essential in German presentations and meetings. Polite but direct.
26. **tip:** "Konsekutivsaetze: Result clauses." German has several ways to express consequences. (1) "sodass" (so that, as a result): "Er sprach klar, sodass alle verstanden." (2) "so... dass" (so... that): "Er war so nervoes, dass er stockte." (3) Adverbs: "infolgedessen" (as a result of that), "folglich" (consequently), "demnach" (accordingly). The "sodass" pattern puts the result in a subordinate clause (verb at the end). The adverbs connect independent main clauses. Both express cause and effect. In presentations, these are essential for building logical arguments.

## Lesson 4: "Gesetzt den Fall" (Given the Case)

**Type:** Grammar deep dive
