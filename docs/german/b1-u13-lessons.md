## Lesson 1: "Der rote Stift" (The Red Pen)

**Type:** Story intro
**Mode:** Cinematic. Dr. Yilmaz's VHS office, afternoon sun through venetian blinds, Turkish kilim rug, linguistics books, Goethe bust on shelf. Verumius's CV draft spread out with red corrections everywhere.
**Characters:** Dr. Yilmaz (patient but firm, red pen in hand), Verumius (nervous, staring at the sea of red)
**Step count:** 22
**id:** deu_r13l1
**board:** true
**New vocabulary (18):** Bewerbung(die), Lebenslauf(der), Erfolg(der), Karriere(die), Eindruck(der), Vorbereitung(die), Vorstellung(die), Vorstellungsgespach(das), bereit, korrekt, persoenlich, hoeflich, erstellen, erwarten, reagieren, beachten, anfangs, per
**Grammar:** EXPOSED: Prateritum in Yilmaz's narration ("Ich schrieb hunderte Bewerbungen"), Konjunktiv II in polite phrasing ("Das wurde ich anders formulieren")
**Communicative functions:** Discussing job applications, expressing nervousness, receiving constructive criticism, formal register awareness
**Cumulative vocab after: 1,473**

### Step sequence:
1. **intro:** "Der rote Stift". Goals: Enter the world of German job applications. Learn core application vocabulary. Understand what makes a German Bewerbung different from other countries.
2. **story:** speaker:narrator, mood:warm. Yilmaz's VHS office. Afternoon light filters through venetian blinds. Books everywhere. A small Goethe bust watches from the shelf. Verumius sits across from Dr. Yilmaz, his CV draft between them. Red marks cover nearly every line.
3. **story:** speaker:yilmaz, mood:patient. "Verumius, deine Bewerbung ist... kreativ. Aber in Deutschland ist eine Bewerbung ein offizielles Dokument. Sie muss perfekt sein."
4. **story:** speaker:verumius, mood:nervous. "Aber ich habe alles geschrieben. Mein Name, meine Arbeit..."
5. **story:** speaker:yilmaz, mood:firm. "Ein deutscher Lebenslauf braucht Struktur. Foto, Personaldaten, Ausbildung, Berufserfahrung. Alles korrekt, alles vollstandig."
6. **teach:** Bewerbung(die) -- Fun info: From "bewerben" (to apply). Be- + werben (to advertise/recruit). You are literally "advertising yourself." A German Bewerbung always includes a cover letter (Anschreiben), CV (Lebenslauf), and certificates (Zeugnisse). It is a three-part package.
7. **teach:** Lebenslauf(der) -- Fun info: Literally "life-run" (Leben + Lauf). The story of your life on paper. Germans traditionally include a photo, date of birth, and marital status on their CV. This surprises many non-Germans.
8. **teach:** Erfolg(der) -- Fun info: From "erfolgen" (to result/follow). Success is literally "what follows" from your effort. "Viel Erfolg!" (Much success!) is how Germans wish you good luck before an interview.
9. **teach:** Karriere(die) -- Fun info: Borrowed from French "carriere" (racecourse). Same root as English "career." Germans distinguish between Karriere (ambitious upward path) and Beruf (your profession). Not every Beruf is a Karriere.
10. **teach:** Eindruck(der) -- Fun info: "Ein" (in) + "Druck" (pressure/print). An impression is literally something pressed into you. "Der erste Eindruck zahlt" (The first impression counts) is a universal German saying.
11. **teach:** Vorbereitung(die) -- Fun info: "Vor" (before) + "Bereitung" (preparation). Preparing before the event. Germans are famous for Vorbereitung. A job interview without preparation is unthinkable. The compound tells you: do it BEFORE.
12. **teach:** Vorstellung(die) -- Fun info: "Vor" (before/in front of) + "Stellung" (position). Literally "placing yourself before someone." Means both "introduction" and "imagination." "Hast du eine Vorstellung?" can mean "Do you have an idea?" or "Do you have an appointment?"
13. **teach:** Vorstellungsgesprach(das) -- Fun info: Vorstellung + Gesprach (conversation). A "getting-to-know-you conversation." The formal German word for job interview. Das Vorstellungsgesprach is always neuter because Gesprach is the last element.
14. **teach:** bereit -- Fun info: "Ready." "Bist du bereit?" (Are you ready?) is what coaches, teachers, and mentors ask before the big moment. Related to "bereiten" (to prepare). Ready means you have prepared.
15. **teach:** korrekt -- Fun info: Same as English "correct." From Latin "correctus." In German, korrekt also means "fair/decent" in slang: "Er ist ein korrekter Typ" (He is a decent guy). Double meaning: accurate AND upstanding.
16. **teach:** persoenlich -- Fun info: From Person + -lich. "Personal." Your persoenliche Daten (personal data) go on every German form. In job interviews, persoenliche Fragen about family or religion are actually illegal to ask.
17. **teach:** hoeflich -- Fun info: From "Hof" (court). Being hoeflich originally meant behaving like someone at a royal court. Courtly manners became "polite." Same etymology as English "courteous" from "court."
18. **teach:** erstellen -- Fun info: "Er-" (to make/achieve) + "stellen" (to place). To create or produce something formal. You "erstellen" a document, report, or CV. More formal than "machen." The professional word for creation.
19. **teach:** erwarten -- Fun info: "Er-" (out) + "warten" (to wait). To expect is to "wait out" for something. "Was erwarten Sie von diesem Job?" is a classic interview question.
20. **teach:** reagieren -- Fun info: From Latin "re-agere" (to act back). Same word in English, French, and German. "Wie reagieren Sie auf Kritik?" (How do you react to criticism?) is another classic interview question.
21. **teach:** beachten -- Fun info: "Be-" (upon) + "achten" (to regard/respect). To pay attention to, to observe. "Bitte beachten Sie die Regeln" (Please observe the rules). Achtung + be- prefix = active attention.
22. **teach:** anfangs -- Fun info: "At first/initially." From Anfang (beginning). The -s ending turns it into an adverb. "Anfangs war ich nervoes" (At first I was nervous). Perfect for telling the story of how you started somewhere.
23. **teach:** per -- Fun info: From Latin "per" (through/by means of). "Per E-Mail" (by email), "per Post" (by mail). Short, efficient, and used in formal writing. German borrowed it directly from Latin and never let go.
24. **mc:** Core vocabulary. q: "Verumius needs to write a document about his education and work experience. What is it called?" opts: [Lebenslauf, Bewerbung, Vorstellung, Eindruck] ans: Lebenslauf. hint: "This is the document that runs through your life story."
25. **mc:** Meaning check. q: "Dr. Yilmaz says: 'Der erste Eindruck zahlt.' What does Eindruck mean?" opts: [impression, success, preparation, application] ans: impression. hint: "Something that gets 'pressed into' someone's mind."
26. **fb:** Complete the sentence. s: "Verumius muss seine {1} gut vorbereiten." a: ["Bewerbung"] opts: [Bewerbung, Karriere, Erfolg, Eindruck] hint: "The three-part package you send when applying for a job in Germany."

## Lesson 2: "Berufe und Qualifikationen" (Professions and Qualifications)

**Type:** Vocab teach
**Mode:** Bubble. No scene. Pure vocabulary.
**Characters:** None (pure vocabulary)
**Step count:** 24
**id:** deu_r13l2
**board:** true
**New vocabulary (20):** Beruf -- already known from A1. REPLACING. Actual 20: Qualifikation(die), Fachigkeit(die), Fortbildung(die), Weiterbildung(die), Zertifikat(das), Diplom(das), Abitur(das), Abschluss(der), Lehre(die), Lehrling(der), Lehrstelle(die), Praktikant(der), Praktikantin(die), Ingenieur(der), Architekt(der), Architektin(die), Mechaniker(der), Mechanikerin(die), beruflich, berufstaetig
**Grammar:** EXPOSED: Prateritum via example sentences ("Ich studierte drei Jahre in Berlin"), gendered profession pairs (Architekt/Architektin)
**Communicative functions:** Describing professional qualifications, talking about education, identifying professions
**Cumulative vocab after: 1,493**

### Step sequence:
1. **intro:** "Berufe und Qualifikationen." Goals: Learn words for professions and qualifications. Understand the German education and training system. Master masculine/feminine profession pairs.
2. **teach:** Qualifikation(die) -- Fun info: Same Latin root as English "qualification." Die Qualifikation is what makes you qualified. German job ads list Qualifikationen more precisely than most countries. They expect you to prove every single one.
3. **teach:** Faehigkeit(die) -- Fun info: From "faehig" (capable) + "-keit" (noun suffix). An ability or skill. "Soziale Faehigkeiten" (social skills) appear on every German job listing. The -keit ending always makes feminine nouns.
4. **teach:** Fortbildung(die) -- Fun info: "Fort" (further/onward) + "Bildung" (education). Continuing professional education. Germans take Fortbildung seriously. Many employers pay for it. It shows you never stop learning.
5. **teach:** Weiterbildung(die) -- Fun info: "Weiter" (further) + "Bildung" (education). Nearly synonymous with Fortbildung but broader. Weiterbildung can be personal growth too, not just professional. Both are die (feminine) because of Bildung.
6. **teach:** Zertifikat(das) -- Fun info: From Latin "certificare" (to make certain). Das Zertifikat is neuter. Germans love certificates. You can get a Zertifikat for almost anything: language level, first aid, fire safety, even coffee brewing.
7. **teach:** Diplom(das) -- Fun info: From Greek "diploma" (folded paper). Das Diplom was the traditional German university degree before the Bologna reform. Some older Germans still proudly use "Diplom-Ingenieur" as a title.
8. **teach:** Abitur(das) -- Fun info: From Latin "abire" (to go away). Das Abitur is the German high school leaving exam. Without it, university is nearly impossible. It takes 12-13 years of schooling to reach. The equivalent of A-levels or SATs but harder.
9. **teach:** Abschluss(der) -- Fun info: "Ab" (off/away) + "Schluss" (closing). A completion, a degree. "Welchen Abschluss haben Sie?" (What degree do you have?) is a standard interview question. The word implies you closed a chapter of your education.
10. **teach:** Lehre(die) -- Fun info: "Teaching/apprenticeship." From "lehren" (to teach). Die Lehre is Germany's famous dual education system: you work at a company AND attend school. 50% of Germans do a Lehre instead of university.
11. **teach:** Lehrling(der) -- Fun info: "Lehre" + "-ling" (person suffix). An apprentice. The -ling suffix often has a diminutive or junior quality: Lehrling (apprentice), Fruehling (spring, the "young" season). Always masculine grammatically.
12. **teach:** Lehrstelle(die) -- Fun info: "Lehre" + "Stelle" (position). An apprenticeship position. Finding a Lehrstelle is a big deal for German teenagers. Companies compete for good Lehrlinge, and Lehrlinge compete for good Lehrstellen.
13. **teach:** Praktikant(der) -- Fun info: From "Praktikum" (internship) + "-ant" (person suffix). A male intern. The -ant ending comes from Latin and always signals a person doing something. Always masculine.
14. **teach:** Praktikantin(die) -- Fun info: Praktikant + "-in" (feminine suffix). A female intern. German always has masculine/feminine pairs for professions. The -in suffix is the universal feminine marker. Always die.
15. **teach:** Ingenieur(der) -- Fun info: From French "ingenieur," from Latin "ingenium" (talent/cleverness). Germany is famous for engineering. "Made in Germany" was originally a British warning label. Germans turned it into a badge of pride. Der Ingenieur is a title of respect.
16. **teach:** Architekt(der) -- Fun info: From Greek "architekton" (master builder). Der Architekt. Germany's Bauhaus movement revolutionized architecture worldwide. German architects are required to have a university degree and state registration.
17. **teach:** Architektin(die) -- Fun info: Architekt + "-in." Die Architektin. The -in suffix works on every profession: Lehrer/Lehrerin, Arzt/Aerztin, Architekt/Architektin. Once you know the pattern, you can feminize any profession.
18. **teach:** Mechaniker(der) -- Fun info: From Greek "mechanikos." Der Mechaniker. In Germany, a Mechaniker must complete a 3.5-year apprenticeship. German car mechanics are among the best trained in the world because of this system.
19. **teach:** Mechanikerin(die) -- Fun info: Mechaniker + "-in." Die Mechanikerin. More and more women enter traditionally male professions in Germany. The language always had the feminine form ready.
20. **teach:** beruflich -- Fun info: "Beruf" (profession) + "-lich" (adjective suffix). Professional, work-related. "Beruflich laeuft es gut" (Professionally, things are going well). The -lich suffix turns nouns into adjectives, like English "-ly."
21. **teach:** berufstaetig -- Fun info: "Beruf" + "taetig" (active/engaged). Employed, working. "Sind Sie berufstaetig?" (Are you employed?) is a form question. Being berufstaetig is a core part of German identity.
22. **tip:** "The German -in pattern." Every masculine profession has a feminine form: add -in. Lehrer/Lehrerin, Ingenieur/Ingenieurin, Architekt/Architektin. The plural feminizes too: Lehrerinnen, Architektinnen. Modern German increasingly uses gender-neutral forms: "Architekt*innen" or "Ingenieur:innen." You will see all variants.
23. **mc:** Profession pairs. q: "A female engineer in German is..." opts: [Ingenieurin, Ingenieur, Architektin, Mechanikerin] ans: Ingenieurin. hint: "Take the masculine profession word for engineer and add the feminine suffix."
24. **match:** Education vocabulary. pairs: [{trg:"Abitur",src:"high school exam"},{trg:"Lehre",src:"apprenticeship"},{trg:"Diplom",src:"university degree"},{trg:"Zertifikat",src:"certificate"}]
25. **fb:** Complete the sentence. s: "Nach dem Studium hat sie einen {1} in Informatik." a: ["Abschluss"] opts: [Abschluss, Anfang, Lehrling, Praktikant] hint: "The word for completing your education, literally a 'closing off.'"
26. **mc:** Vocab check. q: "Weiterbildung means..." opts: [continuing education, university degree, apprenticeship, job interview] ans: continuing education. hint: "Weiter means further. Bildung means education. Put them together."

## Lesson 3: "Ich studierte in Istanbul" (I Studied in Istanbul)

**Type:** Story development + grammar intro
**Mode:** Bubble. Same office, closer on the desk. Lebenslauf template on screen. Yilmaz coaching Verumius through CV narrative writing.
**Characters:** Dr. Yilmaz (encouraging, nodding), Verumius (concentrating on formal writing)
**Step count:** 24
**id:** deu_r13l3
**board:** true
**New vocabulary (20):** Angabe(die), Personalie(die), Personenstand(der), Personal(das), Empfehlung(die), Bericht(der), Bereich(der), Beitrag(der), Beschaeftigung(die), Bedingung(die), Bedarf(der), Anspruch(der), Werk(das), erfolgreich, erforderlich, bestimmt, komplett, offiziell, angeben, beschaeftigen
**Grammar:** TAUGHT: #3 Prateritum expanded. Verb table for strong verbs (ging/kam/schrieb/sprach/gab/nahm/fand/stand/las). Tip card explaining written vs. spoken past tense. EXPOSED: #4 Konjunktiv II in Yilmaz's polite suggestions.
**Communicative functions:** Writing formal narratives, describing work history, using past tense in professional context
**Cumulative vocab after: 1,513**

### Step sequence:
1. **intro:** "Ich studierte in Istanbul." Goals: Learn the Prateritum (narrative past tense). Write your work history in formal German. Master professional documentation vocabulary.
2. **story:** speaker:yilmaz, mood:encouraging. "Fuer den Lebenslauf brauchst du das Prateritum. Nicht 'Ich habe studiert,' sondern 'Ich studierte.' Das klingt professioneller."
3. **story:** speaker:verumius, mood:thinking. "Also: 'Ich studierte Linguistik in Istanbul. Ich arbeitete drei Jahre als Uebersetzer.' So?"
4. **story:** speaker:yilmaz, mood:pleased. "Genau! Und bei starken Verben aendert sich der Vokal: 'Ich schrieb,' nicht 'Ich schreibte.' Das musst du lernen."
5. **teach:** Angabe(die) -- Fun info: "An" (at/on) + "Gabe" (giving). Information you give about yourself. "Persoenliche Angaben" is the first section on every German CV. Die Angabe is always feminine because of the -e ending from Gabe.
6. **teach:** Personalie(die) -- Fun info: From "Person" + Latin suffix. Personal data/details. "Personalien aufnehmen" is what police do when they take down your details. On a CV, your Personalien include name, address, date of birth.
7. **teach:** Personenstand(der) -- Fun info: "Personen" + "Stand" (status). Marital status. Ledig (single), verheiratet (married). German CVs traditionally include this. Many modern career advisors now recommend leaving it out.
8. **teach:** Personal(das) -- Fun info: From Latin "personalis." Staff, personnel. "Die Personalabteilung" (HR department) handles your Bewerbung. Das Personal is neuter and collective. One word for all the people who work somewhere.
9. **teach:** Empfehlung(die) -- Fun info: From "empfehlen" (to recommend). A recommendation. "Empfehlungsschreiben" (letter of recommendation) is a standard part of German job applications. Germans value written Empfehlungen highly.
10. **teach:** Bericht(der) -- Fun info: From "berichten" (to report). A report or account. "Arbeitsbericht" (work report), "Jahresbericht" (annual report). Der Bericht is always masculine. Germans document everything.
11. **teach:** Bereich(der) -- Fun info: "Be-" + "Reich" (realm/area). A field, area, or domain. "In welchem Bereich arbeiten Sie?" (In which field do you work?) is a networking question. Der Bereich covers everything from "Fachbereich" (department) to "Lebensbereich" (area of life).
12. **teach:** Beitrag(der) -- Fun info: "Bei-" (alongside) + "Trag" (carrying). A contribution. "Einen Beitrag leisten" (to make a contribution) is formal language for doing your part. Also means "fee" in contexts like "Mitgliedsbeitrag" (membership fee).
13. **teach:** Beschaeftigung(die) -- Fun info: From "beschaeftigen" (to employ/occupy). Employment or occupation. "Beschaeftigungsverhaeltnis" (employment relationship) is a word that only German bureaucracy could produce. Die Beschaeftigung is feminine because of -ung.
14. **teach:** Bedingung(die) -- Fun info: From "bedingen" (to require/condition). A condition or requirement. "Arbeitsbedingungen" (working conditions) matter in every job. The -ung ending always makes feminine nouns.
15. **teach:** Bedarf(der) -- Fun info: From "beduerfen" (to require). Need or demand. "Bei Bedarf" (if needed/on demand) appears in contracts. Der Bedarf is always masculine. The professional way to say "if you need it."
16. **teach:** Anspruch(der) -- Fun info: "An" + "Spruch" (saying/claim). A claim or entitlement. "Anspruch auf Urlaub" (entitlement to vacation). German workers have strong Ansprueche by law: minimum 20 vacation days, health insurance, parental leave.
17. **teach:** Werk(das) -- Fun info: "Work" in the sense of a created work or factory. "Das Werk von Goethe" (the works of Goethe), "Automobilwerk" (car factory). Cognate with English "work." Das Werk is neuter. Different from "Arbeit" (labor).
18. **teach:** erfolgreich -- Fun info: "Erfolg" (success) + "reich" (rich). Literally "rich in success." The -reich suffix means "rich in" or "full of." "Eine erfolgreiche Bewerbung" is what you want.
19. **teach:** erforderlich -- Fun info: From "erfordern" (to require). Required, necessary. "Erforderliche Qualifikationen" appears in every job listing. More formal than "noetig" (needed).
20. **teach:** bestimmt -- Fun info: "Determined/certain/specific." From "bestimmen" (to determine). "Ein bestimmter Bereich" (a specific area). Also an adverb meaning "definitely": "Das klappt bestimmt!" (That will definitely work!).
21. **teach:** komplett -- Fun info: From French "complet," from Latin "completus." Complete, entire. "Ihre Bewerbung ist komplett" (Your application is complete) is what you want to hear from HR.
22. **teach:** offiziell -- Fun info: From French "officiel," from Latin "officium." Official. "Ein offizielles Dokument" (an official document). Pronounced "off-ih-tsee-ELL" with stress on the last syllable. German borrowed it with French pronunciation.
23. **teach:** angeben -- Fun info: "An" + "geben" (to give). To state, indicate, or specify. "Bitte geben Sie Ihre Adresse an" (Please state your address). Separable verb: "Ich gebe meine Daten an." Also colloquially means "to show off."
24. **teach:** beschaeftigen -- Fun info: From "Schaeft" (shaft/handle, i.e., being busy with tools). To employ or to occupy oneself. "Die Firma beschaeftigt 200 Mitarbeiter" (The company employs 200 workers). Reflexive: "Ich beschaeftige mich mit..." (I occupy myself with...).
25. **tip:** "Prateritum: The narrative past." Spoken German mostly uses Perfekt (Ich habe geschrieben). But written German, especially formal texts like CVs, uses Prateritum (Ich schrieb). Strong verbs change their vowel: schreiben/schrieb, gehen/ging, kommen/kam, sprechen/sprach. Weak verbs add -te: arbeiten/arbeitete, studieren/studierte. For sein, haben, and modals, even spoken German uses Prateritum: war, hatte, konnte, musste.

## Lesson 4: "Ich wuerde mich freuen" (I Would Be Pleased)

**Type:** Grammar deep dive
**Mode:** Bubble. Grammar instruction with CV/interview context.
**Characters:** None (grammar instruction with Yilmaz examples)
**Step count:** 26
**id:** deu_r13l4
**board:** true
**New vocabulary (20):** Aushilfe(die), Teilzeit(die), Vollzeit(die), Einkommen(das), Angestellte(der), Arbeiter(der), Arbeiterin(die), Arbeitsstelle(die), Arbeitserlaubnis(die), Arbeitslosigkeit(die), Lehrerin(die), Meister(der), Profi(der), Fachmann(der), Fachfrau(die), Fachleute, absolut, flexibel, ideal, individuell
**Grammar:** TAUGHT: #4 Konjunktiv II expanded (wuerde + Infinitiv, Wunschsaetze: "Wenn ich doch nur..."). Verb table for wuerde conjugation. Tip card for polite requests and wish sentences. PRACTICED: #3 Prateritum (3+ quiz steps recycled).
**Communicative functions:** Making polite requests, expressing wishes, using formal register in applications, describing employment types
**Cumulative vocab after: 1,533**

### Step sequence:
1. **intro:** "Ich wuerde mich freuen." Goals: Master the Konjunktiv II with wuerde + Infinitiv. Express polite wishes and requests. Write formal application sentences.
2. **teach:** Aushilfe(die) -- Fun info: "Aus" (out) + "Hilfe" (help). Temporary help, a casual worker. "Aushilfsjob" is a part-time or temporary job. Die Aushilfe is feminine. Many students work as Aushilfe alongside their studies.
3. **teach:** Teilzeit(die) -- Fun info: "Teil" (part) + "Zeit" (time). Part-time. "Teilzeitarbeit" (part-time work). Die Teilzeit is feminine. In Germany, parents have a legal right to switch to Teilzeit after having children.
4. **teach:** Vollzeit(die) -- Fun info: "Voll" (full) + "Zeit" (time). Full-time. Typically 38-40 hours per week in Germany. "Vollzeitstelle" (full-time position). The contrast pair: Teilzeit vs. Vollzeit appears in every job listing.
5. **teach:** Einkommen(das) -- Fun info: "Ein" (in) + "kommen" (to come). Income, literally what "comes in." Das Einkommen is neuter. Germans rarely discuss their Einkommen openly. Talking about salary is considered impolite in many social situations.
6. **teach:** Angestellte(der) -- Fun info: From "anstellen" (to employ). An employee (white-collar). Der/die Angestellte changes gender with the article. Like "Deutsche," it declines as an adjective. A quirk of German nouns derived from past participles.
7. **teach:** Arbeiter(der) -- Fun info: "Arbeit" (work) + "-er" (person). A worker (traditionally blue-collar). Der Arbeiter. The distinction between Arbeiter and Angestellte was legally important in Germany until 2005 for insurance purposes.
8. **teach:** Arbeiterin(die) -- Fun info: Arbeiter + "-in." A female worker. Die Arbeiterin. Same pattern as always: masculine profession + -in = feminine. The German language never leaves you without a feminine form.

7. **teach:** Kennzeichen(das) -- Fun info: "Kenn" (know) + "Zeichen" (sign). A characteristic, a distinguishing mark. Das Kennzeichen. Also means "license plate": your car's Kennzeichen identifies it. On forms: "Besondere Kennzeichen" (special characteristics).
8. **teach:** Matura(die) -- Fun info: From Latin "maturus" (ripe/mature). Die Matura is the Austrian and Swiss equivalent of the German Abitur. Same exam, different name. Verumius might encounter both terms when applying across German-speaking countries.

10. **teach:** Mehrwertsteuer(die) -- Fun info: "Mehr" (more) + "Wert" (value) + "Steuer" (tax). Value-added tax (VAT). Die Mehrwertsteuer is currently 19% in Germany. The longest everyday compound in German tax vocabulary. Everyone knows the abbreviation: MwSt.
11. **teach:** Profisportler(der) -- Fun info: "Profi" + "Sportler" (athlete). A professional athlete. Der Profisportler. Germany's Bundesliga (football), Handball-Bundesliga, and Formula 1 produce many Profisportler. Sports vocabulary meets professional vocabulary.
12. **teach:** Profisportlerin(die) -- Fun info: Profisportler + "-in." Die Profisportlerin. German women's football has grown enormously. The national team has won multiple European Championships.
13. **teach:** abhaengig -- Fun info: "Ab" (off) + "haengig" (hanging). Dependent, literally "hanging from." "Finanziell abhaengig" (financially dependent). Also used for addiction: "abhaengig von Kaffee" (dependent on coffee). The opposite: "unabhaengig" (independent).
14. **teach:** abwesend -- Fun info: "Ab" (away) + "wesend" (being). Absent. "Herr Mueller ist heute abwesend" (Mr. Mueller is absent today). The opposite of anwesend. Ab- (away from) vs. an- (at/present).

16. **teach:** angenehm -- Fun info: From "annehmen" (to take on/accept). Pleasant, agreeable. "Angenehm!" is what Germans say when introduced. Literally "pleasant!" Short for "Es ist mir angenehm, Sie kennenzulernen" (It is pleasant to meet you).

20. **teach:** anstellen -- Fun info: "An" (at) + "stellen" (to place). To hire/employ. "Wir stellen neue Mitarbeiter an" (We are hiring new employees). Separable. Same meaning as "einstellen." Also colloquial: "Was stellst du an?" (What are you up to/doing?).
21. **teach:** anstrengen -- Fun info: "An" (on) + "strengen" (to tighten). To exhaust, to make an effort. "Die Arbeit strengt mich an" (The work exhausts me). Reflexive: "Ich strenge mich an" (I make an effort). The more you tighten yourself, the harder you work.

10. **teach:** anhaben -- Fun info: "An" (on) + "haben" (to have). To wear, to have on. "Was hast du an?" (What are you wearing?). Separable. Colloquial. More casual than "tragen" (to wear/carry). Perfect for Lukas's informal register.
11. **teach:** ansprechen -- Fun info: "An" (at) + "sprechen" (to speak). To address someone, to bring up a topic. "Darf ich Sie ansprechen?" (May I address you?). Separable. "Das Thema ansprechen" (to bring up the topic). Two meanings: talking TO and talking ABOUT.
12. **teach:** anwenden -- Fun info: "An" (on) + "wenden" (to turn). To apply, to use. "Kenntnisse anwenden" (to apply knowledge). Separable. What you learned in theory, you anwenden in practice. "Anwendung" (application) is the noun form.
13. **teach:** anzeigen -- Fun info: "An" (at) + "zeigen" (to show). To display, to report, to advertise. "Eine Stelle anzeigen" (to advertise a position). Also: "Jemanden anzeigen" (to report someone to police). "Anzeige" is both an ad and a police report.
14. **teach:** begleiten -- Fun info: "Be-" + "gleiten" (to glide/accompany). To accompany. "Darf ich Sie begleiten?" (May I accompany you?). The be- prefix makes it transitive. Gleiten alone means to glide. Together: to glide alongside someone.
15. **teach:** bekanntgeben -- Fun info: "Bekannt" (known) + "geben" (to give). To announce, to make known. "Die Firma gibt die Ergebnisse bekannt" (The company announces the results). Separable. Making something known to everyone.
16. **teach:** benoetigen -- Fun info: "Be-" + "noetigen" (to compel). To require, to need. More formal than "brauchen." "Wir benoetigen Ihre Unterlagen" (We require your documents). Standard in business correspondence. The be- prefix makes it transitive.
17. **teach:** besorgen -- Fun info: "Be-" + "sorgen" (to care/worry). To obtain, to get. "Koennen Sie mir die Unterlagen besorgen?" (Can you get me the documents?). Also: "besorgt sein" (to be worried). The meaning depends on whether it is reflexive.
18. **teach:** beweisen -- Fun info: "Be-" + "weisen" (to show/point). To prove. "Das beweist seine Qualifikation" (That proves his qualification). Strong verb: beweisen/bewies/bewiesen. In job contexts: proving your worth.
19. **teach:** erfordern -- Fun info: "Er-" + "fordern" (to demand). To require, to demand. "Die Stelle erfordert Erfahrung" (The position requires experience). Not separable. More formal than "brauchen." The official word in job listings.
20. **teach:** erkundigen -- Fun info: "Er-" + "kundigen" (related to "Kunde" = knowledge/customer). To inquire. Always reflexive: "Ich erkundige mich nach der Stelle" (I inquire about the position). Formal and polite. "Sich erkundigen" is what you do before applying.
21. **teach:** funktionieren -- Fun info: From French "fonctionner," from Latin "functio." To function, to work. "Das funktioniert!" (That works!). One of the most-used words in everyday German. Pronounced "funk-tsio-NEE-ren." Same concept across languages.
22. **teach:** handeln -- Fun info: "To act, to trade, to negotiate." "Es handelt sich um..." (It concerns...) is formal German for introducing a topic. "Handeln" covers action, commerce, and negotiation. Der Handel = trade. Die Handlung = plot/action.
23. **teach:** melden -- Fun info: "To report, to register." "Sich melden" (to get in touch, to report). "Bitte melden Sie sich am Empfang" (Please report to reception). Also: "Sich bei der Polizei melden" (to report to the police). Essential for German bureaucracy.
24. **teach:** Absenderin(die) -- Fun info: "Ab" (from) + "Senderin" (female sender). The female sender. Die Absenderin. The person who sends a letter or email. "Absender/Absenderin" appears on every German letter and official email.
25. **teach:** meinetwegen -- Fun info: "Meinet" (my) + "wegen" (because of). "For my sake" or "as far as I am concerned" or colloquially "fine by me." "Meinetwegen kannst du gehen" (For all I care, you can go). Can be generous or dismissive depending on tone.
26. **teach:** halbtags -- Fun info: "Halb" (half) + "tags" (during the day). Half-day, part-time (mornings or afternoons only). "Halbtags arbeiten" (to work half-days). Different from Teilzeit: halbtags is specifically half a day, Teilzeit can be any reduced hours.
27. **teach:** perfekt -- Fun info: From Latin "perfectus" (completed). Perfect. Same word in English and German. "Dein Lebenslauf ist perfekt!" Also the name of the German past tense "Perfekt" (Ich habe gemacht). Double meaning: the grammar tense and the adjective.
