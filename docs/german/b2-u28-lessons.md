## Lesson 1: "Sonntagsbesuch" (Sunday Visit)

**Type:** Story intro
**Mode:** Cinematic. Hildi's living room on Sunday afternoon. Heinrich in his armchair, blanket on lap. Hildi serving coffee and Kuchen. Lace curtains, afternoon light. A room unchanged for 40 years.
**Characters:** Opa Heinrich (delighted, about to tell a story), Hildi (fond, serving), Verumius (happy, notebook ready)
**Step count:** 22
**id:** deu_r28l1
**board:** true
**New vocabulary (18):** Sprichwort(das)[V1], Redewendung(die)[V1], Weisheit(die)[V1], Redensart(die), Maerchen(das)[V1], Erzaehlung(die), Metapher(die), Gleichnis(das), Dichter(der), Dichterin(die), literarisch, poetisch, zeitlos, volkstümlich, ueberliefert, erzaehlen, dichten, zitieren
**Grammar:** EXPOSED: Konjunktiv I in Heinrich's indirect speech ("Er sagte, die Backstube sei sein Koenigreich"). Natural exposure only.
**Communicative functions:** Discussing proverbs and oral tradition, naming literary forms, describing cultural wisdom, introducing storytelling vocabulary
**Cumulative vocab after: ~3,386**

### Step sequence:
1. **intro:** "Sonntagsbesuch." Goals: Enter the world of German proverbs and oral tradition. Learn core literary vocabulary. Understand why Sprichwoerter matter in German culture.
2. **story:** speaker:narrator, mood:warm. Sunday afternoon at Hildi's. The room smells of coffee and old wood. Heinrich sits in his armchair, blanket on his lap. Hildi places Kuchen on the table. Afternoon light filters through lace curtains. Verumius arrives with a notebook. He has learned to write things down.
3. **story:** speaker:heinrich, mood:delighted. "Ah, Verumius! Setz dich. Heute erzaehle ich dir etwas Wichtiges. Kein Lehrbuch. Keine Grammatik. Sprichwoerter."
4. **story:** speaker:verumius, mood:happy. "Sprichwoerter? Wie 'Uebung macht den Meister'?"
5. **story:** speaker:heinrich, mood:animated. "Genau! Aber ein Sprichwort ist mehr als Worte. Es ist Weisheit, die Generationen ueberdauert hat. Jedes hat eine Geschichte."
6. **story:** speaker:hildi, mood:fond. "Er erzaehlt sie jedes Mal ein bisschen anders. Aber unterbrich ihn nicht."
7. **teach:** Sprichwort(das)[V1] -- Fun info: "Sprich" (speak) + "Wort" (word). A proverb, a saying. Das Sprichwort. Literally a "speak-word." German has thousands of Sprichwoerter, many dating back centuries. "Morgenstund hat Gold im Mund" is one of the oldest.
8. **teach:** Redewendung(die)[V1] -- Fun info: "Rede" (speech) + "Wendung" (turn). An idiom, a figure of speech. Die Redewendung. Literally a "speech-turn." Different from a Sprichwort: a Redewendung is a fixed phrase used in sentences, while a Sprichwort is a complete sentence of wisdom.
9. **teach:** Weisheit(die)[V1] -- Fun info: From "weise" (wise) + "-heit" (state of being). Wisdom. Die Weisheit. "Alte Weisheit" (ancient wisdom) is what Sprichwoerter carry. The -heit suffix always creates feminine abstract nouns. "Weisheitszahn" (wisdom tooth) is the bonus compound.
10. **teach:** Redensart(die) -- Fun info: "Reden" (to speak) + "Art" (kind/manner). A manner of speaking, an expression. Die Redensart. Broader than Redewendung. Any characteristic way of expressing something. "Das ist so eine Redensart" (That is just a saying) dismisses literal interpretation.
11. **teach:** Maerchen(das)[V1] -- Fun info: From "Maer" (old word for story/news) + "-chen" (diminutive). A fairy tale. Das Maerchen. The Brothers Grimm collected German Maerchen and made them world-famous. The diminutive suffix makes it neuter and gives it a gentle quality.
12. **teach:** Erzaehlung(die) -- Fun info: From "erzaehlen" (to tell/narrate). A narrative, a story. Die Erzaehlung. More literary than "Geschichte" (story/history). A short literary work. "Kurzerzaehlung" = short story. The formal word for narrative fiction.
13. **teach:** Metapher(die) -- Fun info: From Greek "metaphora" (transfer). A metaphor. Die Metapher. Feminine in German. "Das Leben ist eine Reise" is a classic Metapher. German literary analysis loves identifying Metaphern in texts.
14. **teach:** Gleichnis(das) -- Fun info: From "gleich" (same/equal) + "-nis" (abstract noun suffix). A parable, an allegory. Das Gleichnis. Biblical parables are "Gleichnisse." Heinrich's bakery stories are Gleichnisse: simple tales carrying deeper meaning.
15. **teach:** Dichter(der) -- Fun info: From "dichten" (to compose poetry/write). A poet, a literary writer. Der Dichter. Goethe is "der groesste deutsche Dichter" (the greatest German poet). The word covers poets and literary authors. "Dichter und Denker" (poets and thinkers) is how Germans describe their cultural tradition.
16. **teach:** Dichterin(die) -- Fun info: Dichter + "-in." Die Dichterin. A female poet/writer. Annette von Droste-Huelshoff, Else Lasker-Schueler, Ingeborg Bachmann: Germany's great Dichterinnen deserve the title.
17. **teach:** literarisch -- Fun info: From Latin "litterarius." Literary. "Literarische Werke" (literary works). "Ein literarischer Abend" (a literary evening) is a German cultural tradition: readings, wine, and discussion.
18. **teach:** poetisch -- Fun info: From Greek "poietikos" (creative). Poetic. "Poetische Sprache" (poetic language) uses imagery, rhythm, and metaphor. "Das klingt sehr poetisch" is both a compliment and sometimes gentle teasing.
19. **teach:** zeitlos -- Fun info: "Zeit" (time) + "-los" (without). Timeless. "Zeitlose Weisheit" (timeless wisdom) is what good Sprichwoerter carry. They outlive the people who coined them. "-los" always means "without": zeitlos, endlos, grenzenlos.
20. **teach:** volkstümlich -- Fun info: "Volk" (people/folk) + "tuemlich" (characteristic of). Folk, popular, traditional. "Volkstümliche Sprichwoerter" (folk proverbs) come from ordinary people, not scholars. Volkstümlich carries warmth and earthiness.
21. **teach:** ueberliefert -- Fun info: "Ueber" (over) + "liefert" (delivered). Handed down, transmitted through generations. Past participle of "ueberliefern." "Muendlich ueberliefert" (orally transmitted) describes how proverbs survived before writing.
22. **teach:** erzaehlen -- Fun info: From old German "irzellen" (to recount). To tell, to narrate. "Heinrich erzaehlt gern Geschichten." Not separable. Different from "sagen" (to say): erzaehlen implies a story, sagen implies a statement.
23. **teach:** dichten -- Fun info: From Latin "dictare" (to dictate). To compose poetry, to write literary works. "Goethe dichtete in Weimar." Also colloquially: "Das ist gedichtet!" (That is made up!). Creating with words.
24. **teach:** zitieren -- Fun info: From Latin "citare" (to summon). To quote. "Ich zitiere Heinrich: 'Morgenstund hat Gold im Mund.'" Not separable. "Ein Zitat" (a quote) is the noun. Germans love zitieren in academic and professional contexts.
25. **mc:** Core vocabulary. q: "Heinrich tells Verumius stories that carry wisdom passed down through generations. What is the German word for a proverb?" opts: [Sprichwort, Redewendung, Maerchen, Gleichnis] ans: Sprichwort. hint: "A 'speak-word.' A complete sentence of folk wisdom that has survived generations."
26. **fb:** Complete the sentence. s: "Diese alte {1} hat Generationen ueberdauert." a: ["Weisheit"] opts: [Weisheit, Erzaehlung, Metapher, Redensart] hint: "The German word for wisdom. What proverbs carry from generation to generation."
27. **mc:** Meaning check. q: "A Gleichnis is best described as..." opts: [a parable or allegory with deeper meaning, a grammatical exercise, a type of poem, a newspaper article] ans: a parable or allegory with deeper meaning. hint: "From 'gleich' (equal/same). A simple story that stands for something deeper."

## Lesson 2: "Dichtung und Wahrheit" (Poetry and Truth)

**Type:** Vocab teach
**Mode:** Bubble. No scene. Pure vocabulary.
**Characters:** None (pure vocabulary)
**Step count:** 24
**id:** deu_r28l2
**board:** true
**New vocabulary (20):** Gedicht(das), Vers(der), Strophe(die), Reim(der), Lyrik(die), Prosa(die), Roman(der), Kurzgeschichte(die), Novelle(die), Drama(das), Fabel(die), Gattung(die), Werk(das), Handlung(die), Hauptfigur(die), Schauplatz(der), Epoche(die), episch, lyrisch, dichterisch
**Grammar:** EXPOSED: Konjunktiv I in example sentences ("Der Dichter schrieb, die Nacht sei wie ein Traum"). Natural exposure through teach card examples.
**Communicative functions:** Naming literary genres and forms, describing literary elements, discussing German literary tradition
**Cumulative vocab after: ~3,406**

### Step sequence:
1. **intro:** "Dichtung und Wahrheit." Goals: Learn words for literary genres and forms. Master the vocabulary of German literary tradition. Describe poems, stories, and novels in German.
2. **teach:** Gedicht(das) -- Fun info: From "dichten" (to compose). A poem. Das Gedicht. Neuter because of the Ge- prefix. "Ein Gedicht aufsagen" (to recite a poem) is a German school tradition. Schiller's "Die Buergschaft" is the classic recitation piece.
3. **teach:** Vers(der) -- Fun info: From Latin "versus" (a turning, a line). A verse, a line of poetry. Der Vers. "Der erste Vers" (the first line). In German poetry, each line is a Vers. "Freie Verse" (free verse) has no rhyme or fixed meter.
4. **teach:** Strophe(die) -- Fun info: From Greek "strophe" (turning). A stanza. Die Strophe. A group of Verse forming a unit. "Die erste Strophe" (the first stanza). German poetry analysis always counts Strophen and Verse.
5. **teach:** Reim(der) -- Fun info: From Old French "rime." A rhyme. Der Reim. "Sich einen Reim auf etwas machen" (to make a rhyme out of something) means to make sense of something. German children learn Reime (nursery rhymes) early.
6. **teach:** Lyrik(die) -- Fun info: From Greek "lyrikos" (of the lyre). Lyric poetry. Die Lyrik. One of the three Gattungen (genres) of literature. Lyrik is personal, emotional, rhythmic. German Lyrik from Goethe to Celan is world-renowned.
7. **teach:** Prosa(die) -- Fun info: From Latin "prosa" (straightforward). Prose. Die Prosa. Non-verse writing. "Prosa schreiben" (to write prose). The opposite of poetry. "Prosaisch" as an adjective means both "in prose" and "unimaginative."
8. **teach:** Roman(der) -- Fun info: From French "roman" (novel). A novel. Der Roman. Masculine. "Der Bildungsroman" (coming-of-age novel) is a German literary invention. Thomas Mann's novels are landmarks of German Roman tradition.
9. **teach:** Kurzgeschichte(die) -- Fun info: "Kurz" (short) + "Geschichte" (story). A short story. Die Kurzgeschichte. Post-war German literature excelled at Kurzgeschichten. Heinrich Boell and Wolfgang Borchert wrote defining examples.
10. **teach:** Novelle(die) -- Fun info: From Italian "novella" (short new story). A novella. Die Novelle. In German literary theory, a Novelle has specific features: a central conflict, a turning point, and a "Falke" (falcon, i.e., a symbolic object). Stricter than a Kurzgeschichte.
11. **teach:** Drama(das) -- Fun info: From Greek "drama" (action/deed). A play, a drama. Das Drama. One of the three Gattungen. Schiller and Brecht are Germany's great Dramatiker. "Kein Drama!" (No drama!) is also everyday slang for "no big deal."
12. **teach:** Fabel(die) -- Fun info: From Latin "fabula" (story). A fable. Die Fabel. Animal stories with moral lessons. German fables by Lessing are classics. Also means "plot": "Die Fabel des Romans" (the plot of the novel). Double meaning.
13. **teach:** Gattung(die) -- Fun info: From "Gatte" (companion, i.e., of the same kind). Genre, category. Die Gattung. The three literary Gattungen: Epik (prose fiction), Lyrik (poetry), Dramatik (drama). Also used in biology: species. Classification into kinds.
14. **teach:** Werk(das) -- Fun info: From Germanic "werk" (work/creation). A work (of art/literature). Das Werk. "Das Werk von Kafka" (the works of Kafka). Also: factory ("Automobilwerk"). A completed creation, the result of labor.
15. **teach:** Handlung(die) -- Fun info: From "handeln" (to act). A plot, an action. Die Handlung. "Die Handlung spielt in Berlin" (The plot takes place in Berlin). The thread of events in a story. Also: a physical shop ("Buchhandlung" = bookshop).
16. **teach:** Hauptfigur(die) -- Fun info: "Haupt" (main) + "Figur" (figure/character). The main character, the protagonist. Die Hauptfigur. "Wer ist die Hauptfigur?" (Who is the main character?) is the first question in German Literaturunterricht.
17. **teach:** Schauplatz(der) -- Fun info: "Schau" (show/view) + "Platz" (place). Setting, scene. Der Schauplatz. "Der Schauplatz der Handlung" (the setting of the plot). Where the story unfolds. Literally a "show-place."
18. **teach:** Epoche(die) -- Fun info: From Greek "epoche" (stopping point). An era, a literary period. Die Epoche. German literary history is organized by Epochen: Klassik, Romantik, Expressionismus. Each Epoche has defining characteristics.
19. **teach:** episch -- Fun info: From Greek "epikos" (of epic poetry). Epic. In literary theory: belonging to Epik (narrative prose). In slang: "Das war episch!" (That was epic!). Both formal literary term and modern youth slang.
20. **teach:** lyrisch -- Fun info: From Greek "lyrikos." Lyrical. Belonging to Lyrik. "Lyrische Sprache" (lyrical language) is emotionally charged and rhythmic. Also used loosely: "ein lyrischer Moment" (a lyrical moment).
21. **teach:** dichterisch -- Fun info: From "Dichter" + "-isch." Poetic, of a poet. "Dichterische Freiheit" (poetic license) means a writer can bend rules for art. The adjective form of being a Dichter. "Dichterisch begabt" (poetically gifted).
22. **tip:** "The three literary Gattungen." German literary theory divides all literature into three Gattungen (genres): (1) Epik: narrative prose. Romane, Novellen, Kurzgeschichten, Maerchen, Fabeln. A narrator tells a story. (2) Lyrik: poetry. Gedichte, Verse, Strophen, Reime. Emotional, rhythmic, personal. (3) Dramatik: theater. Dramen, Tragoedien, Komoedien. Dialogue and stage directions, no narrator. Every text belongs to one of these three. This system comes from ancient Greek poetics and German schools still teach it.

25. **fb:** Complete the sentence. s: "Die {1} des Romans spielt in Berlin im 19. Jahrhundert." a: ["Handlung"] opts: [Handlung, Gattung, Strophe, Epoche] hint: "The word for 'plot' or 'action' of a story. From 'handeln' (to act)."
26. **mc:** Vocabulary check. q: "The German word 'Schauplatz' means..." opts: [setting or scene of a story, theater ticket, show program, spotlight] ans: setting or scene of a story. hint: "A 'show-place.' Where the events of the story unfold."

## Lesson 3: "Er sagte, die Backstube sei sein Koenigreich" (He Said the Bakery Was His Kingdom)

**Type:** Story development + grammar intro
**Mode:** Bubble. Hildi's living room, close focus. Heinrich narrating a bakery story. Verumius writing. Hildi correcting details.
**Characters:** Opa Heinrich (animated storytelling), Verumius (thinking, writing), Hildi (fond correction)
**Step count:** 24
**id:** deu_r28l3
**board:** true
**New vocabulary (20):** Zitat(das), Kontext(der), Perspektive(die), Motiv(das), Symbol(das), Vergleich(der), Wortspiel(das), Doppeldeutigkeit(die), Anspielung(die), Lektuere(die), angeblich, anscheinend, bekanntlich, offenbar, laut, behaupten, berichten, betonen, schildern, meinen
**Grammar:** TAUGHT: #1 Konjunktiv I (er habe, sie sei, man koenne, er sage). Tip card explaining Konjunktiv I as the "reporter's mood." Verb table for Konjunktiv I formation. EXPOSED: #10 Indirekte Rede tense shift in Heinrich's stories ("Er erzaehlte, er habe um vier Uhr angefangen").
**Communicative functions:** Reporting what others say, using indirect speech, analyzing literary devices, discussing texts critically
**Cumulative vocab after: ~3,426**

### Step sequence:
1. **intro:** "Er sagte, die Backstube sei sein Koenigreich." Goals: Learn the Konjunktiv I for indirect speech. Master literary analysis vocabulary. Report what others say in formal German.
2. **story:** speaker:narrator, mood:intimate. Heinrich leans forward. His eyes brighten. He is about to tell a story he has told a hundred times, each time slightly different. Verumius's pen is ready.
3. **story:** speaker:heinrich, mood:animated. "Mein Grossvater sagte immer: 'Morgenstund hat Gold im Mund.' Und er hatte recht. Er stand jeden Morgen um vier Uhr auf. Die Backstube war sein Koenigreich."
4. **story:** speaker:verumius, mood:writing. "Also: Heinrich erzaehlte, sein Grossvater habe jeden Morgen um vier Uhr angefangen. Die Backstube sei sein Koenigreich gewesen."
5. **story:** speaker:hildi, mood:correcting. "Es waren halb vier, nicht vier. Aber erzaehl weiter, Papa."
6. **teach:** Zitat(das) -- Fun info: From Latin "citatum" (something cited). A quote, a citation. Das Zitat. "Ein bekanntes Zitat" (a famous quote). German essays always require Zitate with proper attribution. "Zitat Ende" (end quote) is said aloud in speeches.
7. **teach:** Kontext(der) -- Fun info: From Latin "contextus" (weaving together). Context. Der Kontext. "Im Kontext betrachtet" (viewed in context) changes meaning. A Sprichwort without Kontext can be misunderstood. "Aus dem Kontext gerissen" = taken out of context.
8. **teach:** Perspektive(die) -- Fun info: From Latin "perspectivus" (of looking through). Perspective, point of view. Die Perspektive. "Aus Heinrichs Perspektive" (from Heinrich's perspective). Literary analysis always asks: whose Perspektive is this?
9. **teach:** Motiv(das) -- Fun info: From Latin "motivum" (moving). A motif, a theme. Das Motiv. A recurring element in a text. "Das Brot-Motiv" (the bread motif) runs through Heinrich's stories. Also means "motive" for an action.
10. **teach:** Symbol(das) -- Fun info: From Greek "symbolon" (token/sign). A symbol. Das Symbol. "Brot ist ein Symbol fuer Gemeinschaft" (Bread is a symbol of community). Something concrete that represents something abstract. Literary analysis identifies Symbole.
11. **teach:** Vergleich(der) -- Fun info: From "vergleichen" (to compare). A comparison, a simile. Der Vergleich. "Ein Vergleich: Das Leben ist wie ein Buch." A simile uses "wie" (like) or "als" (as). Different from a Metapher, which states identity directly.
12. **teach:** Wortspiel(das) -- Fun info: "Wort" (word) + "Spiel" (game/play). A pun, a play on words. Das Wortspiel. Germans love Wortspiele, though they are famously accused of having no humor. "Ein gelungenes Wortspiel" (a successful pun) earns groans and respect.
13. **teach:** Doppeldeutigkeit(die) -- Fun info: "Doppel" (double) + "Deutigkeit" (meaning-ness). Ambiguity, double meaning. Die Doppeldeutigkeit. "Ein Wort mit Doppeldeutigkeit" has two meanings. Many Sprichwoerter work through Doppeldeutigkeit.
14. **teach:** Anspielung(die) -- Fun info: From "anspielen auf" (to allude to). An allusion, a reference. Die Anspielung. "Eine Anspielung auf Goethe" (an allusion to Goethe). Indirect reference that rewards those who recognize it. Subtlety as art.
15. **teach:** Lektuere(die) -- Fun info: From Latin "lectura" (reading). Reading material, a text being read. Die Lektuere. "Pflichtlektuere" (required reading) is what German students must read. "Eine interessante Lektuere" means both the text and the act of reading it.
16. **teach:** angeblich -- Fun info: "An" + "geblich" (from "geben," to give). Allegedly, supposedly. "Angeblich hat er um vier Uhr angefangen" (Allegedly he started at four). Signals doubt about what is being reported. Distancing adverb.
17. **teach:** anscheinend -- Fun info: "An" + "scheinend" (appearing). Apparently. "Anscheinend war es halb vier" (Apparently it was half past three). Based on appearances. Slightly less certain than "offenbar" but more confident than "angeblich."
18. **teach:** bekanntlich -- Fun info: "Bekannt" (known) + "-lich." As is well known. "Bekanntlich sind Sprichwoerter zeitlos" (As is well known, proverbs are timeless). Signals shared knowledge. The speaker assumes everyone knows this.
19. **teach:** offenbar -- Fun info: "Offen" (open) + "bar" (visible/bearable). Obviously, evidently. "Offenbar hatte Heinrich recht" (Obviously Heinrich was right). Something openly visible. Stronger than "anscheinend," weaker than "sicher."
20. **teach:** laut -- Fun info: Originally "loud," but as a preposition: "according to." "Laut Heinrich begann der Tag um vier" (According to Heinrich, the day began at four). Preposition + dative/genitive. A compact way to attribute a statement.
21. **teach:** behaupten -- Fun info: "Be-" + "haupten" (from "Haupt" = head). To claim, to assert. "Er behauptet, er habe nie verschlafen" (He claims he never overslept). Implies the statement may not be verified. Not separable.
22. **teach:** berichten -- Fun info: "Be-" + "richten" (to direct/set right). To report. "Der Journalist berichtet ueber das Ereignis." Factual reporting. More neutral than "behaupten." Not separable. "Bericht" (report) is the noun.
23. **teach:** betonen -- Fun info: "Be-" + "Ton" (tone/emphasis). To emphasize. "Heinrich betonte, dass Brot wichtig sei" (Heinrich emphasized that bread was important). Placing tone/weight on something. Not separable.
24. **teach:** schildern -- Fun info: From "Schild" (sign/shield). To describe, to depict vividly. "Er schildert die Backstube in allen Details." More vivid than "beschreiben." Painting a picture with words. Originally: to paint a sign.
25. **teach:** meinen -- Fun info: From old German "meinen" (to think/intend). To mean, to think, to be of the opinion. "Was meinen Sie?" (What do you think?). "Er meinte, die alten Zeiten seien besser gewesen" uses Konjunktiv I for indirect speech.
26. **tip:** "Konjunktiv I: The reporter's mood." German has a special verb form for reporting what someone else said: the Konjunktiv I. Formation: verb stem + -e endings. sein: er sei. haben: er habe. koennen: er koenne. sagen: er sage. "Heinrich sagte, die Backstube sei sein Koenigreich." (Heinrich said the bakery was his kingdom.) The Konjunktiv I signals: "I am reporting, not stating my own view." Used in newspapers, academic writing, and formal speech. In everyday German, many speakers use Konjunktiv II (waere, haette) instead, but B2 learners need to recognize and produce Konjunktiv I.

## Lesson 4: "Als ob er alles wuesste" (As If He Knew Everything)

**Type:** Grammar deep dive
**Mode:** Bubble. Grammar instruction with literary and proverb context.
**Characters:** None (grammar instruction with Heinrich's stories as examples)
**Step count:** 24
