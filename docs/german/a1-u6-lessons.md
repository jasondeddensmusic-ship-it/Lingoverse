### Grammar

- Recycle #10 separable verbs (einschlafen, aufwachen)
- Recycle #6 accusative with body parts ("Ich wasche meinen Arm")

### Communicative Functions
- #13 Describe daily routine (introduce: morning routine vocabulary)
- #20 React to news ("Das Wasser ist kalt!" — exclamatory reaction)

### Step Sequence
1. `intro` — Unit 6 intro: "Alles auf Deutsch." Verumius is settling in. Goals: daily routine, phone calls, appointments, time/dates.
2. `teach` — Dusche(die) [gender: fem/crimson]. Fun: Germans love their Duschen. Average German showers 6 minutes.
3. `teach` — duschen. Fun: Reflexive in formal German (sich duschen), but everyday speech drops the sich.
4. `teach` — Schlaf(der) [masc/blue]. Fun: "Schönheitsschlaf" = beauty sleep. Germans take it seriously.
5. `teach` — schlafen. Fun: Irregular! schläft in 3rd person. One of the first strong verbs you meet.
6. `teach` — einschlafen. Fun: Separable! "Ich schlafe ein" = I fall asleep. The ein flies to the end.
7. `teach` — wecken. Fun: Not to confuse with "wecken" (bread roll in southern Germany).
8. `teach` — frühstücken. Fun: Literally "to early-piece." Frühstück = early + Stück. Germans take breakfast seriously.
9. `teach` — Hunger(der) [masc/blue]. Fun: "Ich habe einen Bärenhunger" = I have a bear's hunger = I'm starving.
10. `teach` — Durst(der) [masc/blue]. Fun: Used with haben, not sein. "Ich habe Durst," never "Ich bin Durst."
11. `teach` — spazieren gehen. Fun: Two-verb combo. Germans walk A LOT. "Sonntagsspaziergang" = Sunday stroll tradition.
12. `teach` — früh [time-adj]. Fun: "Der frühe Vogel fängt den Wurm" = the early bird catches the worm. Same proverb!
13. `teach` — heute [time-adj]. Fun: From Old High German "hiutu." Heute is one of the 50 most common German words.
14. `teach` — fertig [adj]. Fun: Means both "finished" and "ready." Context decides. "Bist du fertig?" = Are you done/ready?
15. `teach` — normal [adj]. Fun: Borrowed from Latin/French. Sounds almost identical in English. True cognate.
16. `tip` — **Präteritum of sein/haben** (#22 intro): In spoken German, ONLY sein and haben use simple past. Everything else uses Perfekt. war/warst/war/waren, hatte/hattest/hatte/hatten. Verb table with all forms.
17. `teach` — Kopf(der), Hand(die), Arm(der), Bauch(der) — 4 body words grouped. [genders: blue/crimson/blue/blue]. Fun per card.
18. `teach` — Rücken(der), Bein(das), Fuß(der), Finger(der) — 4 body words grouped. [genders: blue/green/blue/blue]. Fun per card.
19. `mc` — "Das Wasser ___ kalt." opts: [war, hatte, ist, hat]. ans: war. Hint: Simple past of sein.
20. `fb` — "Ich ___ jeden Morgen." s with {1}. a: frühstücke. opts: [frühstücke, frühstücken, schlafe, dusche]. Hint: What do you do with food in the morning?

**Type**: Vocabulary + Grammar | **Mode**: Bubble | **Scene**: Daily Routine
**Characters**: Verumius (describing his day to himself/diary)

### New Vocabulary (20 words)
**time (10)**: Tag(der), Morgen(der), Nacht(die), Woche(die), Wochenende(das), Uhr(die), Minute(die), Sekunde(die), Datum(das), Kalender(der)
**time-adv (7)**: Abends, danach, endlich, gerade, inzwischen, nachher, vorher
**time-adj-batch-2 (3)**: gestern, morgen(adv), spät

### Grammar
- #26 Time expressions (core): Uhr for clock time ("Es ist drei Uhr"), gestern/heute/morgen, am + day, um + clock time

- Recycle #8 modal können ("Ich kann um drei Uhr kommen")

### Communicative Functions
- #11 Tell time and state dates (primary focus)
- #13 Describe daily routine (continued)

### Step Sequence
1. `intro` — "Mein Tag": Verumius organizes his German life. Times, days, schedules.
2. `teach` — Tag(der) [masc/blue]. Fun: Also means "hello" in southern Germany! "Tag!" = casual greeting.
3. `teach` — Morgen(der) [masc/blue]. Fun: "Morgen" = morning AND tomorrow. "Morgen Morgen" = tomorrow morning. Germans find this funny too.
4. `teach` — Nacht(die) [fem/crimson]. Fun: "Gute Nacht" but "heute Nacht" = tonight. Nacht is always feminine, like la nuit in French.
5. `teach` — Woche(die) [fem/crimson]. Fun: From Old German "wehsel" (change/turn). A week = one turn of days.
6. `teach` — Wochenende(das) [neut/green]. Fun: Literally "week-end." Compound noun! die Woche + das Ende = das Wochenende.
7. `teach` — Uhr(die) [fem/crimson]. Fun: Means both "clock" and "o'clock." "Es ist drei Uhr" AND "Die Uhr ist kaputt."
8. `teach` — Minute(die) [fem/crimson]. Fun: Same word in English! From Latin "minuta" (small). One of the easiest German words.
9. `teach` — Sekunde(die) [fem/crimson]. Fun: Also from Latin. "Sekunde mal!" = "Just a second!" Very common.
10. `teach` — Datum(das) [neut/green]. Fun: Plural is "Daten" which also means "data." Context is everything.
11. `teach` — Kalender(der) [masc/blue]. Fun: From Latin "calendae." Germans love paper calendars. Many households still use wall calendars.
12. `teach` — gestern. Fun: Yesterday. "Gestern war alles besser" = yesterday everything was better. Classic German nostalgia phrase.
13. `teach` — morgen(adv). Fun: Lowercase morgen = tomorrow. Uppercase Morgen = morning. German capitalization matters!
14. `teach` — spät. Fun: "Besser spät als nie" = better late than never. Identical proverb.
15. `tip` — **Time expressions** (#26): um + clock time (um drei Uhr), am + day (am Montag), gestern/heute/morgen standalone, gerade = right now, vorher/nachher/danach = before/after/then.
16. `teach` — Abends, danach, endlich, gerade — 4 time adverbs grouped. Fun per card: Abends (in the evening, -s ending = habitual), danach (after that, compound: da+nach), endlich (finally! one of the most satisfying German words), gerade (right now/straight, dual meaning).
17. `teach` — inzwischen, nachher, vorher — 3 time adverbs grouped. Fun per card: inzwischen (meanwhile, from "in zwischen"), nachher (afterward, nach+her), vorher (before, vor+her).
18. `mc` — "Es ist 15:00. Es ist ___." opts: [drei Uhr, fünfzehn, fünf Uhr, dreizehn Uhr]. ans: drei Uhr. Hint: Germans use both 24h and 12h. What's 15:00 in 12h?
19. `fb` — "Gestern ___ ich sehr müde." s with {1}. a: war. opts: [war, bin, hatte, habe]. Hint: Past tense of sein, first person.
20. `mc` — "Ich dusche ___ um sieben Uhr." opts: [morgens, gestern, danach, spät]. ans: morgens. Hint: What time-word means "in the morning" as a habit?
21. `drag_fill` — "Ich frühstücke {1} sieben {2}." blanks: {1: "um", 2: "Uhr"}. pool: [um, am, Uhr, Morgen, Tag]. Hint: Clock time needs um + number + Uhr.
22. `mc` — "___ hatte ich keinen Hunger." opts: [Gestern, Morgen, Endlich, Gerade]. ans: Gestern. Hint: Which word means "yesterday"?

## LESSON 3: "Montag bis Sonntag" (Monday to Sunday)
**Type**: Vocabulary | **Mode**: Bubble | **Scene**: Daily Routine (calendar planning)
**Characters**: Verumius (organizing his week)
**Steps**: 20 | **New words**: 19 | **Cumulative**: 683

### New Vocabulary (19 words)
**days (7)**: Montag(der), Dienstag(der), Mittwoch(der), Donnerstag(der), Freitag(der), Samstag(der), Sonntag(der)
**months-batch-1 (6)**: Januar(der), Februar(der), März(der), April(der), Mai(der), Juni(der)
**ordinals-batch-1 (3)**: erste, zweite, dritte
**prepositions-batch-1 (1)**: seit
**adj-batch-2 (2)**: wichtig, eilig

### Grammar
- #26 Time expressions deepened: am + Montag/Dienstag, im + Januar, seit + time ("seit drei Wochen"), ordinal dates ("der erste Januar")
- Recycle #1 nominative (der Montag), #2 gender articles with days/months

### Communicative Functions
- #11 Tell time and state dates (dates focus)
- #14 Make appointments (introduce: "Am Montag habe ich einen Termin")

### Step Sequence
1. `intro` — Days, months, and dates: The backbone of German scheduling.
2. `teach` — Montag(der) [masc/blue]. Fun: Moon-day! Mond + Tag. Same as English Monday (Moon's day).
3. `teach` — Dienstag(der) [masc/blue]. Fun: Named after Tyr/Ziu (Norse god). English Tuesday = Tyr's day. Same origin!
4. `teach` — Mittwoch(der) [masc/blue]. Fun: Literally "mid-week." Mitte + Woche. The only German day NOT named after a god.
5. `teach` — Donnerstag(der) [masc/blue]. Fun: Thunder-day! Donner + Tag. Thursday = Thor's day. Same thunder god.
6. `teach` — Freitag(der) [masc/blue]. Fun: Named after Freya (goddess of love). Friday = Freya's day. Same goddess!
7. `teach` — Samstag(der) [masc/blue]. Fun: From Hebrew "Shabbat." In northern Germany: "Sonnabend" (sun-evening). Two names for one day!
8. `teach` — Sonntag(der) [masc/blue]. Fun: Sun-day! Sonne + Tag. Identical to English. Shops are CLOSED on Sundays in Germany.
9. `tip` — **am + day**: All days are masculine, all use "am" (an + dem): "Am Montag gehe ich arbeiten." Never "auf Montag" or "in Montag."
10. `teach` — Januar(der), Februar(der), März(der) — grouped. Fun: Januar (named after Janus, two-faced god), Februar (purification month), März (Mars, god of war).
11. `teach` — April(der), Mai(der), Juni(der) — grouped. Fun: April (opening/spring), Mai (Maia, goddess of growth), Juni (Juno, queen of gods).
12. `teach` — erste, zweite, dritte — ordinals. Fun: "der erste" adds -te to the number. 1st/2nd/3rd are irregular (like English). From 4th onward: regular -te.
13. `teach` — seit [prep]. Fun: Triggers DATIVE. Means "since/for" with time. "Seit drei Tagen" = for three days. Present tense with seit (unlike English "I have been...").
14. `teach` — wichtig [adj]. Fun: From "Gewicht" (weight). Important = weighty. Same metaphor as English "carries weight."
15. `teach` — eilig [adj]. Fun: "Es ist eilig" = it's urgent. "Ich habe es eilig" = I'm in a hurry. Very useful at offices!
16. `mc` — "Mittwoch bedeutet ___." opts: [Mitte der Woche, Tag des Mondes, Tag des Donners, Tag der Sonne]. ans: Mitte der Woche. Hint: What does "Mitt" + "Woch" literally combine to mean?
17. `fb` — "___ Montag habe ich einen Termin." s with {1}. a: Am. opts: [Am, Im, Um, Seit]. Hint: Days use a specific preposition contraction.
18. `mc` — "Der ___ Januar ist Neujahr." opts: [erste, zweite, dritte, vierte]. ans: erste. Hint: New Year's Day is the 1st.
19. `match` — pairs: [{nl:"Montag",en:"Monday"},{nl:"Mittwoch",en:"Wednesday"},{nl:"Freitag",en:"Friday"},{nl:"Sonntag",en:"Sunday"}]
20. `fb` — "Ich wohne ___ drei Wochen in Berlin." s with {1}. a: seit. opts: [seit, vor, nach, um]. Hint: Which preposition means "for" with a duration that continues to now?

## LESSON 4: "Juli bis Dezember" (July to December)
**Type**: Vocabulary + Grammar | **Mode**: Bubble | **Scene**: Daily Routine (seasonal planning)
**Characters**: Verumius (looking at his calendar, planning the year)
**Steps**: 20 | **New words**: 19 | **Cumulative**: 702

### New Vocabulary (19 words)
**months-batch-2 (6)**: Juli(der), August(der), September(der), Oktober(der), November(der), Dezember(der)
**seasons (4)**: Frühling(der), Sommer(der), Herbst(der), Winter(der)
**ordinals-batch-2 (3)**: vierte, fünfte, sechste
**weather (5)**: Wetter(das), Regen(der), regnen, Schnee(der), Temperatur(die)
**adv (1)**: leider

### Grammar
- #26 Time expressions deepened: im + month ("im Juli"), im + season ("im Sommer"), ordinal dates continued ("der vierte Juli")
- #22 Präteritum deepened: "Im Winter war es kalt. Ich hatte keinen Urlaub."
- Recycle #13 es gibt ("Es gibt Schnee im Winter")

### Communicative Functions
- #11 Tell time and state dates (months/seasons/weather)
- #20 React to news ("Leider regnet es" — expressing disappointment)

### Step Sequence
1. `intro` — Months, seasons, and weather: Describing when things happen and what it's like outside.
2. `teach` — Juli(der) [masc/blue]. Fun: Named after Julius Caesar. Same in almost every European language.
3. `teach` — August(der) [masc/blue]. Fun: Named after Emperor Augustus. August is Germany's main vacation month.
4. `teach` — September(der), Oktober(der), November(der), Dezember(der) — grouped. Fun: Sept=7, Okt=8, Nov=9, Dez=10 in old Roman calendar (year started in March). Near-identical to English.
5. `teach` — Frühling(der) [masc/blue]. Fun: Literally "early-ling." Früh (early) + -ling (suffix). Spring = the early season.
6. `teach` — Sommer(der) [masc/blue]. Fun: Cognate with English "summer." Proto-Germanic root. Identical meaning for 2000+ years.
7. `teach` — Herbst(der) [masc/blue]. Fun: Cognate with English "harvest." Same origin! English shifted to "autumn" (French), German kept Herbst.
8. `teach` — Winter(der) [masc/blue]. Fun: Identical to English. From Proto-Germanic. German winters are serious: -10°C is normal in Bavaria.
9. `teach` — Wetter(das) [neut/green]. Fun: Neuter! "Wie ist das Wetter?" = How's the weather? One of the top small-talk topics in Germany.
10. `teach` — Regen(der) [masc/blue]. Fun: Germany gets ~800mm rain per year. "Regenwetter" = rainy weather. Compound noun!
11. `teach` — regnen. Fun: Impersonal verb: "Es regnet." You can't say "Ich regne." Only "es" (it) can rain.
12. `teach` — Schnee(der) [masc/blue]. Fun: Cognate with English "snow." From Proto-Germanic *snaiwaz. Germans get excited about the first Schnee.
13. `teach` — Temperatur(die) [fem/crimson]. Fun: Near-identical cognate. Germany uses Celsius. 20°C = comfortable. 30°C = Germans complain it's too hot.
14. `teach` — leider. Fun: "Unfortunately." From "Leid" (suffering). Germans use this word A LOT. It softens bad news politely.
15. `teach` — vierte, fünfte, sechste — ordinals. Fun: From 4th onward, add -te to the number (vier+te, fünf+te, sechs+te). Regular pattern!
16. `tip` — **im + month/season**: "Im Januar" (in January), "im Sommer" (in summer). im = in + dem (dative contraction). Months and seasons are ALL masculine (der).
17. `mc` — "Wir fahren ___ Sommer nach Spanien." opts: [im, am, um, seit]. ans: im. Hint: Seasons use which preposition contraction?
18. `fb` — "Es ___ seit drei Tagen." s with {1}. a: regnet. opts: [regnet, schneit, Regen, Wetter]. Hint: The verb for rain, with "es" as subject.
19. `mc` — "Der vierte ___ ist der amerikanische Nationalfeiertag." opts: [Juli, Juni, August, Mai]. ans: Juli. Hint: Independence Day: July 4th.
20. `drag_fill` — "Gestern {1} es kalt und ich {2} keinen Regenschirm." blanks: {1: "war", 2: "hatte"}. pool: [war, hatte, ist, habe, bin]. Hint: Past tense of sein and haben.

## LESSON 5: "Der Anruf" (The Phone Call)
**Type**: Story + Vocabulary | **Mode**: Cinematic -> Bubble | **Scene**: The Phone Call
**Characters**: Verumius, automated phone menu voice, Hausverwaltung employee
**Steps**: 22 | **New words**: 20 | **Cumulative**: 722

### New Vocabulary (20 words)
**phone (7)**: Anruf(der), Anrufbeantworter(der), Empfänger(der), Handy(das), Nachricht(die), Telefon(das), Vorwahl(die)
**phone-verbs (3)**: anrufen, klingeln, telefonieren
**body-batch-2 (8)**: Auge(das), Gesicht(das), Haar(das), Mund(der), Nase(die), Ohr(das), Zahn(der), Zeh(der)
**prepositions-batch-2 (2)**: um, wegen

### Grammar
- #5 Imperative expanded in phone context: "Drücken Sie die Eins!" "Warten Sie bitte!" "Sprechen Sie nach dem Ton!"
- #22 Präteritum: "Das Wasser war kalt. Ich hatte kein warmes Wasser."
- Recycle #8 modal müssen ("Ich muss die Hausverwaltung anrufen")

### Communicative Functions
- #16 Ask for help / make requests ("Können Sie mir helfen? Ich habe kein warmes Wasser.")
- #20 React to news ("Was? Zwanzig Minuten warten?!")

### Step Sequence
1. `intro` — "Der Anruf": Verumius must call the building management. His hot water is broken. Time to learn phone German!
2. `teach` — Telefon(das) [neut/green]. Fun: Greek origin (tele=far, phon=sound). Neuter because it's a Gerät (device). Germans still say "Am Telefon" = on the phone.
3. `teach` — Handy(das) [neut/green]. Fun: FALSE FRIEND! "Handy" is NOT English. Germans invented this word for mobile phone. English "handy" = praktisch.
4. `teach` — Anruf(der) [masc/blue]. Fun: Compound: an (at) + Ruf (call). "Einen Anruf machen" = to make a call. Separable noun from anrufen.
5. `teach` — anrufen. Fun: Separable! "Ich rufe dich an." The "an" flies to the end. Past: "Ich habe angerufen."
6. `teach` — klingeln. Fun: Onomatopoeia! "Kling kling!" = ring ring! "Das Telefon klingelt" = the phone is ringing.
7. `teach` — telefonieren. Fun: NOT separable (unlike anrufen). "Ich telefoniere mit meinem Chef." Uses "mit" for the other person.
8. `teach` — Anrufbeantworter(der) [masc/blue]. Fun: Monster compound: Anruf + Beantworter (answerer). Literally "call-answerer." 18 letters!
9. `teach` — Empfänger(der) [masc/blue]. Fun: Recipient. From "empfangen" (to receive). Used for mail AND phone.
10. `teach` — Nachricht(die) [fem/crimson]. Fun: "Nach" (after) + "Richt" (direction/report). A message = something directed to you. Plural: Nachrichten = the news!
11. `teach` — Vorwahl(die) [fem/crimson]. Fun: Area code. Vor (before) + Wahl (choice/dial). The number you dial BEFORE the main number.
12. `teach` — um [prep]. Fun: Means "at" (time), "around" (place), and "in order to" (purpose). Three meanings, one tiny word.
13. `teach` — wegen [prep]. Fun: "Because of." Takes GENITIVE (wegen des Regens) but in spoken German often dative (wegen dem Regen). Both accepted at A1.
14. `teach` — Auge(das), Gesicht(das), Haar(das), Mund(der) — body batch 2a. [genders]. Fun per card: Auge (neuter! plural Augen), Gesicht (face, also means "vision" in old German), Haar (singular=one hair, Haare=head of hair), Mund (mouth, Mund auf! = open your mouth!).
15. `teach` — Nase(die), Ohr(das), Zahn(der), Zeh(der) — body batch 2b. [genders]. Fun per card: Nase (feminine, "Ich habe die Nase voll" = I'm fed up, literally "my nose is full"), Ohr (neuter! plural Ohren), Zahn (tooth, Zahnarzt = dentist = tooth-doctor), Zeh (toe, masculine, sometimes also "die Zehe").
16. `tip` — **Imperative in formal contexts** (#5 expanded): Sie-form imperative = infinitive + Sie. "Warten Sie!" "Sprechen Sie!" "Drücken Sie die Eins!" Used everywhere: phone menus, offices, doctors. Always with Sie for strangers.
17. `mc` — "Das Telefon ___." opts: [klingelt, anruft, telefoniert, spricht]. ans: klingelt. Hint: Which verb means "to ring"?
18. `fb` — "Ich ___ die Hausverwaltung ___." s: "Ich {1} die Hausverwaltung an." a: rufe. opts: [rufe, klingele, telefoniere, spreche]. Hint: Separable verb for "to call someone."
19. `mc` — "___ Sie bitte eine Nachricht!" opts: [Hinterlassen, Hinterlasse, Hinterlässt, Hinterlasst]. ans: Hinterlassen. Hint: Formal imperative = infinitive + Sie.
20. `mc` — "Das Wasser ist kalt ___ einer kaputten Heizung." opts: [wegen, um, seit, mit]. ans: wegen. Hint: Which preposition means "because of"?
21. `match` — pairs: [{nl:"Anruf",en:"phone call"},{nl:"Nachricht",en:"message"},{nl:"Vorwahl",en:"area code"},{nl:"Empfänger",en:"recipient"}]
22. `fb` — "___ Sie bitte nach dem Ton!" s with {1}. a: Sprechen. opts: [Sprechen, Sprich, Sprecht, Gesprochen]. Hint: Formal imperative for "speak."

## LESSON 6: "Beim Arzt" (At the Doctor's)
**Type**: Vocabulary + Communicative | **Mode**: Bubble | **Scene**: Hausverwaltung Success (connecting health + appointments)
**Characters**: Verumius, doctor's receptionist
**Steps**: 20 | **New words**: 18 | **Cumulative**: 740

### New Vocabulary (18 words)
**health (7)**: Arzt(der), Ärztin(die), Gesundheit(die), krank, Krankenhaus(das), Krankheit(die), Medikament(das)
**appointments (5)**: Termin(der), Ergebnis(das), Fehler(der), Regel(die), Aufgabe(die)
**life-verbs-batch-1 (6)**: anfangen, aufhören, aufpassen, benutzen, beschreiben, vergessen

### Grammar
- #22 Präteritum practiced: "Ich war letzte Woche krank. Ich hatte Kopfschmerzen."
- Recycle #10 separable verbs (anfangen, aufhören, aufpassen)
- Recycle #8 modal sollen ("Sie sollen zum Arzt gehen")

### Communicative Functions
- #14 Make appointments ("Ich brauche einen Termin beim Arzt")
- #16 Ask for help ("Können Sie mir helfen? Ich bin krank.")
- #17 Express ability ("Ich kann am Montag um 10 Uhr kommen")

### Step Sequence
1. `intro` — "Beim Arzt": Health, appointments, and describing what's wrong. Essential survival German.
2. `teach` — Arzt(der) [masc/blue]. Fun: From Latin "archiater" (chief healer). In Germany, you need a "Überweisung" (referral) from your Hausarzt for specialists.
3. `teach` — Ärztin(die) [fem/crimson]. Fun: Female form adds -in. Arzt → Ärztin. This pattern works for many professions: Lehrer → Lehrerin.
4. `teach` — krank [adj]. Fun: "Krank" also means "sick" in slang (like English "sick" = cool, but less common). "Krankfeiern" = to call in sick (literally "celebrate being sick").
5. `teach` — Krankenhaus(das) [neut/green]. Fun: Literally "sick-house." Kranken (sick people) + Haus. Compound noun logic!
6. `teach` — Krankheit(die) [fem/crimson]. Fun: -heit suffix = abstract noun (like English -ness). Krank + heit = sickness/illness.
7. `teach` — Gesundheit(die) [fem/crimson]. Fun: You already know this one! Germans say it when someone sneezes. Literally: "health(ness)."
8. `teach` — Medikament(das) [neut/green]. Fun: From Latin "medicamentum." The everyday word. "Medizin" exists too but sounds more formal/scientific.
9. `teach` — Termin(der) [masc/blue]. Fun: NOT "terminal"! Means "appointment." False friend alert. "Einen Termin machen" = to make an appointment.
10. `teach` — Ergebnis(das) [neut/green]. Fun: Er- (result prefix) + geben (give) + -nis (abstract suffix). A "given result." The -nis suffix always makes it neuter.
