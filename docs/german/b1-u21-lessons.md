## Lesson 1: "Leipzig Hauptbahnhof" (Leipzig Central Station)

**Type:** Story intro
**Mode:** Cinematic. Leipzig Hauptbahnhof interior, massive renovated historic station, grand arched ceiling, modern shops inside classical architecture, bright daylight through glass roof. Mia excited and proud, showing Verumius her hometown.
**Characters:** Verumius (protagonist, curious), Mia (guide, proud/excited, looser energy than in Berlin), Narrator
**Step count:** 22
**New vocabulary (16):** Grenze(die), Mauer(die), Freiheit(die), Gesellschaft(die), historisch, politisch, national, Erinnerung(die), Vergangenheit(die), Entwicklung(die), entstehen, erleben, erfahren, überhaupt, vor allem, ursprünglich
**Grammar:** EXPOSED: Adj declension without article (#24) in narrator sentences. RECYCLED: Relative clauses (from U20), Nebensatz word order (weil/dass from A2), Perfekt (from A2)
**Communicative functions:** Describe a city and its history (exposure), express excitement about showing someone your hometown (exposure), introduce historical context (exposure)
**Cumulative vocab after: 2,715**

### Step sequence:
1. **intro:** "Leipzig Hauptbahnhof." Goals: Travel to Leipzig with Mia. Learn core vocabulary for history, society, and change. Begin exploring the story of German reunification.
2. **story:** speaker:narrator, mood:excited. "Der ICE halt in Leipzig. Mia steht schon an der Tur, bevor der Zug steht. Sie hat Verumius seit Wochen von ihrer Stadt erzahlt. Jetzt ist er endlich hier." / "The ICE stops in Leipzig. Mia is already at the door before the train has stopped. She has been telling Verumius about her city for weeks. Now he is finally here."
3. **story:** speaker:mia, mood:proud. "Das ist Leipzig Hauptbahnhof! Einer der grossten Bahnhofe Europas. Frisch renoviert, mit Geschaften drin. Fruher war das hier alles grau." / "This is Leipzig Central Station! One of the largest train stations in Europe. Freshly renovated, with shops inside. It all used to be grey here."
4. **teach:** Grenze(die). Fun info: "Border/boundary." From Old High German "greniza," borrowed from Slavic languages. The most famous German Grenze was the one that split the country in two from 1949 to 1989. "Grenzenlos" (boundless) became a symbol of reunification.
5. **teach:** Mauer(die). Fun info: "Wall." From Latin "murus." Die Berliner Mauer (the Berlin Wall) stood from 1961 to 1989. When Germans say "die Mauer," everyone knows which wall they mean. "Mauerfall" (fall of the wall) needs no further explanation.
6. **teach:** Freiheit(die). Fun info: "Freedom." Frei (free) + -heit (abstract suffix). The word that echoed through Nikolaikirche in 1989. "Freiheit" was the single most powerful word of the peaceful revolution. Leipzig calls itself "Heldenstadt" (hero city) because of it.
7. **teach:** Gesellschaft(die). Fun info: "Society." From "Geselle" (companion) + -schaft. Literally: companionship. A GmbH (Gesellschaft mit beschrankter Haftung) is a "society with limited liability." The word covers both grand social structures and small business partnerships.
8. **teach:** historisch. Fun info: "Historical." From Greek "historikos." Germans distinguish between "historisch" (historically significant) and "geschichtlich" (relating to history as a discipline). Leipzig is "historisch" because history happened here.
9. **teach:** politisch. Fun info: "Political." From Greek "politikos" (of citizens). "Politisch korrekt" entered German from English. Germans debate politics with passion. "Politische Bildung" (political education) is taught in every German school.
10. **teach:** national. Fun info: "National." German national identity is complicated. After 1945, "national" became a loaded word. Today it coexists carefully with European identity. "Nationalmannschaft" (national team) is one of the few safe uses.
11. **teach:** Erinnerung(die). Fun info: "Memory/remembrance." Er- + inner (inner) + -ung. To bring something inward again. "Erinnerungskultur" (remembrance culture) is a core German value. Germany confronts its past through memorials, museums, and education.
12. **teach:** Vergangenheit(die). Fun info: "Past." From "vergangen" (gone by). Ver- + gangen (gone). The past that has gone away. "Vergangenheitsbewaltigung" (coming to terms with the past) is a famously long German word for a famously difficult German process.
13. **teach:** Entwicklung(die). Fun info: "Development." From "entwickeln" (to develop). Ent- (out of) + wickeln (to wrap). Literally: unwrapping. Development is an unwrapping of potential. "Entwicklungsland" (developing country) uses the same logic.
14. **teach:** entstehen. Fun info: "To arise/come into being." Ent- (out of) + stehen (to stand). Something standing forth into existence. "Die Stadt ist im 12. Jahrhundert entstanden" (The city arose in the 12th century). Perfekt: ist entstanden.
15. **teach:** erleben. Fun info: "To experience." Er- (achieving prefix) + leben (to live). To truly live through something. "Ein Erlebnis" (an experience) is something you actively lived. Stronger than "Erfahrung" (experience gained over time).
16. **teach:** erfahren. Fun info: "To find out / to experience." Er- + fahren (to drive/travel). Literally: to travel toward knowledge. "Ich habe erfahren, dass..." (I found out that...). Also means "experienced" as an adjective: "ein erfahrener Arzt" (an experienced doctor).
17. **teach:** überhaupt. Fun info: "At all / in general." One of German's most versatile words. "Hast du uberhaupt verstanden?" (Did you understand at all?). "Uberhaupt nicht" (not at all). Adds emphasis, doubt, or generalization depending on context.
18. **teach:** vor allem. Fun info: "Above all / especially." Literally "before everything." A connector that signals what matters most. "Leipzig ist schon, aber vor allem ist es historisch" (Leipzig is beautiful, but above all it is historic).
19. **teach:** ursprünglich. Fun info: "Original(ly)." From "Ursprung" (origin). Ur- (primal/original) + Sprung (jump/leap). The primal leap, the very beginning. "Ursprunglich wollte ich..." (Originally I wanted to...) is how Germans preface changed plans.
20. **mc:** Historical vocabulary. q: "Leipzig calls itself 'Heldenstadt.' What word describes the quality that the people of Leipzig fought for in 1989?" opts: [Freiheit, Gesellschaft, Grenze, Erinnerung] ans: Freiheit. hint: "This word means freedom, built from frei + -heit."
21. **fb:** Context completion. s: "Die Berliner {1} fiel am 9. November 1989." a: ["Mauer"] opts: [Mauer, Grenze, Freiheit, Gesellschaft] hint: "Which word means wall, specifically the famous one?"
22. **match:** Core history pairs. pairs: [{trg:"Grenze",src:"border"},{trg:"Freiheit",src:"freedom"},{trg:"Erinnerung",src:"memory"},{trg:"Vergangenheit",src:"past"}]

## Lesson 2: "Stimmen der Geschichte" (Voices of History)

**Type:** Vocab teach
**Mode:** Bubble. No scene. Pure vocabulary across politics, society, and change domains.
**Characters:** None (pure vocabulary)
**Step count:** 24
**New vocabulary (18):** Bevölkerung(die), Bürger(der), Bürgerin(die), Gewalt(die), Krieg(der), Friede(der), Kampf(der), Reform(die), Wahl(die), Politik(die), Politiker(der), Politikerin(die), Rede(die), Öffentlichkeit(die), öffentlich, sozial, führen, dienen
**Grammar:** RECYCLED: Nebensatz word order (A2), genitive prepositions (from U15). EXPOSED: Adj declension without article (#24) in example sentences, Reciprocal verbs (#30) in examples
**Communicative functions:** Discuss politics and society (taught), describe civic participation (taught), understand news vocabulary (exposure)
**Cumulative vocab after: 2,733**

### Step sequence:
1. **intro:** "Stimmen der Geschichte." Goals: Learn vocabulary for politics, civic life, and social structures. Build the words you need to discuss history and current events in German.
2. **teach:** Bevölkerung(die). Fun info: From "Volk" (people). Be- + volker + -ung. The process of populating, and the population itself. "Die Bevolkerung Deutschlands" (the population of Germany) is about 84 million. One word for both the people and the act of peopling a land.
3. **teach:** Bürger(der). Fun info: "Citizen." From "Burg" (castle/fortress). Originally the people who lived inside the castle walls. "Burgerrecht" (civil right) and "Burgermeister" (mayor, literally citizen-master) both come from this root. English "burgess" is a cousin.
4. **teach:** Bürgerin(die). Fun info: "Female citizen." -in suffix. "Burgerinnen und Burger" is the standard inclusive phrasing in official German. The 1989 Leipzig demonstrators chanted "Wir sind das Volk!" (We are the people!), claiming their rights as Burger.
5. **teach:** Gewalt(die). Fun info: "Violence / power." From "walten" (to rule). One word, two meanings: "Gewalt anwenden" (to use violence) and "Gewaltenteilung" (separation of powers). The Leipzig revolution was remarkable because it succeeded without Gewalt.
6. **teach:** Krieg(der). Fun info: "War." From Old High German "chreg" (stubbornness/effort). "Der Kalte Krieg" (the Cold War) divided Germany. "Nachkriegszeit" (post-war period) shaped everything about modern Germany. A short, heavy word.
7. **teach:** Friede(der). Fun info: "Peace." Also spelled "Frieden" (both forms are correct). From Proto-Germanic *frithuz (protection). "Friedensgebet" (peace prayer) was what started the 1989 movement at Nikolaikirche. "Friedlich" (peaceful) describes how Leipzig changed the world.
8. **teach:** Kampf(der). Fun info: "Fight/struggle." From Latin "campus" (battlefield). "Kampf um Freiheit" (fight for freedom). Not always physical: "Kampf gegen den Klimawandel" (fight against climate change). One of German's most emotionally charged words.
9. **teach:** Reform(die). Fun info: "Reform." From Latin "reformare" (to reshape). "Reformen durchfuhren" (to carry out reforms) is what politicians promise. The 1989 movement started as a call for Reform, not reunification. History took its own course.
10. **teach:** Wahl(die). Fun info: "Election / choice." From "wahlen" (to choose). "Freie Wahlen" (free elections) was a key demand of the 1989 demonstrators. "Die Qual der Wahl" (the agony of choice) is a German rhyming idiom for having too many options.
11. **teach:** Politik(die). Fun info: "Politics." From Greek "politike" (civic affairs). No plural form. "In die Politik gehen" (to go into politics). Germans take their Politik seriously: voter turnout regularly exceeds 70%.
12. **teach:** Politiker(der). Fun info: "Politician (male)." Politik + -er. "Ein erfahrener Politiker" uses adjective declension you will learn this unit. German political titles are long: "Bundestagsabgeordneter" (member of the Bundestag) has 24 letters.
13. **teach:** Politikerin(die). Fun info: "Politician (female)." -in suffix. Germany has had a female Chancellor (Bundeskanzlerin) and numerous female ministers. The -in ending is standard, not optional, in formal German for all professional titles.
14. **teach:** Rede(die). Fun info: "Speech." From "reden" (to speak/talk). "Eine Rede halten" (to give a speech). Not just formal addresses: "Wovon ist die Rede?" (What are we talking about?) is everyday German. "Redefreiheit" = freedom of speech.
15. **teach:** Öffentlichkeit(die). Fun info: "The public / publicity." From "offentlich" (public). Offen (open) + -lich + -keit. Triple suffix chain. "In der Offentlichkeit" (in public). "Die Offentlichkeit informieren" (to inform the public). The space where citizens and government meet.
16. **teach:** öffentlich. Fun info: "Public." From "offen" (open). "Offentliche Verkehrsmittel" (public transport) and "offentliche Meinung" (public opinion). What is offentlich belongs to everyone. Contrast: "privat" (private).
17. **teach:** sozial. Fun info: "Social." From Latin "socialis." "Soziale Marktwirtschaft" (social market economy) is Germany's economic model: capitalism with a social safety net. "Sozialwohnung" (social housing) and "Sozialhilfe" (social welfare) are core institutions.
18. **teach:** führen. Fun info: "To lead / to guide." From Proto-Germanic *forjan. "Fuhrung" (leadership/guided tour). "Ein Gesprach fuhren" (to lead a conversation). Also: "Krieg fuhren" (to wage war). The word serves both peaceful guidance and forceful direction.
19. **teach:** dienen. Fun info: "To serve." From Proto-Germanic *thewjan. "Dem Frieden dienen" (to serve peace). "Der Dienst" (service). "Beamter im offentlichen Dienst" (civil servant). In Leipzig, many felt they had served a system that did not serve them back.
20. **story:** speaker:mia, mood:reflective. "Meine Grosseltern haben in der DDR gelebt. Sie haben einander nicht sofort vertraut. Das war normal. Man wusste nie, wer fur die Stasi arbeitete." / "My grandparents lived in the GDR. They did not trust each other right away. That was normal. You never knew who worked for the Stasi." [Exposes reciprocal "einander" naturally]
21. **mc:** Civic vocabulary. q: "The 1989 demonstrators demanded 'freie Wahlen.' What does Wahl mean?" opts: [election, speech, violence, reform] ans: election. hint: "This word comes from wahlen (to choose)."
22. **fb:** Political context. s: "Die Burger forderten {1} und Demokratie." a: ["Freiheit"] opts: [Freiheit, Gewalt, Krieg, Kampf] hint: "Which word means freedom, the opposite of oppression?"
23. **mc:** Compound meaning. q: "Offentlichkeit comes from offen. What does offentlich mean?" opts: [public, official, open, honest] ans: public. hint: "What is offen (open) belongs to everyone, is visible to all."
24. **match:** Society pairs. pairs: [{trg:"Bevolkerung",src:"population"},{trg:"Gewalt",src:"violence"},{trg:"Wahl",src:"election"},{trg:"Rede",src:"speech"}]

## Lesson 3: "Altes Rathaus" (The Old Town Hall)

**Type:** Story development + grammar
**Mode:** Bubble. Leipzig Marktplatz, Renaissance Altes Rathaus building, cobblestone square, Auerbachs Keller entrance. Warm afternoon light. Mia narrating with passion.
**Characters:** Mia (animated/passionate guide), Verumius (absorbing history, thinking)
**Step count:** 24
**New vocabulary (16):** Denkmal(das), Einfluss(der), Fortschritt(der), Generation(die), Symbol(das), Zeichen(das), Wirkung(die), Hoffnung(die), kritisch, häufig, ständig, ewig, verändern, nachdenken, wirken, einerseits
**Grammar:** TAUGHT: Adj declension without article #24 (kaltes Wasser pattern: strong endings when no article precedes). EXPOSED: Adj declension genitive #25 in narrator sentences. RECYCLED: Relative clauses + prep (U20), Adverbial conjunctions (U20)
**Communicative functions:** Describe historical landmarks (taught), express complex opinions about history (taught), use adjectives without articles for abstract/mass nouns (taught)
**Cumulative vocab after: 2,749**

### Step sequence:
1. **intro:** "Altes Rathaus." Goals: Explore Leipzig's historic center with Mia. Learn adjective declension without articles (strong endings). Discuss monuments, influence, and cultural change.
2. **story:** speaker:mia, mood:animated. "Das ist der Marktplatz. Und dort: das Alte Rathaus. Renaissance-Architektur aus dem 16. Jahrhundert. Goethe hat in Auerbachs Keller gesessen und Faust geschrieben. Echter historischer Boden!" / "This is the market square. And there: the Old Town Hall. Renaissance architecture from the 16th century. Goethe sat in Auerbachs Keller and wrote Faust. Real historic ground!"
3. **teach:** Denkmal(das). Fun info: "Monument/memorial." Denk (think) + Mal (mark/time). A thinking-mark, a place that makes you think. "Denkmalschutz" (monument protection) is serious in Germany. You cannot even change the windows of a protected building.
4. **teach:** Einfluss(der). Fun info: "Influence." Ein (in) + Fluss (flow). Something flowing in. "Grossen Einfluss haben" (to have great influence). Leipzig's influence on German music is enormous: Bach, Mendelssohn, and Schumann all worked here.
5. **teach:** Fortschritt(der). Fun info: "Progress." Fort (forward) + Schritt (step). A forward step. "Fortschritte machen" (to make progress). The word itself walks forward. "Technischer Fortschritt" (technological progress) is a favorite German compound.
6. **teach:** Generation(die). Fun info: "Generation." From Latin "generatio." "Die nachste Generation" (the next generation). Germans speak of the "Kriegsgeneration," "Nachkriegsgeneration," and "Wiedervereinigungsgeneration." Each carries different weight.
7. **teach:** Symbol(das). Fun info: "Symbol." From Greek "symbolon" (sign/token). "Das Brandenburger Tor ist ein Symbol der Einheit" (The Brandenburg Gate is a symbol of unity). Leipzig's Nikolaikirche is a Symbol of peaceful revolution.
8. **teach:** Zeichen(das). Fun info: "Sign/mark." From "zeichnen" (to draw). "Ein Zeichen setzen" (to set a sign, to make a statement). More concrete than Symbol: a Zeichen is something visible, a Symbol carries deeper meaning. Traffic signs are Zeichen, not Symbole.
9. **teach:** Wirkung(die). Fun info: "Effect/impact." From "wirken" (to have an effect). "Nebenwirkung" (side effect) is what you read on medicine packages. "Langfristige Wirkung" (long-term effect) is how historians describe what happened in Leipzig.
10. **teach:** Hoffnung(die). Fun info: "Hope." From "hoffen" (to hope). "Die Hoffnung stirbt zuletzt" (hope dies last) is a German proverb. In 1989, Hoffnung was the fuel that brought 70,000 people onto Leipzig's streets with candles.
11. **teach:** kritisch. Fun info: "Critical." From Greek "kritikos" (able to judge). "Kritisch denken" (to think critically). "Eine kritische Masse" (a critical mass). Germans value "kritisches Denken" in education. Being kritisch is not negative, it means you think carefully.
12. **teach:** häufig. Fun info: "Frequent(ly)." From "Haufen" (pile/heap). Something that piles up happens haufig. "Haufig gestellte Fragen" (frequently asked questions, FAQ). More formal than "oft" (often) but means the same.
13. **teach:** ständig. Fun info: "Constant(ly)." From "Stand" (standing/state). Something that keeps standing, that persists. "Sie redet standig" (She talks constantly). Often used with mild complaint. "Standige Vertretung" (permanent representation) is a diplomatic term.
14. **teach:** ewig. Fun info: "Eternal." From Old High German "ewig" (age-long). "Ewig und drei Tage" (forever and three days) is the German version of "forever and a day." "Das Ewige Licht" (the eternal light) burns in every Catholic church.
15. **teach:** verändern. Fun info: "To change/alter." Ver- + andern (to make different). Reflexive: "sich verandern" (to change oneself). "Die Welt verandern" (to change the world). Stronger than "andern" alone: verandern implies transformation.
16. **teach:** nachdenken. Fun info: "To reflect/think about." Nach (after) + denken (to think). Thinking-after, pondering. "Denk mal daruber nach" (Think about it). Separable: "Ich denke daruber nach." Requires uber + accusative for the topic.
17. **teach:** wirken. Fun info: "To have an effect / to seem." "Das wirkt komisch" (That seems strange). "Er wirkt mude" (He seems tired). Also: "an etwas wirken" (to work on something). A word that bridges appearance and reality.
18. **teach:** einerseits. Fun info: "On one hand." Einer (one) + seits (side). Always paired with "andererseits" (on the other hand). "Einerseits ist Leipzig schon, andererseits hat es eine schwere Geschichte." A connector for balanced arguments.
19. **tip:** "Adjective declension without articles: strong endings." When no article comes before the adjective, the adjective itself must show gender, number, and case. It takes the ending that the definite article WOULD have had.
\n- Nominative: guter Wein (der), kalte Milch (die), frisches Brot (das), neue Ideen (die pl.)
\n- Accusative: guten Wein, kalte Milch, frisches Brot, neue Ideen
\n- Dative: gutem Wein, kalter Milch, frischem Brot, neuen Ideen
\n- Rule: The adjective does the article's job. Strong endings = article endings.
\n- Common contexts: mass nouns (Wasser, Brot), abstracts (Freiheit, Hoffnung), plurals without article (neue Ideen).

## Lesson 4: "Nikolaikirche" (The Church of St. Nicholas)

**Type:** Grammar
**Mode:** Cinematic. Nikolaikirche interior, pink-and-white palm-leaf columns, soaring ceiling, soft light through tall windows, sacred and weighted with history. Mia quiet and reflective. The most emotionally serious scene so far.
**Characters:** Mia (reflective/emotional, rare vulnerability), Verumius (thinking, understanding the weight), Narrator
**Step count:** 24
**New vocabulary (14):** Versammlung(die), Gemeinschaft(die), Jugend(die), Zeuge(der), Zeugin(die), Realität(die), Wirklichkeit(die), gleichzeitig, menschlich, völlig, geschehen, entwickeln, überlegen, andererseits
**Grammar:** TAUGHT: Adj declension genitive #25 (des guten Mannes: always -en after articles in genitive). TAUGHT: Reciprocal verbs #30 (sich kennen, einander, miteinander). PRACTICED: #24 strong endings (3+ quiz steps)
**Communicative functions:** Narrate historical events (taught), express empathy and emotional responses (taught), use reciprocal verbs to describe relationships (taught), construct balanced arguments with einerseits/andererseits (practiced)
**Cumulative vocab after: 2,763**

### Step sequence:
1. **intro:** "Nikolaikirche." Goals: Stand inside the church where the 1989 peaceful revolution began. Master genitive adjective declension. Learn reciprocal verbs (each other). Express emotional responses to history.
2. **story:** speaker:narrator, mood:reverent. "Die Nikolaikirche ist still. Rosa-weisse Saulen steigen wie Palmen zur Decke. Hier begann alles. Hier beteten Menschen fur Freiheit, wahrend bewaffneter Polizisten draussen warteten." / "The Nikolaikirche is quiet. Pink-white columns rise like palm trees to the ceiling. Everything began here. Here, people prayed for freedom while armed police waited outside."
3. **story:** speaker:mia, mood:emotional. "Im Oktober 1989 kamen 70.000 Menschen hierher. Ohne Gewalt. Nur mit Kerzen. Die Polizei war da, aber niemand hat geschossen. Das war der Anfang des Endes." / "In October 1989, 70,000 people came here. Without violence. Only with candles. The police were there, but nobody fired. That was the beginning of the end."
4. **teach:** Versammlung(die). Fun info: "Assembly/gathering." Ver- + sammeln (to collect) + -ung. A coming-together. "Versammlungsfreiheit" (freedom of assembly) is a constitutional right. The 1989 Montagsdemonstrationen were Versammlungen that changed history.
5. **teach:** Gemeinschaft(die). Fun info: "Community." Gemein (common) + -schaft. What people share in common. "Europaische Gemeinschaft" (European Community). Different from "Gesellschaft" (society): Gemeinschaft is warmer, more personal, built on bonds rather than structures.
6. **teach:** Jugend(die). Fun info: "Youth." No plural form. "Die Jugend" refers to young people collectively. "Jugendherberge" (youth hostel) is a German invention. "In meiner Jugend..." (In my youth...) is how stories of the DDR often begin.
7. **teach:** Zeuge(der). Fun info: "Witness." From "zeugen" (to testify). Weak noun: der Zeuge, den Zeugen, dem Zeugen. "Zeitzeuge" (contemporary witness) is someone who lived through a historical period. Leipzig has many Zeitzeugen of 1989.
8. **teach:** Zeugin(die). Fun info: "Female witness." -in suffix. "Als Zeugin aussagen" (to testify as a witness). Mia's grandparents are Zeitzeuginnen and Zeitzeugen of the DDR era. Their stories carry the weight of lived experience.
9. **teach:** Realität(die). Fun info: "Reality." From Latin "realitas." "Die Realitat sieht anders aus" (Reality looks different). Germans sometimes pair it with Wirklichkeit for emphasis: both mean reality, but Realitat is borrowed, Wirklichkeit is native German.
10. **teach:** Wirklichkeit(die). Fun info: "Reality." From "wirklich" (real). Wirk- (effect/work) + -lich + -keit. What actually has effect, what truly works. "In Wirklichkeit" (in reality) is how Germans reveal what is really going on beneath the surface.
11. **teach:** gleichzeitig. Fun info: "Simultaneous(ly)." Gleich (same) + Zeit (time) + -ig. At the same time. "Gleichzeitig traurig und hoffnungsvoll" (simultaneously sad and hopeful). Describes the feeling in Nikolaikirche perfectly.
12. **teach:** menschlich. Fun info: "Human." From "Mensch" (human being). "Menschliche Wurde" (human dignity) is Article 1 of the German constitution. "Menschlich" means both "human" and "humane." To be human is to be compassionate.
13. **teach:** völlig. Fun info: "Completely." From "voll" (full). "Vollig normal" (completely normal). "Vollig anders" (completely different). An intensifier stronger than "ganz" (quite). "Vollig unerwartet" (completely unexpected) describes what happened in November 1989.
14. **teach:** geschehen. Fun info: "To happen/occur." Irregular: geschieht, geschah, ist geschehen. More formal than "passieren." "Was ist geschehen?" (What happened?) carries weight. "Geschichte" (history) comes from the same root: history is what has happened.
15. **teach:** entwickeln. Fun info: "To develop." Ent- (out of) + wickeln (to wrap). To unwrap. "Sich entwickeln" (to develop oneself). "Die Stadt hat sich schnell entwickelt" (The city developed quickly). Reflexive for organic growth, transitive for deliberate creation.
16. **teach:** überlegen. Fun info: "To consider/think over." Uber (over) + legen (to lay). To lay thoughts over a matter. "Ich uberlege mir das" (I will think it over). Also an adjective: "uberlegen" means "superior." Same word, stress changes meaning.
17. **teach:** andererseits. Fun info: "On the other hand." Anderer (other) + seits (side). Partner of "einerseits." "Andererseits muss man die Geschichte verstehen" (On the other hand, one must understand history). Essential for B1 argumentation.
18. **tip:** "Genitive adjective declension: the easiest rule." After any genitive article (des, der, des, der, eines, einer, eines), the adjective ALWAYS ends in -en. No exceptions.
\n- des guten Mannes, der guten Frau, des guten Kindes, der guten Leute
\n- eines guten Mannes, einer guten Frau, eines guten Kindes
\n- Common contexts: wahrend des Kalten Krieges (during the Cold War), trotz des schlechten Wetters (despite the bad weather), wegen des grossen Erfolgs (because of the great success)
\n- Genitive is the easiest case for adjective declension: always -en with articles.
19. **tip:** "Reciprocal verbs: sich and einander." When a plural subject does something TO EACH OTHER, German uses "sich" (ambiguous) or "einander" (explicit).
\n- Sie kennen sich. (They know each other OR themselves.)
\n- Sie kennen einander. (They know each other. Unambiguous.)
\n- With prepositions, einander becomes one word: miteinander (with each other), fureinander (for each other), voneinander (from each other)
\n- Contrast: "Sie waschen sich" (They wash themselves, reflexive) vs. "Sie helfen sich/einander" (They help each other, reciprocal)
20. **mc:** Genitive adjective. q: "Complete: 'wahrend des _____ Krieges' (during the Cold War)" opts: [Kalten, Kalter, Kaltes, Kaltem] ans: Kalten. hint: "After 'des' in genitive, adjectives always end in -en."

6. **teach:** Heim(das). Fun info: "Home." Warmer than "Haus" (house). "Heimat" (homeland) builds on this root. "Sich heimisch fuhlen" (to feel at home). "Altenheim" (retirement home), "Kinderheim" (children's home). Heim is where you belong.
7. **teach:** Flucht(die). Fun info: "Flight/escape." From "fliehen" (to flee). "Fluchtversuch" (escape attempt) from the DDR was a crime. "Fluchtling" (refugee) comes from this root. Over 3.5 million people fled East Germany before the Wall was built.
8. **teach:** Erlebnis(das). Fun info: "Experience (lived)." Er- + leben (to live) + -nis. Something you actively lived through. Stronger than Erfahrung. "Ein unvergessliches Erlebnis" (an unforgettable experience). The -nis ending is always neuter.
9. **teach:** Erziehung(die). Fun info: "Upbringing/education." From "erziehen" (to raise/educate). Er- + ziehen (to pull). Pulling someone up. "Gute Erziehung" (good upbringing) is highly valued. "Erziehungsurlaub" (parental leave) shows how seriously Germany takes it.
10. **teach:** Verhältnis(das). Fun info: "Relationship/ratio." Ver- + halten (to hold) + -nis. How things hold together. "Das Verhaltnis zwischen Ost und West" (the relationship between East and West). Also means "affair" in romantic context. Plural: Verhaltnisse (conditions/circumstances).
11. **teach:** Verhalten(das). Fun info: "Behavior." Ver- + halten (to hold). How one holds oneself. "Auffalliges Verhalten" (conspicuous behavior). Also a verb: "sich verhalten" (to behave). In the DDR, "richtiges Verhalten" (correct behavior) was state-defined.
12. **teach:** Geschlecht(das). Fun info: "Gender/sex/lineage." From "schlagen" (to strike, originally: kind/type). "Geschlechtergleichheit" (gender equality). Also means "generation/lineage": "das Geschlecht der Habsburger" (the House of Habsburg). Grammar uses it too: Geschlecht = grammatical gender.
13. **teach:** Mehrheit(die). Fun info: "Majority." Mehr (more) + -heit. The-more-ness. "Die Mehrheit der Bevolkerung" (the majority of the population). "Zweidrittelmehrheit" (two-thirds majority) is needed for constitutional changes. Opposite: Minderheit.
14. **teach:** Minderheit(die). Fun info: "Minority." Minder (less) + -heit. The-less-ness. "Ethnische Minderheiten" (ethnic minorities). Germany recognizes four national minorities: Danes, Frisians, Sorbs, and Sinti/Roma. Each has constitutional protection.
15. **teach:** Religion(die). Fun info: "Religion." From Latin "religio." Germany has a unique system: "Kirchensteuer" (church tax) is automatically deducted from your salary unless you formally leave your church. "Religionsfreiheit" (religious freedom) is constitutional.
16. **teach:** Gewerkschaft(die). Fun info: "Trade union." Ge- + Werk (work) + -schaft. A collective of workers. "IG Metall" is Germany's largest Gewerkschaft with 2+ million members. Unions are powerful in Germany: they negotiate industry-wide wages.
17. **teach:** gleichberechtigt. Fun info: "Equal (in rights)." Gleich (equal) + berechtigt (entitled). "Gleichberechtigt sein" (to be equal in rights). Article 3 of the German constitution: "Manner und Frauen sind gleichberechtigt." Four syllables, one fundamental principle.
18. **teach:** dauernd. Fun info: "Constant(ly)/lasting." From "dauern" (to last). "Dauernder Regen" (constant rain). "Das dauert dauernd!" (That always takes forever!) uses the word playfully. More emotional than "standig": implies slight annoyance or endlessness.
19. **teach:** sogenannt. Fun info: "So-called." So (so) + genannt (called, from nennen). "Die sogenannte friedliche Revolution" (the so-called peaceful revolution). Can be neutral (naming) or skeptical (doubting). "Sogenannte Experten" (so-called experts) implies they are not really experts.
20. **teach:** fliehen. Fun info: "To flee." Irregular: flieht, floh, ist geflohen. "Aus der DDR fliehen" was life-threatening. "Fluchtig" (fleeting, fugitive) comes from this verb. Over 5,000 people successfully fled across the Berlin Wall.
21. **teach:** erziehen. Fun info: "To raise/educate." Irregular: erzieht, erzog, hat erzogen. Er- + ziehen (to pull). "Kinder erziehen" (to raise children). "Gut erzogen" (well-raised) is a compliment. The Erzieher/Erzieherin works in kindergartens.
22. **mc:** Reciprocal + vocabulary. q: "Hildi says her husband and she 'haben einander vertraut.' What does einander mean here?" opts: [each other, themselves, always, nobody] ans: each other. hint: "Einander is the explicit reciprocal word, removing ambiguity from sich."
23. **fb:** Genitive adjective in context. s: "Wahrend der {1} Jahre in der DDR erlebte Hildi viel." a: ["langen"] opts: [langen, lange, langer, langem] hint: "After 'der' in genitive plural, adjectives end in -en. Always."
